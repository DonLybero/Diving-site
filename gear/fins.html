Top Fins in 2026

Fins are where opinions get loudest — paddle versus split, short and stiff versus long and soft, rubber versus composite. Strip away the tribalism and the consensus across reviewers is remarkably stable: the same ten fins keep winning tests year after year, several of them decades-old designs that simply refuse to be beaten. Your choice comes down to diving style: strong-current reef diving rewards a stiff paddle you can frog-kick, travel diving rewards light weight and compact length, and cold-water boat diving rewards a spring-strap rubber workhorse that shrugs off a decade of abuse.

What to look for
  • Open-heel fins with boots are the default for most diving; full-foot only for warm-water boat dives.
  • Swap bungee or spring straps in for buckles — cheap upgrade, no more fiddling with gloved hands.
  • Stiffer blades transmit more power but tire untrained legs; efficiency matters more than raw stiffness.
  • Check the weight if you fly to dive — rubber classics can eat 4kg of your baggage allowance.

1. Mares Avanti Quattro+

The Avanti Quattro+ has been the default paddle fin on dive boats for two decades, and the design explains why. Four longitudinal channels flex into a scoop mid-stroke, holding water on a blade with roughly 780 cm2 of thrust area, so it rewards a steady cadence rather than a sprint kick. Blade lengths run 33-39 cm across four sizes and a size-R fin weighs about 0.9 kg, which packs into a checked bag without argument. The Hi-Flex blade compound handles flutter, frog and scissor kicks without technique changes. Bungee straps are now standard, retiring the buckle strap that was the weak point of earlier versions. It is not the fin for ripping drift dives, since strong legs outkick the blade, and the foot pocket has no drainage, so buy it snug. Six colorways make it easy to spot a buddy in a guided group.

2. Scubapro Seawing Nova

The Seawing Nova's defining feature is a hinge: the wing-shaped Monprene blade pivots on an articulated joint to hold roughly a 45-degree angle of attack regardless of how hard you kick, so cruising costs measurably less effort than a fixed paddle. The current generation molds the top of that joint solid after the first version's cracking problem, and the single-material construction leaves no bonded seams to fail. The footplate runs the full length of the heel with co-molded TPV grip pads, so standing on a wet ladder in full kit is less of a gamble. Bungee straps with an oversized finger loop make donning one-handed. Frog and back kicks work but feel vaguer than on a stiff paddle, which is why the stiffer Gorilla variant exists. Kicking harder past the design cadence buys little extra speed; this is a cruising fin, not a sprint fin. Sizes run XS-XL in six colorways.

3. Scubapro Jet Fin

The Jet Fin is a mid-1960s design Scubapro still sells essentially unchanged, because for one job nothing has displaced it. The short, stiff vented blade sheds water on the recovery stroke and loads up on the power stroke, which keeps frog kicks, back kicks and helicopter turns precise; that is why it remains standard kit with technical and public-safety divers. Each fin runs about -0.27 kg in fresh water in size XL, which pulls the floaty boots of a drysuit diver into flat trim. Stainless spring straps come standard and go on with thick gloves. The trade-offs are unambiguous: around 1.5 kg per fin is real luggage weight, the negative buoyancy sinks feet in a 3 mm tropical rig, and a long straight flutter kick will remind the legs how stiff the blade is. Sizes run M to 3XL and the foot pocket assumes boots. Buy it for cold water and precision, not for travel.

4. Apeks RK3

The RK3 is Apeks's take on the military-issue vented rubber fin, and its brief is control in bad places rather than speed. The blade is short and wide, 49 cm in Medium up to 57 cm in Super, so it pivots in confined wreck spaces and holds a back kick without stirring the bottom. The standard thermoplastic compound floats slightly, which suits wetsuit divers; the higher-density RK3 HD flips negative for drysuit trim with the same geometry. The oversized foot pocket is cut for drysuit boots, and the spring straps mount on 316 stainless pin-and-nut hardware that replaces in the field with basic tools. Four sizes with no half steps means fit gets managed with boot thickness. The cost of the short blade is straight-line speed, and the flexible standard compound softens further when kicking hard against current. Black, white and yellow colorways match team-issue color coding.

5. TUSA HyFlex Switch

The Switch is TUSA's answer to the travel-fin problem: instead of shrinking the blade, it unbolts it. Two stainless screws with O-ring washers and an interlocking tab join the blade to the foot pocket, and with the blades off the whole kit packs to about 41 cm. Assembled, it dives like a conventional paddle — the polyurethane PuRiMaX blade snaps back faster than the soft EVA used in most compact fins, and molded vortex vents reduce drag on the upstroke. Divers report clean turning and backfinning and no cramping after repeated full-power finning against current, and ScubaBoard posters note that once bolted together you cannot tell the fin separates. The catch is mass: at roughly 1.8-2.0 kg per pair, the Switch saves volume rather than grams. Sizing is also coarse, four pockets covering EU 34-50 with everything above 44 lumped into one L/XL. If the joint bothers you, it is a reason to walk away; if it does not, this is one of the few travel fins that gives up nothing in the water. Divers wanting a harder kick can step up to the Switch Pro, whose blade compound is 20% stiffer.

6. Scubapro Go Sport

The Go Sport is the boot-fit sibling of Scubapro's Go Travel, scaled up so a drysuit boot fits where the original only took bare feet. The whole fin is a single molding of Monprene, a thermoplastic elastomer with no laminated joints to peel apart, and Scubapro leans on that for its durability claim rather than marketing gloss. The blade is pre-angled 25° with a central power panel that stiffens it lengthwise and channels water off the tip; in pool testing it out-pulled larger fins for the effort spent. In size M each fin weighs about 0.7 kg and measures roughly 51 cm, so a pair packs inside IATA carry-on limits — rare for a fin that takes cold-water boots. Removable skegs bolt onto the blade to cut sideslip for divers who want more tracking. The honest weakness is stiffness: against a strong current the blade folds earlier than a full-size paddle, and the fix is buying the stiffer Gorilla variant instead. Fit discipline matters too, because the pocket is cut for a boot and swims on a thin sock. As a one-fin quiver for both tropical trips and temperate shore dives, little else covers the same range.

7. Atomic Aquatics SplitFin

Atomic's SplitFin is the remaining serious argument for the split-blade concept: two flexible surfaces that twist into shallow propeller blades on each kick stroke. The point is efficiency, not power — a narrow, quick flutter kick holds cruising speed with noticeably less leg effort, which is why long-time owners talk about air consumption and the absence of calf cramps rather than sprint speed. Atomic controls the blade's flex with stiff battens and semi-rigid side rails, so the fin resists the total fold-up that gave split fins a bad name. The EZ-LOK buckles release with a single click and hold their adjustment between dives. The design's limits are real and well documented on ScubaBoard: frog kicks force a wait while the blade snaps back, reverse kicks go nowhere, and the fin has a hard ceiling on top speed no matter how hard you kick, which counts against it in strong current. At about 71 cm in size M it is also long for luggage, and the foot pocket runs narrow. Two compounds are offered — the standard power compound and a lighter high-energy version in the Red, Pink, Purple and Smoke colourways — with a one-year warranty on either. Buy it for relaxed reef and drift diving; buy a paddle for wrecks and current.

8. Cressi Frog Plus

The Frog Plus is Cressi's long-running budget open-heel fin, made in Italy by three-material injection molding: rubber over the foot pocket and side rails, a dual-density polymer blade, and softer channels that cup water down the centerline. Its one genuinely clever trick is geometry — the foot pocket sits under the blade instead of behind it, which adds about 20% working surface without making the fin longer, and keeps per-fin weight between 680 g and roughly 900 g depending on size. In the water it is a well-mannered mid-power paddle: comfortable pocket, predictable tracking, a grippy non-slip sole for boat decks, and enough thrust for ordinary reef diving. Push it hard, though, and the blade bends past its efficient curve and the thrust goes soft, so holding ground in a stiff current means fast kicking and tired ankles. The strap system is the other long-term annoyance — the buckle twist-locks into a socket in the fin body rather than onto a stud, so generic spring straps do not fit and Cressi's own replacements are chronically out of stock; buying the EBS bungee version up front sidesteps the problem. For divers who want a durable, packable fin at a fraction of premium money and do not dive heavy current, the trade is fair.

9. Aqua Lung Storm

Aqua Lung molds the Storm from a single piece of Monprene TPV, which removes the bonded joints where cheap travel fins usually fail and shrugs off UV and cracking. An L/XL pair weighs about 1.4 kg and measures 56 cm end to end, so it stows flat in a carry-on with room left for a regulator. The open-heel pocket is cut for bare feet or thin neoprene socks - it fits like a full-foot fin but dons like an open-heel, with a fixed silicone bungee and heel pad instead of buckles. The blade is short and wide and behaves more like a channel fin than its paddle shape suggests; at cruising pace it is efficient, but in real current the short length forces a faster cadence than a full-size paddle. That makes it a boat and warm-water fin, not a cold-shore workhorse. Five sizes run 2XS to 2XL and nine colorways cover junior to adult. At roughly $65 the compromise you accept is thrust ceiling, not build quality.

10. Mares Power Plana LT

The Power Plana LT takes the XR-Tech blade geometry of Mares' rubber tec fin and remolds it in TPV, dropping per-fin weight to 0.9-1.2 kg across the XS-XL run, with overall length between 51 and 56 cm. The result is slightly positive in the water, which suits wetsuit and travel divers and is precisely wrong for drysuit divers fighting floaty feet - they should stay with the rubber Power Plana. Test divers rate it smooth across flutter, frog and back kicks, with the stable blade profile holding a frog-kick glide without wobble. The blade runs directly into the foot pocket with no gap to snag a line, and the spring straps mount in multiple positions with a loop big enough for 5 mm gloves. The honest limit is output: the soft TPV works best at a relaxed cadence, and sprinting into current it folds earlier than the rubber version. At $217.95 MSRP it costs more than many all-rubber tec fins. As a travel-weight fin that still frog-kicks like a tec blade, it has few direct rivals.

11. Beuchat Powerjet

The Powerjet is Beuchat's adjustable-heel paddle, built from three compounds with lateral ribs molded into a flexible frame so the 39 cm blade loads and releases like a spring on each kick. Beuchat sells it in two builds - Regular Flex in yellow or blue, Powerful Flex in black only - so your stiffness choice dictates your color. At 1,020 g per fin it sits mid-pack, heavier than TPV travel fins but lighter than classic all-rubber jets. Stainless spring straps come standard rather than as an upgrade, which is not a given at this price. Divers prone to calf cramps report the Regular Flex delivers usable thrust without the leg load of a stiff tec blade, and owners describe it as neither too stiff nor too flexible. The catch is distribution: North American dealers are thin, forum coverage is sparse, and spares are easier to source for Scubapro or Mares. Sizes run S to XL. For divers who want a spring-loaded paddle without a rubber fin's weight, it is a quiet, sensible pick.

12. Scubapro Seawing Supernova

The Seawing Supernova replaces the failure-prone hinge of the old Seawing Nova with Pivot Control hinges and a flexible central panel that counter-pivots, holding the blade in the 40-50 degree angle-of-attack band whether you are finning gently or at full power. It is quick in a straight line - at 1.67 kg per pair, the winglet-tipped blade makes flutter kicking notably efficient - but frog and back kicks take relearning, and some ScubaBoard users never made peace with them. The fin is modular: blade and foot pocket separate via lock plates, and replacement blades sell in blue, yellow, turquoise and pink (Midi and Maxi), so a worn blade does not mean new fins. The bungee heel strap is replaceable and mounts in three positions. In the water it runs floaty; one drysuit diver reported adding 0.5 kg to each ankle to hold trim, so treat it as a wetsuit fin. Complete fins come in black or white across five sizes, XS to XL. You pay a premium over conventional paddles and carry one more small part - the lock clips - to not lose.

13. Hollis F1 Bat Fin

The F1 is a monolithic natural-rubber paddle with a vented blade, and it makes no concession to travel: about 1.47 kg per fin in size Regular, roughly 3.5 kg a pair in XL. That mass is the point - the fin runs about 0.9 kg negative per fin in fresh water (XL), which pins floaty drysuit boots down without ankle weights. The vented blade dumps water on the recovery stroke and rewards a strong frog kick; divers who flutter hard report it tears up calves and ankles before it runs out of thrust. The foot pocket is taller and wider than most, sized around drysuit boots, and the stainless spring straps with grip tabs mount in multiple positions for fit tuning. Sizing is its own system: three sizes (Regular, XL, XXL) cover US men's 7-15, most divers size up from other brands, and anyone under a US 7 is out of the range entirely. Warranty is a 2-year limited. As a cold-water and tec fin it competes directly with classic jet fins; as anything you carry through an airport, it is a liability.
