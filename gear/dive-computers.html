Top Dive Computers in 2026

No category of dive gear has moved faster. Ten years ago a colour screen was exotic; today the mid-market standard is a phone-bright AMOLED display, Bluetooth log sync and a rechargeable battery, and the watch-style computers double as daily fitness trackers. Two brands anchor the top of every reviewer's list: Shearwater, whose Peregrine and Perdix 2 are the recreational and tec benchmarks respectively, and Garmin, whose Descent range won over divers who want one watch for everything. Below them, the Zoop Novo, Leonardo and Puck Pro+ keep doing what they've always done — reliable, readable decompression guidance for the price of a fin set.

What to look for
  • Buy the screen you can read at 30m with fogged lenses — big numerals beat extra features.
  • Conservative vs liberal algorithms differ by minutes of no-deco time; Bühlmann with adjustable gradient factors is the transparent choice.
  • Air integration is genuinely useful but adds a transmitter (~$300) — budget for it or skip it consciously.
  • If it's also your watch, check battery life in dive mode; if it's dive-only, a user-changeable battery saves service costs.

1. Shearwater Peregrine

A palm-sized colour screen readable across a boat deck, running full Bühlmann ZHL-16C with adjustable gradient factors — the same transparent decompression model as technical computers, in a simple recreational package. It charges wirelessly, syncs logs to your phone and needs no manual. For most divers this is both the first computer and the last.

2. Garmin Descent Mk3i

A complete dive computer inside a premium multisport watch: air integration with up to ten tanks via transceivers, AMOLED screen, 200m rating, and full mapping, training and health tracking between dives. If you already live on the watch ecosystem it removes a whole device from your life. Battery runs weeks as a watch and full dive days in dive mode.

3. Shearwater Perdix 2

The technical benchmark: trimix, multi-gas switching, closed-circuit support and adjustable gradient factors behind a 2.2-inch screen that stays readable in silt. A titanium bezel and a user-replaceable AA battery reflect its expedition intent. Recreational divers buy it because it will still be the right computer when their diving grows — nothing about it expires.

A full sports watch with GPS and wrist heart-rate that becomes a serious dive computer underwater: bright AMOLED screen, Bühlmann decompression with adjustable conservatism, and air integration via a tank pod. 6. Suunto Zoop Novo

Big segment digits, five buttons, a deliberately conservative decompression model that keeps new divers far from trouble, and a battery you change yourself with a coin. Nitrox to 50%, a planning mode, and nothing to misunderstand. It remains the world's most common first computer because it does less — and that is the argument.

7. Cressi Leonardo

One button, one clear screen, air and nitrox modes and a user-changeable battery: the least expensive credible dive computer from a major manufacturer, which is why it's on rental wrists on every continent. The single-button interface takes a minute to learn and never confuses afterwards. A genuine safety upgrade over renting, for the price of a few boat dives.

The puck format grown up: large digits, an intuitive two-button flow, nitrox to 99% with gas switching, and Bluetooth upload of your logbook to the phone app. Slim enough to wear as a daily watch and priced under most entry rivals while doing more. The sensible middle ground between bare-bones and colour-screen money.
