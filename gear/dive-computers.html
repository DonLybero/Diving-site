Top Dive Computers in 2026

No category of dive gear has moved faster. Ten years ago a colour screen was exotic; today the mid-market standard is a phone-bright AMOLED display, Bluetooth log sync and a rechargeable battery, and the watch-style computers double as daily fitness trackers. Two brands anchor the top of every reviewer's list: Shearwater, whose Peregrine and Perdix 2 are the recreational and tec benchmarks respectively, and Garmin, whose Descent range won over divers who want one watch for everything. Below them, the Zoop Novo, Leonardo and Puck Pro+ keep doing what they've always done — reliable, readable decompression guidance for the price of a fin set.

What to look for
  • Buy the screen you can read at 30m with fogged lenses — big numerals beat extra features.
  • Conservative vs liberal algorithms differ by minutes of no-deco time; Bühlmann with adjustable gradient factors is the transparent choice.
  • Air integration is genuinely useful but adds a transmitter (~$300) — budget for it or skip it consciously.
  • If it's also your watch, check battery life in dive mode; if it's dive-only, a user-changeable battery saves service costs.

1. Shearwater Peregrine

Shearwater built its reputation on technical computers, and the Peregrine is that firmware trimmed to recreational scope: Bühlmann ZHL-16C with adjustable gradient factors, in air, nitrox, three-gas nitrox and gauge modes, with no compass and no air integration. The 5.5 cm, 320 x 240 color LCD is the point — digits big enough to read at a glance on a 77 x 68 x 25 mm, 122 g case rated to 120 m. The internal battery charges on an included induction pad and runs about 30 hours at medium brightness, so a week of diving needs one top-up. Logs store 200 hours at 10-second sampling and move over Bluetooth to Shearwater Cloud, which handles firmware updates well and dive browsing less well. In-water gripes are limited to glare on the lens during shallow stops; for travelers, the bigger annoyance is a charging pad that slips out of position in a packed bag and is not easily replaced mid-trip. The Adventures Editions add a white case with teal accents and a gray case with orange accents alongside the black original, all functionally identical and priced the same. If you need a compass or tank pressure the answer is the Peregrine TX; what the base model sells is the screen and the algorithm at recreational money.

2. Garmin Descent Mk3i

The Descent Mk3i is a 43 mm multisport watch that happens to be a full trimix dive computer, and Garmin charges accordingly: $1,399.99 for the watch plus $499.99 per T2 transceiver for air integration. The dive side is legitimate — Bühlmann ZHL-16C across single-gas, multi-gas trimix, CCR, gauge and apnea modes, a 200 m rating, and a 1.2 in, 390 x 390 AMOLED under sapphire that reads clearly at depth. SubWave sonar is the differentiator: up to eight transceivers on screen, buddies' pressures included, plus preset diver-to-diver messages at roughly 30 m range — though a message can take up to 45 seconds to land depending on orientation. At 68.3 g with the band it wears as an everyday watch, with multi-band GNSS, heart-rate tracking, storage for about 2,000 songs and roughly 10 days between charges in smartwatch mode against about 30 hours in dive mode. Owner complaints are mostly software: the Dive app logs users out at inconvenient moments, and every setting must be rebuilt from scratch even coming from an older Descent. The 43 mm case ships in carbon gray DLC titanium with a black band or bronze PVD titanium with a French gray band; a 51 mm version exists for larger wrists and longer battery numbers. It earns its price only if the smartwatch half works on land every day — a dedicated dive computer covers the underwater half for a third of the money.

3. Shearwater Perdix 2

The Perdix 2 is the default technical computer on most dive boats, and the spec explains why: open-circuit recreational and technical, closed-circuit and gauge modes on Bühlmann ZHL-16C with gradient factors, rated to 260 m, running on a single AA cell the owner swaps in seconds. Battery chemistry sets the runtime — about 40 hours on alkaline, 60 on 1.5 V lithium, 100 on a Saft LS14500 — which is why it turns up on liveaboards and expeditions where a charger is a liability. The 2022 revision hardened the hardware: titanium bezel, titanium piezo buttons and an aluminosilicate lens over the 5.5 cm, 320 x 240 display, all at 266 g. Air integration reads up to four Swift transmitters at once, covering sidemount and stages without paging through screens. Real complaints are few and specific: the bottom-row gas label is locked into the layout where some owners want runtime or average depth, the bungee-and-elastic mounting is overkill on a bare arm in the tropics, and it is emphatically not something you wear to dinner. Current colorways are Ti Black and Ti Silver; a bronze Journeys Edition ran as a limited release in 2025 and has mostly sold through. The 2-year warranty is standard Shearwater, and resale value holds unusually well.

4. Suunto Ocean

Suunto's first AMOLED dive watch does two jobs without an obvious compromise: a 60 m-rated computer running Bühlmann 16 GF with adjustable gradient factors, and a 99 g multisport watch with dual-band GNSS, offline maps and 95-plus sport modes. The 466 x 466 px sapphire-covered touchscreen is the sharpest display Suunto has put on a dive product, and three oversized buttons carry every underwater function so the touch layer can stay disabled at depth. Air integration works over Suunto Tank PODs — up to five at once, which matters to instructors watching students' gas. The limits are firm: open-circuit recreational diving only, five gases up to 100% oxygen but no trimix or CCR, and the dive screen cannot show compass heading and tank pressure simultaneously. Firmware lands once or twice a year, and owner forums document always-on-display and brightness bugs that lingered after the 2025 Q3 update, plus occasional Tank POD dropouts cured by repositioning the wrist. Dive-mode battery runs to roughly 40-60 hours, so a week of repetitive diving means packing the charging cable. As a single watch for divers who also run, ride and swim, it earns the wrist space; divers who never leave the water pay for a lot of watch they will not use.

5. Garmin Descent G1

The Descent G1 is Garmin's entry dive computer built on the Instinct chassis: 45.5 mm, 61 g, fiber-reinforced polymer with a sapphire lens and a 100 m water rating to EN 13319. The monochrome memory-in-pixel screen looks dated next to AMOLED rivals but reads cleanly in direct sunlight and sips power — 25 hours in dive mode, about three weeks as a watch, and the Solar version stretches watch mode toward four months. Under the plain face sits real range: Bühlmann ZHL-16c with adjustable gradient factors, multi-gas including trimix and 100% oxygen, gauge, closed-circuit rebreather and apnea modes. It stores 200 dives, marks surface entry and exit points by GPS, and syncs to the Garmin Dive app alongside the usual Garmin training metrics. What it lacks is air integration — it does not pair with Garmin's transmitters, so a pressure gauge stays on the regulator. Divers on forums split on the display: perfectly legible in clear shallow water, cramped and gray in a murky quarry. As a travel watch that happens to handle technical gases, or as a backup computer, the capability per dollar is difficult to fault.

6. Suunto Zoop Novo

The Zoop Novo has been the default first computer for over a decade, and the 2024 refresh changed the colors, not the recipe. It is a 66 x 66 x 26 mm puck with a large segmented display, phosphorescent backlight and four buttons, rated to 80 m operating depth and running air, nitrox (21-50%), gauge and free modes. Suunto's RGBM algorithm is conservative by design; it can be made stricter — P0-P2 personal settings plus three altitude ranges to 3,000 m — but never more liberal, and its repetitive-dive penalties are the standing complaint on dive forums. Violate a decompression ceiling and it locks itself out of dive mode with no override. The compensation is cheap ownership: the CR2450 battery is user-replaceable in about ten minutes with Suunto's kit, lasts roughly two years or 150 dives, and owners commonly report units surviving 7-10 years. The downsides are bulk — the puck can catch when threading an arm through a BCD strap — and a PC download cable that costs extra. For a rental-desk-tough computer that does the job until the algorithm starts to chafe, it remains the reference point.

7. Cressi Leonardo

The Leonardo is Cressi's single-button entry computer and it has outlived several generations of flashier rivals by doing three modes — air, nitrox, gauge — without fuss. The 67 mm case weighs 135 g, the segment display uses digits big enough to read across a buddy check, and depth is displayed to 120 m, far past its recreational brief. The Cressi RGBM algorithm tracks nine tissue compartments and manages real deco stops if you overstay, but it is stingy: expect noticeably shorter no-deco limits than most computers in the same water, and off-gassing credit only below 6 m. Navigation is the price of the price — one button, pressed or held, cycles everything, and nitrox is programmable from 21 to 50% with a PO2 alarm from 1.2 to 1.6 bar. The CR2430 coin cell is cheap and lasts around 150 dives, and a full reset function clears tissue loading between users, which is why dive schools buy them six at a time. Alarms are loud and insistent, the safety stop counts in whole minutes, and there is no compass and no upgrade path. As a first computer or a console backup it is hard to waste money on; as an only computer for an ambitious logbook it will eventually feel tight.

8. Mares Puck Pro+

The Puck Pro+ is the pared-down puck that half the world's dive schools strap onto students, and the plus version keeps the formula while adding gas switching and connectivity. It runs the Mares-Wienke RGBM across 10 tissue compartments, displays depth to 150 m, and takes two nitrox mixes anywhere from 21 to 99% O2 with switching allowed mid-dive — deco-stage capability at an entry price. The segment display is large for the 113 g case and the mineral glass lens resists the scuffs that kill cheaper acrylic screens. Navigation is one button; it works, but a missed menu item means riding the loop around again, and settings changes with thick gloves are deliberate work. The CR2450 battery is user-replaceable and rated to roughly 300 dives, so the computer can sit in a gear bin for a year and wake up ready. The catch on connectivity: logbook download and firmware updates run over Bluetooth only through the Bluelink Pro dongle, sold separately. There is no compass and no air integration, and the RGBM trims repetitive-dive allowances more than liberal algorithms. For a first computer or a dedicated travel spare, the trade-offs are the right ones.

9. Aqualung i330R

The i330R is Aqualung's play to bring the color-screen, rechargeable format down to entry-level money, and on the spec sheet it succeeds. The TFT display reads cleanly in full sun, the case is a compact 5.5 cm square, and a 2-hour USB-C charge is good for up to 30 hours of diving. It runs Buhlmann ZHL-16C — the same algorithm family as computers costing three times as much — across air, nitrox, gauge and free modes, with three nitrox mixes to 100% O2 and mid-dive switching. Two buttons make menu work faster than the single-button pucks it undercuts, and Bluetooth pushes logs to the DiverLog+ app, keeping only the last 24 dives on the unit itself. The record is not spotless: an early recall for altitude miscalculation above roughly 900 m was fixed, but used buyers should verify serials, and the lens scratches easily enough that owners fit protectors before the first trip. The sealed battery cuts both ways — no coin-cell swaps, but also a charger to pack and a cell that ages out of a computer you cannot open. There is no compass and no air integration. For divers who want a modern color display without a modern-color-display price, this is the honest budget pick.

10. Apeks DSX

The DSX is Apeks's first dive computer, and it is pitched at technical divers rather than the resort market. A 61 mm (2.4 in) backlit color TFT sits in a 79 × 62 × 23 mm case with a titanium bezel, rated to 100 m. It manages six gases across open-circuit sport and tech modes, a constant-setpoint CCR mode, sidemount with gas-switch notifications, and gauge, with air integration for up to six wireless transmitters. Charging works by cable or the included induction mat, and a surface GPS records entry and exit points and can point you back to a saved mark. The catch has been software: early firmware threw system errors when searching for out-of-range transmitters, and app-delivered updates were a recurring owner complaint, though releases have kept coming. The screen also scratches more readily than rivals, so budget for a protector. At $1,399 the hardware is genuinely tech-grade; buy it for the gas flexibility, not for software polish.

11. Aqualung i470TC

The i470TC puts Bluetooth and wireless air integration into a watch-sized stainless steel case with a tempered glass lens, for around $500. It tracks three nitrox mixes to 100% O2 and pairs with up to three transmitters, so a deco bottle or a rented rig does not force compromises. The practical win is the battery: an off-the-shelf cell you change yourself, with settings and tissue loading retained through the swap. Alarms are audible and visual, with an LED warning light firing alongside the beeper, and logs sync over Bluetooth to the free DiverLog+ app. The interface is the weak point — buttons sit close, owners describe fumbling into gauge mode while adjusting nitrox, and the computer then locks deco calculations for 24 hours. On-unit history caps at 24 dives, thin for a liveaboard week without phone syncs. As a first air-integrated computer it is honest value; learn the button order on land first.

12. Mares Sirius

The Sirius is Mares's watch-format flagship, built around a color MIP display that trades brightness for runtime — about 30 hours of diving per charge at medium brightness, 20 with a transmitter connected. It runs Buhlmann ZH-L16C with adjustable gradient factors and predictive multigas across five mixes, plus hoseless integration for up to five transmitters. A tilt-compensated compass with bearing memory and a stopwatch rounds out the tool set, and dives sync over Bluetooth to the Mares app. Charging happens on an included wireless base, so there are no contacts to corrode. Two colorways, Black and Black/Silver, ship on a three-piece quick-change silicone strap, with replacement kits in aqua, blue, red, yellow and black. The display is the honest trade-off: crisp in sunlight, dim on night dives, and one owner reported the backlight refusing to trigger underwater. The strap buckle's spring pin also compresses too easily, letting a knock pop the buckle off — a sore point on a computer near $1,000 before the roughly $370 transmitter.

13. Shearwater Perdix 3

Shearwater relaunched its flagship in 2026 by swapping the Perdix 2's LCD for a 6.1 cm AMOLED panel at 450 x 600 pixels — four times the resolution — without growing the case, which actually dropped 9 g to 181 g. The bezel is machined titanium and the two buttons are titanium piezo units with no moving parts, so there is nothing mechanical to flood and they respond through thick dry gloves. The spec sheet reads like a technical diver's checklist: 260 m depth rating, Buhlmann ZHL-16C with gradient factors, trimix, fixed-PPO2 CCR/bailout, and air integration for up to 4 Swift transmitters. Power stays deliberately old-fashioned — one user-replaceable AA, good for roughly 60 hours on a lithium cell, with alkaline and rechargeable cells accepted — which matters on a liveaboard a long way from a service centre. The trade-offs are honest ones: US$1,295 buys the computer alone, the full-size housing is conspicuous on a wrist in board shorts, and forum threads point out Garmin offered an AMOLED screen with 8-transmitter support three years earlier. None of that changes the calculus much for divers moving from recreational into technical territory, where the AA battery, the 260 m rating and the glove-proof buttons are the specs that count. It replaces the Perdix 2 as the default full-size pick.

14. Shearwater Teric

Years after launch the Teric is still the reference watch-sized dive computer, mostly because Shearwater refused to cut the technical spec to fit the 55 mm case. It runs the same Buhlmann ZHL-16C gradient-factor algorithm as the Perdix line, with trimix, CCR/bailout and gauge modes and a 200 m depth rating, behind a 400 x 400 AMOLED under sapphire crystal with a 316 stainless bezel. At 120 g on a standard 22 mm strap it wears like an ordinary large watch, and it alerts by both vibration and sound — a combination the newer Tern TX dropped. Charging is wireless, on the supplied dock or any Qi pad, and a charge covers 30-plus hours of diving. The weak point is the sealed battery: expected life is about five years, replacement is a service-centre job rather than a screwdriver job, and forum reports of overnight standby drain on some units are common enough to be a pattern, though Shearwater's warranty service draws consistent praise for speed. Air integration with Swift transmitters is supported but each transmitter is a separate purchase on top of the US$1,199 body. Buy it if you want Perdix capability that fits under a shirt cuff; budget for the battery's second life.

15. Shearwater Tern TX

The Tern TX is Shearwater's cheapest route to wireless air integration, and the spec it shares with the flagship is the surprising one: it monitors up to 4 transmitters, matching the Perdix 3, from a 53 mm case weighing 100 g. The 1.3-inch 360 x 360 AMOLED runs the same Buhlmann ZHL-16C gradient-factor algorithm, with air, nitrox, 3-gas nitrox and gauge modes to a 120 m rating. Charging is a wireless USB-C puck rather than a dock, a charge lasts about 20 hours of diving at medium brightness, and standby stretches to six months — divers report covering 20 dives between charges. The 22 mm quick-release straps swap without tools, with silicone bands in five colours and Remora webbing in three. The economies show in three places: alerts are vibration-only where the Teric also sounds an alarm, the lens is toughened alkali-aluminosilicate glass rather than sapphire, and the mode list omits trimix and CCR entirely. That last point defines the buyer — this is a recreational computer with premium hardware, not a technical computer in waiting. At US$775, several hundred less than a Teric, that is a reasonable trade for a travelling single-tank diver.

16. Suunto Nautic

The Nautic is Suunto's dedicated big-screen dive computer: a 3.26 in AMOLED panel in a 101 x 60 mm glass-fibre reinforced polyamide case, depth-rated to 200 m and driven by four buttons that work with 8 mm gloves. It runs Bühlmann ZHL-16C with adjustable gradient factors and covers multi-gas, trimix and sidemount with dual tank-pressure readouts from Suunto Tank PODs. Battery life is the headline number — up to 120 hours of diving at medium brightness — and dive-tested reviews report the claim holding across multi-day trips with days off between charges. GPS logs entry and exit points and tracks the route so the Suunto app can replay the dive in 3D, and a small LED flashlight is built into the case. The trade-off is bulk: 232 g with strap, and it is a dive instrument, not something you keep on at dinner. At $699 it costs less than half of Garmin's Descent X50i in the same big-screen class, though divers wanting a longer-proven tech platform still lean Shearwater. For anyone prioritizing legibility and battery life over watch styling, the spec-per-dollar case is strong.

17. Garmin Descent X50i

The Descent X50i is Garmin's first large-format dive computer: a 3 in, 640 x 360 touchscreen in a 62.4 x 98.3 x 24.5 mm fiber-reinforced polymer case with a sapphire lens, rated to 20 ATM (200 m). The feature that separates it from every rival is SubWave sonar — preset messages to buddies up to 30 m away, and live tank pressure, depth and distance for up to eight divers wearing T2 transceivers. Surface GPS, more than 4,000 preloaded dive sites and DiveView maps cover topside navigation, and a built-in LED dive light serves as a backup. Battery runs 13 hours at high brightness, 16 at medium and 20 in night mode — enough for two full days of diving, but early owners reported drain from wake-on-water detection while the unit sat in a bag, only partly cured by firmware. The touchscreen works only at the surface and responds to water drops, so most owners disable it and run the leakproof metal buttons, which handle thick gloves without complaint. At 280 g on the nylon strap and $1,499.99 before transceivers, it is a heavy commitment in both senses. It earns its price with dive teams and instructors who will use the sonar networking; a diver who only needs deco and air integration is paying for capability that stays switched off.

18. Scubapro Galileo 3 (G3)

The Galileo 3 compresses Scubapro's full Galileo feature set into a 47.2 mm watch-style case: stainless steel housing, sapphire lens and a rotating bezel coated in self-luminescent material, rated to 120 m in dive mode and 300 m in gauge. You choose between two algorithms — predictive multi-gas ZHL-16 ADT MB, which feeds tank pressure and workload from the optional transmitter into the deco calculation, or ZHL-16 GF for divers who prefer to set gradient factors themselves. Scuba mode covers trimix, CCR, sidemount and PMG, and the full-text four-button interface carries over from the G2, so there is no icon deciphering underwater. The rechargeable lithium cell delivers about 30 hours of diving per charge and refills through a proprietary USB dongle in roughly 80 minutes; there is no contactless charging. The screen is the weak point — owners report a low-resolution layout with pixelated fonts that reads like a shrunk console display rather than a design built for a watch face. Weight is the other compromise, enough that Scubapro sells a titanium version at 99 g, nearly 40% lighter, at a higher price. Street prices have dropped well below launch, which makes the standard G3 a better value now than when it shipped.
