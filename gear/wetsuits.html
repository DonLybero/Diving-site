Top Wetsuits in 2026

Neoprene thickness tracks water temperature, but the ranges overlap more than any chart admits — how cold you run, how long your dives last and whether you add a hood all move the line. As a working guide: 2 mm and shorties above about 27°C, 3 mm from roughly 24 to 29°C, 5 mm through the mid-teens to low twenties, and 7 mm once you're in the low teens or below. Pick a thickness for the suits and prices at that rating.

2 mm / shorty — warm tropical · ~27-30°C+

Above 27°C most divers cook inside a 5 mm before they even leave the surface. A 2 mm full suit or a shorty holds off the slow chill of a 60-minute dive and keeps hydroids, fire coral and the sun off your skin without the bulk. A shorty drops the arm and leg coverage for less material and a lower price; a thin full suit costs a little warmth-per-gram but covers everything.

What to look for
  • A shorty covers the core and leaves arms and legs free — cooler and cheaper; a thin full suit adds sting and sun protection on longer or deeper dives.
  • Flatlock seams are fine at this thickness: they lie flat and comfortable, and a little water exchange is no problem in warm water.
  • Look for a back-zip with a long leash and smooth-skin neck for easy solo entry between dives.

3 mm — tropical / warm water · ~24-29°C

3 mm is the default for most tropical diving — the Caribbean, the Red Sea in summer, the reefs of the Coral Triangle. It carries enough heat for three or four dives a day in 25–28°C water without leaving you sweating on the boat in between, and a good one rolls small enough to pack beside your regulator. Drop below about 24°C, though, and most divers start reaching for a 5 mm or a hood.

What to look for
  • Glued-and-blindstitched seams keep water out far better than flatlock, and matter more as your dives get longer.
  • A snug fit is what keeps you warm — a 3 mm that fits well beats a 5 mm that gaps at the lower back and floods.
  • Fabric-lined interiors slide on easily when you're damp; extra chest and back panels add real warmth for the weight.

5 mm — temperate / subtropical · ~16-24°C

5 mm covers the awkward middle — Mediterranean summers, Pacific thermoclines, the subtropical diving that's too cold for 3 mm and doesn't yet justify 7 mm. The extra couple of millimetres roughly doubles how long you last before the cold registers, and a hood and vest stretch a well-fitted 5 mm down toward 14–15°C. Fit counts for more here than at any other thickness: a suit that gaps at the back flushes cold water with every kick.

What to look for
  • Seals make the suit: smooth-skin or glide-skin wrist and ankle cuffs cut the cold-water flush that steals your warmth.
  • High-stretch neoprene is worth paying for at 5 mm — thicker rubber fights you unless it's engineered to flex.
  • Add a matching hood and vest for another few degrees; most of your heat is lost through your head and core.

7 mm — cold water · ~10-18°C

7 mm is about as far as a wetsuit sensibly goes — kelp forests, quarries, temperate reefs, shoulder-season diving in the low teens. It only works if it seals: open wrists, ankles and a loose neck let cold water flush through and strip away the warm layer your body just heated, which is why the better suits use smooth-skin or glued cuffs and a snug collar. Below about 10°C most divers switch to a semi-dry or a drysuit rather than pile on more neoprene.

What to look for
  • Sealed or smooth-skin cuffs and a snug neck are non-negotiable at 7 mm — an unsealed suit flushes and you'll get cold fast.
  • Expect to add weight: thick neoprene is buoyant, so plan your weighting and check trim on the first dive.
  • A semi-dry with glide-skin seals holds a warmer water layer than a plain 7 mm — worth it if you dive cold often.
