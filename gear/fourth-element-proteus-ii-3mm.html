Fourth Element Proteus II 3mm Review

The benchmark tropical dive suit: warmer, stretchier and tougher than almost anything else at 3mm.

← Back to scuba gear

#1 in Top Wetsuits in 2026

What makes it good?

The Proteus II is built on a simple premise: a 3 mm suit leaks heat mostly through water exchange, so Fourth Element sealed the exchange paths instead of adding rubber. The Hydrolock system runs an internal neoprene section from the small of the back over the head, forming a barrier between the rear zipper and the skin, while Glideskin wrist seals and internal polyurethane ankle seals close the limbs; seams are double-glued, blind-stitched and taped. The result, confirmed across published tests and owner threads, is a 3 mm that behaves like a conventional 5 mm — testers report flushing water in deliberately to cool off in the 23°C-plus range the maker recommends. The price of that warmth is the donning ritual: the neck seal arrives tight enough that owners describe being choked until they stretch it over a container for a couple of days, and the internal zip dam slides down during donning with no way to reach it, so a buddy is close to mandatory. Fit runs snug overall because the seals only work when they touch skin, and sizing up is the standard advice despite a 16-cut men's range from XS to XXXL with short and tall variants. At $692 on Fourth Element's US store, it is priced against 5 mm flagships rather than other 3 mm suits. It is the suit for divers who chill easily but refuse the buoyancy and bulk penalty of thicker neoprene.

Where it wins

  • Divers repeatedly report the 3 mm holding heat like a standard 5 mm, to the point of hooking a finger behind the neck seal mid-dive to flush cool water in
  • The Hydrolock inner seal runs from the small of the back over the head and puts a solid neoprene barrier between the rear zipper and the skin, closing the classic back-zip leak path
  • Seams are double-glued, blind-stitched and taped, with Glideskin wrist seals and internal PU ankle seals finishing the enclosure
  • Sixteen men's cuts from XS to XXXL, including short and tall variants, cover builds most three-size warm-water suits ignore

Where it falls short

  • The Hydrolock neck seal arrives brutally tight — ScubaBoard owners describe it as choking and resort to stretching it over a container for two days before it becomes wearable
  • Self-donning is genuinely hard: the internal zip dam slides down as the suit is pulled up and cannot be reached to reposition
  • At $692 on Fourth Element's US store, it costs more than many 5 mm suits; the snug seal-driven fit also pushes buyers a size up
