Gear buyer’s guide

A mask is the cheapest piece of kit you'll buy and the one that can ruin a dive fastest. A poor skirt seal means a dive spent clearing water; a narrow lens means missing the eagle ray that glides past your shoulder. The ten masks below are the ones working divers and instructors keep choosing, year after year. They split into two camps: frameless designs that fold flat and give a huge field of view, and twin-lens frames that fit narrow faces and take prescription lenses. Every pick here uses tempered glass and 100% silicone skirts — no PVC anywhere.

What to look for
  • Fit test: press the mask to your face without the strap and inhale through your nose — it should hold on suction alone.
  • Black skirts cut glare and reflections for photographers; clear skirts feel less claustrophobic for new divers.
  • Low-volume masks clear with a single breath and make mask-equalising on descent almost automatic.
  • If you wear glasses, pick a twin-lens frame model that accepts prescription drop-in lenses.

1. Atomic Aquatics Venom Frameless

Atomic builds the Venom Frameless around a single pane of German Schott Superwite glass that passes up to 96% of available light; next to the green tint of ordinary tempered lenses the difference is visible before you hit the water. The skirt is co-molded from two silicone durometers — Atomic's 'Gummi Bear' UltraSoft on the face seal, a stiffer compound behind it — bonded straight to the glass over a patented internal SubFrame. The result behaves like a hybrid: frameless field of view with framed-mask stability, and owners rate its horizontal vision the widest in the single-lens class. It runs big and sits heavier on the face than most frameless masks, and divers with smaller faces report nose pressure and extra strap tension to keep the seal honest. At $160-180 street it costs roughly double a capable mid-range mask, and the forum debate is whether the glass and the seal justify that gap. If the fit works, they do — but try it on a medium-to-large face before paying the premium.

2. Scubapro Synergy 2 Trufit

Scubapro's answer to the one-skirt-fits-nobody problem is Trufit: a thin, soft inner silicone skirt that does the sealing and a firmer outer skirt that carries the structure. In practice the seal conforms without leaving red mask lines after an hour underwater, which is the trait owners mention most. The single tempered lens keeps volume low and the view uninterrupted, rotating micro-adjust buckles fold flat for packing, and a textile Comfort Strap replaces the usual hair-pulling silicone strap out of the box. The double skirt has a cost: 204 g (0.45 lb), heavier than most single-lens masks. A mirrored-lens version kills surface glare but scratches easily and is a poor choice after dark. A sensible pick for divers who leak in stiffer skirts and log long bottom times; night divers should take the clear lens.

3. Cressi Big Eyes Evolution

Cressi angles the two inverted-teardrop lenses inward at 15 degrees, which pushes downward visibility more than 30% past a conventional dual-lens mask — gauges, D-rings and inflator are visible without tipping the head. Internal volume is among the lowest of any framed mask, so clearing takes a partial exhale. The buckles mount on flexible elastomer tabs rather than the frame and fold through 180 degrees, a direct fix for the strap-lug breakage owners reported on the original Big Eyes. It accepts Cressi drop-in optical lenses from -1 to -6 diopters, fitted per eye, and the mask is made in Italy. Street prices run roughly $70-100, under most of the masks it gets compared with. The catch is strap tuning: owners report the adjustment has to be exactly right — one notch off, or a hood swap, and it starts weeping — and the strap itself is unusually slim. The frame bridge between the lenses also sits in the center of your view, which single-lens converts tend to notice.

4. Mares X-Vision

The X-Vision is Mares' staple dual-lens design, and the current generation is cut with a field of view 20% wider than the model it replaced. At 189 g (6.7 oz) it is light for a framed mask, and the low internal volume clears and equalizes with little effort. The detail work earns its keep: small silicone ribs between frame and skirt take descent pressure off the nose bridge, and the X-shaped split strap spreads its load across the back of the head instead of a single line. The skirt seals with a double feathered edge. Fit skews medium-to-wide — narrow faces leak, and Mares sells the smaller X-Vision Mid 2.0 for exactly that reason, so try it on first. New lenses ship with a factory film that defog won't beat until it has been scrubbed out, usually two or three toothpaste passes. Prescription lenses are available across the X-Vision line.

5. Aqua Lung Reveal X2

The Reveal X2 is the twin-lens half of Aqua Lung's Reveal pair, and its argument is the skirt: bands of differently textured silicone graded across the face, softer at the seal line and firmer where the skirt needs to hold shape. Because there is no rigid outer frame carrying the hardware, the buckles mount directly to the skirt and release with a single oversized button that still works in 5 mm gloves. The strap is a split design with a longer middle section and a silicone pad that spreads load across the back of the head instead of concentrating it in two thin bands. The two-lens layout accepts prescription lenses, which the single-lens X1 cannot. The trade-off of all that softness is setup: the skirt seals at a lower strap tension than most divers expect, and owners commonly report a dive or two of fine-tuning before it stops weeping. Aqua Lung also publishes no weight or internal-volume number, an odd omission for a mask sold partly on fit precision.

6. TUSA Freedom Ceos M-212

The Ceos M-212 is TUSA's two-window entry in its Freedom range, and the numbers are its case: 180 cc internal volume, 184 g, a 72 × 157 mm frame and a 113 × 130 mm fit line that makes it one of the more reliable choices for petite-to-medium faces. The Freedom skirt is the working part — silicone graded in thickness across its width, with a dimpled, low-friction surface along the fitting line so the seal slides with facial movement rather than lifting. The skirt edge is rounded in cross-section instead of feathered flat, which spreads pressure and leaves less of a mask line after a long day. The strap is TUSA's 3-D design, pre-curved to the skull rather than stamped flat, with low-profile quick-adjust buckles set into the skirt. Buyers should budget one evening with toothpaste: the production silicone film on new lenses fogs until it is scrubbed off. Wide faces should try the fit line before paying, and anyone needing prescription lenses will be sending the mask to a specialist, since TUSA offers no drop-ins for this model. The M-212S Pro version adds optical glass with anti-reflective and UV coatings for about twenty dollars more.

7. Hollis M1 Frameless

The M1 is Hollis's one mask that everyone keeps recommending, and the reason is the glass: a single Saint-Gobain Diamant lens in low-iron formulation, which transmits more light and shows less of the green cast built into ordinary tempered lenses. Bonding that lens directly to the silicone removes the frame, and with it most of the bulk — about 113 g and a packed size near 10 × 5 × 7.5 cm, flat enough for a BC pocket. The skirt carries a double, feathered sealing edge that stays comfortable across repetitive dive days, and the low volume clears on a short exhale. The buckles are bonded to the skirt and do not swivel, which costs a little strap-angle adjustment; more annoying is that a hard tug pulls the strap clean out of the buckle with a snap, recoverable underwater but not something a mask at this price should do. New lenses need a proper scrub to strip the factory film before they hold defog. There is no drop-in prescription option. As a do-everything single-lens mask under 100 dollars, the compromises are easy to live with.

8. Scubapro Frameless

Scubapro has sold the Frameless in essentially the same form since 1990, and its persistence is the review: a wide rectangular tempered lens bonded straight to a double-edge silicone skirt, no frame, buckles riding on flexible skirt tabs that find their own strap angle. The lens shape buys genuine peripheral vision, and because nothing rigid surrounds it, the mask folds flat into a BC cargo pocket — the reason it has been the default backup of working divers for three decades. The wide split headband spreads load across the back of the head, and the skirt seals reliably on medium-to-wide faces. That last clause is the catch: the standard skirt runs broad, and narrow-faced divers report chronic small leaks they first blame on technique; Scubapro's own Mini Frameless exists to fix this, with the Gorilla covering the opposite end. At roughly 227 g it is also heavier than the frameless label suggests, and the one-piece lens rules out drop-in prescription glass. Buy it for the field of view and the fold-flat packability, not for a narrow face.

9. Atomic Aquatics SubFrame

Atomic builds the SubFrame around a rigid skeleton molded beneath the silicone skirt, so there is no external plastic frame to snap in a dive bag, and the company backs the frame against breakage for life. The two lenses are UltraClear optical glass, which passes roughly 98% of available light where standard green float glass loses more than 14%, and an ARC version adds an anti-reflective coating for divers bothered by internal glare. A removable stainless-steel nose bridge holds the lens retainer, which is what makes the mask serviceable: factory prescription lenses from -1.0 to -8.0 in 0.5-diopter steps drop in without replacing the mask. The double-feathered skirt seals reliably — bearded divers in particular report dry dives — and owners praise how easily the buckles adjust. Two fits are sold, Regular and Medium, and that split is the main buying hazard: some narrow-faced divers find the Medium too small and the Regular right, so try one on before ordering. At about $150 before options it costs three times a basic frameless mask. It earns that gap if you keep a mask for a decade or need prescription glass; it does not if you replace gear every few seasons.

10. Cressi F1 Frameless

The F1 bonds a single tempered lens directly to a double-feathered silicone skirt, so there is no frame between glass and face; the result sits close, weighs under 200 g and folds flat into a BC pocket as a spare. The CE-certified lens is one uninterrupted pane, which is where the wide peripheral and downward view comes from. One-push buckles are molded straight into the skirt to keep drag down, and the nose pocket is proportioned so equalizing works in 5 mm gloves. Cressi sells a dedicated replacement strap, which matters on a mask that routinely costs under $50. The shell runs compact: small-to-medium faces get the best seal, and Cressi makes a separate F1 Small for narrower faces rather than a wide version, so broad faces should look elsewhere. Like most frameless builds the glass ships with mold-release residue, and owners report needing several rounds of burning or abrasive scrubbing before any defog holds. There is no prescription-lens path either. As a first mask or a pocket backup, the price settles the argument.

11. Aqua Lung Plazma

The Plazma is Aqua Lung's frameless mono-lens mask, built on what the company calls Micromask Technology: pressure-reduction zones molded into the silicone skirt at the points where masks dig in over a long dive. The lens has a high brow line and teardrop lower corners, so gauge checks happen with the eyes rather than the neck. Buckles release with a one-touch button that works in thick gloves, and the X-shaped strap spreads its load across the back of the head instead of concentrating it in a single band. Two lens tints are sold — clear, and an amber that cuts surface glare and warms reds at depth — though the tint is tied to specific versions of the mask rather than chosen separately. The skirt is a soft surgical-grade silicone and the mask comes in one size, cut generously. That cut is the catch: divers with narrow faces regularly report seepage, and Aqua Lung offers no small version. For medium-to-large faces it delivers frameless comfort and a wide view at mid-range money.

12. Cressi Calibro

The Calibro is Cressi's low-volume two-lens mask: 85 cm³ internal volume, 118 g, built on the company's Integrated Dual Frame design that rakes the lenses back close to the eyes. The unusual part is the Fog-Stop System, a thin silicone membrane around the nose pocket that deflects exhaled air away from the glass during equalization, which is the main fogging source in small-volume masks. It helps rather than cures — owners still pre-treat the lenses and carry defog, and a few wrote the membrane off entirely. Divers with wider noses also report it rubbing the sides of the nose. The buckles sit on flexible rubber arms integrated into the frame, so strap adjustments do not shift the seal, and every component wears a matte finish to stop internal reflections. Fit runs small-to-medium, so large faces should try it on first. Made in Italy and sold alongside a mirrored-lens SF variant, it suits divers who want a gauge-glance field of view without the fog penalty that low-volume masks usually carry.

13. Beuchat Maxlux S

The Maxlux S is Beuchat's frameless single-lens mask, with the silicone skirt molded straight onto the tempered glass. That construction gets internal volume down to 127 cm3 — 10 cm3 under the standard Maxlux — and weight to 185 g, so it clears on a short exhale and folds flat in a BC pocket. The hypoallergenic skirt is unusually soft and does the sealing work itself, which means you can dive it with a loose strap. The number to check is the 120 mm skirt width: it fits narrow faces well and wide faces poorly. The single lens gives a bright, close view of the reef in front of you, though the horizontal spread is tighter than twin-lens designs. The micrometric buckles adjust in fine steps but their latches want two hands once you are in thick gloves. At roughly $44-58 from Diveinn and scuba.com, it is one of the cheapest ways into a genuinely low-volume mask.

14. Apeks VX1

The VX1 is Apeks' only mask, and it is a focused one: a 160 g frameless single-lens design that packs flat and clears with a small exhale. The 4 mm tempered lens carries an anti-scratch coating and comes two ways — Pure Clear, a low-iron glass that removes the usual green cast, or UV Cut, which filters harsh blue light for shallow tropical work. The surgical-grade silicone skirt is shaped to avoid pressure points over multi-dive days; the trade-off is that its seal is face-dependent, and some divers report losing it on a held inhale. The buckles are the strong point for cold water: quick-release levers on stainless-steel rollers that adjust cleanly in dry gloves, paired with a nose pocket you can actually reach through 5 mm neoprene. Supply of the UV Cut version has been patchy in the US, with pre-orders sitting for months. At $179 at House of Scuba it costs more than most frameless rivals, so try one on first — if it seals on your face, it is a mask that works equally well under a drysuit hood or on a liveaboard.

15. TUSA Paragon

The Paragon is TUSA's flagship twin-lens mask and reads like a spec sheet: CrystalView optical glass, an anti-reflective coating claimed to pass 95% of available light, and UV 420 treatment that filters UV and high-energy visible light. The Tri-Mix frame bolts a metal cross-beam and polyurethane bumpers to the polycarbonate chassis, which is why it survives gear bins that kill lighter masks — and why it weighs 245 g with a 215 cm3 internal volume. The Freedom Fit II skirt uses varied silicone thicknesses, stability ridges and a round sealing edge, and it fits an unusually wide spread of faces; the five-position skirt-mounted strap adjusters let you set the strap angle so it stops pulling hair or riding a hood seam. Prescription-ready lenses keep it viable as eyes age. The complaints are consistent: it costs around $200, it feels heavy next to frameless designs, and a minority of owners report water seeping in where the frame clamps the glass. TUSA's warranty desk has also been slow, with at least one buckle claim going unanswered. Buy it for the optics and the fit, and inspect the frame-to-glass seam on delivery.

16. Hollis M3

The M3 is Hollis' twin-lens workhorse, built around low-iron Saint-Gobain Diamant tempered glass — the low iron content raises light transmission and removes the green cast of ordinary tempered lenses, which is exactly what you want at 30 m or inside a wreck. The rigid frame keeps the profile low and the internal volume small, so clearing costs a mouthful of gas rather than a breath. The skirt is 100% silicone with a double feathered edge, and the wide split strap plus low-profile 4-way buckles hold position under a hood without snagging. Fit is the thing to check: on some faces the silicone presses against the base of the nose and a small trickle of water collects there, a complaint that recurs in owner reports. Hollis quotes no weight or volume numbers, which is an odd omission for a mask pitched on its low volume. It takes optional prescription lenses and ships with a protective box. Street prices run from about $92 at Diveinn to $130 at US shops, mid-pack money for glass this good.
