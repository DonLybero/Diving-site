Scubapro Go Sport Review & Best Price

Built for divers who fly: about 700 grams per fin, short enough for carry-on, with a self-adjusting bungee that fits over boots or bare feet.

#6 in Top Fins in 2026

What makes it good?

The Go Sport is the boot-fit sibling of Scubapro's Go Travel, scaled up so a drysuit boot fits where the original only took bare feet. The whole fin is a single molding of Monprene, a thermoplastic elastomer with no laminated joints to peel apart, and Scubapro leans on that for its durability claim rather than marketing gloss. The blade is pre-angled 25° with a central power panel that stiffens it lengthwise and channels water off the tip; in pool testing it out-pulled larger fins for the effort spent. In size M each fin weighs about 0.7 kg and measures roughly 51 cm, so a pair packs inside IATA carry-on limits — rare for a fin that takes cold-water boots. Removable skegs bolt onto the blade to cut sideslip for divers who want more tracking. The honest weakness is stiffness: against a strong current the blade folds earlier than a full-size paddle, and the fix is buying the stiffer Gorilla variant instead. Fit discipline matters too, because the pocket is cut for a boot and swims on a thin sock. As a one-fin quiver for both tropical trips and temperate shore dives, little else covers the same range.

Where it wins

  • One-piece 100% Monprene molding has no glue joints, so there is nothing to delaminate or tear the way multi-material fins fail after years of hard use
  • The 25° pre-angled blade with a central power panel channels water without the ankle load of a long paddle, and it scored at the top for power versus effort in pool testing
  • At about 0.7 kg per fin in size M and roughly 51 cm long, a pair fits inside carry-on limits
  • The foot pocket is cut for boots, including hard-sole drysuit boots, which most travel-sized fins refuse to swallow
  • The self-adjusting bungee heel goes on and off with one pull, useful on a pitching boat deck

Where it falls short

  • The blade is on the soft side; divers report working hard to hold position against a heavy current, which is exactly why Scubapro sells the stiffer Gorilla version
  • The boot-fit pocket is roomy, so wearing thin socks or bare feet means sloppy power transfer — size to your boot, not your shoe
  • The bungee cannot be adjusted, so between-size feet rely on the cord's stretch being right
