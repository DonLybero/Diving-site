Best Scuba Diving in September

← AugustAll monthsOctober →

September keeps the eastern Pacific on top — Cocos' hammerhead schools and Galápagos' whale sharks at full strength — while the Sea of Cortez switches on with sea lions, mobula fever and the warmest water of its year.

The Whitsundays fill with humpback mothers and calves, and Komodo stays in peak form.

South America

2 picks, all at peak season — Malpelo Island leads.

Malpelo Island — Colombia

Peak 28°C 30m viz Strong current

Five hundred kilometers off Colombia's Pacific coast, Malpelo is a bare volcanic rock rising from a seamount patrolled by sharks in staggering numbers. Walls of 200-plus scalloped hammerheads stack up over cleaning stations like La Nevera, silkies swarm by the hundred, and cold-season dives can turn up the rarely seen smalltooth sand tiger. Reached only by liveaboard, its surge-scoured pinnacles and powerful currents demand reef hooks and serious drift experience.

What to expect in September: whale sharks feeding while silky shark schools swirl and hammerheads patrol the seamount. Conditions: warm with wet-season rains topside, calm seas and superb visibility.

Galapagos Islands — Ecuador

Peak 21°C 18m viz Strong current

Converging ocean currents collide at this volcanic archipelago 1,000 kilometers off Ecuador, stacking cold, nutrient-loaded water against the equator, which is why nothing else dives like the Galapagos. Liveaboards push north to Darwin and Wolf, where whale sharks cruise past walls of scalloped hammerheads from June to November, while central sites add playful sea lions, mola mola, and feeding marine iguanas. Expect negative entries, down-currents, and surge: this is advanced diving, and worth every degree of chill.

What to expect in September: whale sharks, hammerheads and mola crowding Darwin and Wolf in numbers. Conditions: coldest and rough with low visibility.

Central America

Cocos Island carries the region this month.

Cocos Island — Costa Rica

Peak 25°C 18m viz Strong current

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

What to expect in September: hammerheads massing in huge schools while whale sharks and mantas work the seamount. Conditions: wet season with rough seas and low visibility.

Oceania

3 picks, all at peak season — Whitsunday Islands leads.

Whitsunday Islands — Australia

Peak 24°C 32m viz Medium current

Seventy-four islands scatter across the Coral Sea off central Queensland, pairing sheltered fringing reefs with runs to the outer Great Barrier Reef. Hook Island's bays deliver swim-throughs, Maori wrasse, and manta rays from May through September, while Hardy Reef adds giant Queensland grouper on the outer barrier. Humpback whales pass through from June to September. Calm bays and shallow profiles make this one of Australia's easiest places to log relaxed day-boat and liveaboard dives.

What to expect in September: humpback whales heading through with calves while mantas and turtles work the reefs. Conditions: warming calm water with excellent visibility.

Fiji

Peak 26°C 32m viz Strong current

Tidal currents ripping through the Somosomo Strait feed the densest soft-coral gardens in the Pacific, and when the white corals on the Great White Wall open to feed, the whole reef seems to cascade. Beqa Lagoon counters with a famous baited shark dive that brings in big bull sharks, while Namena's Grand Central Station funnels barracuda and grey reefs past like commuters. Resort day boats and liveaboards cover it all; July through October is best.

What to expect in September: mantas still working the channels while sharks crowd the famous feeding dives. Conditions: dry weather and excellent visibility.

French Polynesia

Peak 26°C 35m viz Strong current

The Tuamotu atolls concentrate the drift diving of your life into their passes: ride the incoming tide through Rangiroa's Tiputa alongside a hundred grey reef sharks and resident dolphins, or hang at Fakarava South while the famous wall of sharks stacks overhead. Bora Bora adds a lagoon manta cleaning station for calmer days. The big passes reward divers comfortable in current, reached by resort day boats or liveaboard, June through October.

What to expect in September: sharks crowding the channel mouths while humpback whales breach along the outer reef. Conditions: dry season with thirty to forty metres of visibility.

Asia

3 picks, all at peak season — Nusa Penida leads.

Nusa Penida — Indonesia

Peak 24°C 28m viz Strong current

Twenty minutes across the channel from Bali, Nusa Penida sits square in the Indonesian Throughflow, and its diving runs on that moving water: high-speed drifts along the north coast, reef mantas circling the south-coast cleaning stations all year, and - when cold upwellings arrive from July to October - oceanic sunfish rising to be cleaned at Crystal Bay. Currents shift fast and can pull downward, so dive it with sharp buoyancy and a guide who reads the water.

What to expect in September: mola-mola rising on the coldest upwellings while reef mantas work the cleaning stations. Conditions: dry season with heavy upwelling, cool water and top visibility.

Maldives

Peak 29°C 10m viz Medium current

Scattered across 26 atolls in the Indian Ocean, the Maldives runs on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through channel mouths and over isolated thilas, stacking grey reef sharks, eagle rays and schooling snapper wherever it pushes hardest. December to April brings the clearest water; May to November shifts mantas and whale sharks to the plankton-rich eastern atolls. Liveaboards cover the most ground, resorts add house-reef ease, and solid drift skills pay off everywhere.

What to expect in September: mantas swarming Hanifaru Bay while whale sharks glide through the plankton. Conditions: thick plankton at Hanifaru with lower visibility.

Full guide: Maldives →

Koh Tao — Thailand

Peak 29°C 22m viz Moderate current

Koh Tao is where the Gulf of Thailand earns its reputation as an easy, generous place to dive: warm, calm bays over shallow reef for new divers, and offshore granite pinnacles that punch far above their weight. Chumphon Pinnacle and Sail Rock stack barracuda, trevally and — in spring and early autumn — whale sharks over anemone-covered rock. Budget-friendly and boat rides are short; skip the November-December monsoon and it delivers year-round.

What to expect in September: whale-shark sightings picking back up while the pinnacles hit their fishiest stretch. Conditions: calm seas and clear water before the northeast monsoon builds.

North America

2 picks, all at peak season — Sea of Cortez leads.

Sea of Cortez — Mexico

Peak 30°C 32m viz Medium current

Squeezed between Baja California and mainland Mexico, the Sea of Cortez trades coral walls for sheer biomass: sea lion pups that mouth your fins at Los Islotes, tornadoes of jacks over Cabo Pulmo's hard-coral fingers, and mobula rays massing in the blue. Day boats from La Paz add wrecks like the Salvatierra, and whale sharks gather in the bay from October to April. Advanced divers push out to El Bajo seamount for hammerheads; warm, clear late summer is prime.

What to expect in September: hammerheads circling the seamounts while sea lions hunt and jacks swirl in tornadoes. Conditions: warmest and calm with excellent visibility.

Maui & Kona — USA

Peak 27°C 32m viz Low current

Hawaii's twin dive hubs pack volcanic drama into calm leeward water. Off Kona, dive lights draw plankton, and feeding manta rays, to Garden Eel Cove nearly every night of the year, while Maui day boats run to Molokini, a sunken crescent crater with visibility pushing 45 m and a sheer back wall dropping past 100 m. Lava caverns off Lanai and turtle-thick shore entries round out a destination that serves new divers and wall-drift veterans alike.

What to expect in September: mantas swooping the light column while turtles cruise the reef flats. Conditions: calm seas with excellent visibility.

Middle East

Muscat & Daymaniyat Islands carries the region this month.

Muscat & Daymaniyat Islands — Oman

Peak 30°C 12m viz Low current

An hour's boat ride from Oman's capital, the Daymaniyat Islands are a strictly protected reserve where Gulf of Oman nutrients meet genuinely healthy hard coral. Winter brings clear water, leopard sharks and turtles; summer plankton blooms trade visibility for whale sharks that peak in September and October, with mobula squadrons either side. Add Fahal Island and the sheltered wreck-filled bays of Bandar Khayran, and it suits any diver who values animals over infinity-pool viz.

What to expect in September: whale shark aggregations building, sometimes several per dive, with mobulas alongside. Conditions: blooms easing and visibility rebuilding over warm calm seas.

Africa

2 picks, all at peak season — Protea Banks leads.

Protea Banks — South Africa

Peak 21°C 22m viz Strong current

Protea Banks starts where most reefs end: a deep offshore reef of caves, pinnacles and gullies lying 27-40 m down off South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal coast, dived as fast blue-water drifts with negative entries and open-water ascents. Zambezi (bull) sharks patrol year-round, wintering ragged-tooth sharks pack the caves, and summer adds tiger sharks and schooling hammerheads, with the sardine run streaming past in June and July. This is uncompromising, experienced-divers-only territory.

What to expect in September: late ragged-tooth sharks overlapping with the first scalloped hammerheads while bull sharks hold the reef. Conditions: warming water and good clarity with current running hard either direction.

Marsa Alam — Egypt

Peak 28°C 25m viz Moderate current

Marsa Alam is the quieter, wilder counterweight to Egypt's northern resorts: a long ribbon of fringing reef where you can shore dive straight off the sand, then boat out to serious pelagic theatre. Elphinstone's plateaus host oceanic whitetips from October to December, Abu Dabbab's seagrass shelters dugong and green turtles, and Daedalus delivers hammerheads in liveaboard range. It suits relaxed reef divers and shark-focused drift specialists alike — often on the same trip.

What to expect in September: Oceanic whitetips arriving at Elphinstone; dolphins, turtles and dugong all in play. Conditions: Warm water, mild wind, thinning crowds — the sweet spot begins.

Europe

Azores carries the region this month.

Azores — Portugal

Peak 24°C 30m viz Strong current

Nine volcanic Portuguese islands anchor the mid-Atlantic, and their diving happens in serious blue water. Offshore seamounts like Princess Alice Bank draw squadrons of mobula rays, Condor Bank stages blue and mako shark encounters, and visibility routinely tops 30 meters. Currents run strong out there, so the banks are boat trips for confident divers, though coastal wrecks and Terceira's anchor graveyard offer easier days. July through October is prime season.

What to expect in September: blue sharks still strong on the banks while mobulas school and a whale chance lingers. Conditions: warm and settled with thirty metre visibility.
