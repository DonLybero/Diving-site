Best Scuba Diving in August

Where the water is at its best in August, region by region.

← JulyAll monthsSeptember →

August is big-animal month in the eastern Pacific: Cocos and Galápagos run their hammerhead and whale-shark peaks as cool, nutrient-rich water floods the archipelagos.

The Great Barrier Reef and Komodo both sit at the top of their seasons — mola mola come shallow off Komodo's south coast — and the Azores deliver their best blue-water shark diving.

South America

3 picks, all at peak season — Malpelo Island leads.

Malpelo Island — Colombia

Peak 27°C 30m viz Strong current

Five hundred kilometers off Colombia's Pacific coast, Malpelo is a bare volcanic rock rising from a seamount patrolled by sharks in staggering numbers. Walls of 200-plus scalloped hammerheads stack up over cleaning stations like La Nevera, silkies swarm by the hundred, and cold-season dives can turn up the rarely seen smalltooth sand tiger. Reached only by liveaboard, its surge-scoured pinnacles and powerful currents demand reef hooks and serious drift experience.

What to expect in August: whale sharks cruising in while massive silky shark aggregations swirl and hammerheads school. Conditions: warmest water with calm seas and excellent blue-water visibility.

Galapagos Islands — Ecuador

Peak 21°C 12m viz Strong current

Converging ocean currents collide at this volcanic archipelago 1,000 kilometers off Ecuador, stacking cold, nutrient-loaded water against the equator, which is why nothing else dives like the Galapagos. Liveaboards push north to Darwin and Wolf, where whale sharks cruise past walls of scalloped hammerheads from June to November, while central sites add playful sea lions, mola mola, and feeding marine iguanas. Expect negative entries, down-currents, and surge: this is advanced diving, and worth every degree of chill.

What to expect in August: whale sharks and hammerheads crowding Darwin and Wolf while mola mola rise from the deep. Conditions: coldest water with cold thermoclines and visibility five to twenty metres.

Fernando de Noronha — Brazil

Peak 26°C 30m viz Medium current

Some 350 km off Brazil's northeast coast, this volcanic archipelago sits inside a strictly protected marine park where visibility routinely tops 30 m. Day boats work canyons, tunnels and arches like Pedras Secas while huge spinner dolphin pods, turtles and lemon and reef sharks patrol the blue. Most sites suit newer divers in the dry Aug-Dec season, and technical divers get a genuine prize: an intact navy corvette upright at 62 m.

What to expect in August: spinner dolphins swirling at the surface while sharks and turtles work the clear blue below. Conditions: dry and calm with visibility beyond thirty metres.

Oceania

2 picks, all at peak season — Great Barrier Reef leads.

Great Barrier Reef — Australia

Peak 23°C 70m viz Medium current

Stretching more than 2,300 kilometers along Queensland's coast, the Great Barrier Reef is the largest reef system on Earth, and its best diving lives offshore. Liveaboards run the Ribbon Reefs for Cod Hole's tame potato cod and June-July dwarf minke whale encounters, then push into the Coral Sea for shark action at Osprey's North Horn. Day boats from Cairns cover easy coral gardens, while the current-swept SS Yongala wreck rewards advanced divers.

What to expect in August: humpbacks passing overhead while reef and hammerhead sharks patrol Osprey Reef and potato cod hold court. Conditions: dry, calm and clear with Coral Sea visibility past forty metres.

French Polynesia

Peak 26°C 24m viz Strong current

The Tuamotu atolls concentrate the drift diving of your life into their passes: ride the incoming tide through Rangiroa's Tiputa alongside a hundred grey reef sharks and resident dolphins, or hang at Fakarava South while the famous wall of sharks stacks overhead. Bora Bora adds a lagoon manta cleaning station for calmer days. The big passes reward divers comfortable in current, reached by resort day boats or liveaboard, June through October.

What to expect in August: the shark wall holding thick in the passes while humpback whales sing offshore. Conditions: dry, cool and clear conditions.

Asia

2 picks, all at peak season — Komodo National Park leads.

Komodo National Park — Indonesia

Peak 27°C 30m viz Strong current

Tides squeeze between the Pacific and Indian Oceans through Komodo's narrow straits, and the currents they generate power some of the most electric diving in Indonesia. Hook into the reef at Castle Rock or Crystal Rock as sharks and trevally hunt the flow, fire through the Shotgun's channel, then drift Makassar Reef's manta cleaning stations. The warm north and cold, nutrient-rich south fish differently by season, so liveaboards cover it best. Bring drift experience.

What to expect in August: mola mola rising to the southern cleaning stations while mantas and reef sharks hold the north. Conditions: dry with northern visibility to thirty metres and cold green south.

Maldives

Peak 29°C 18m viz Medium current

Scattered across 26 atolls in the Indian Ocean, the Maldives runs on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through channel mouths and over isolated thilas, stacking grey reef sharks, eagle rays and schooling snapper wherever it pushes hardest. December to April brings the clearest water; May to November shifts mantas and whale sharks to the plankton-rich eastern atolls. Liveaboards cover the most ground, resorts add house-reef ease, and solid drift skills pay off everywhere.

What to expect in August: mantas crowding Hanifaru Bay in the hundreds while whale-sharks join the feeding. Conditions: strong Hanifaru feeding with visibility ten to twenty-five metres.

North America

2 picks, all at peak season — Cancún & Playa del Carmen leads.

Cancún & Playa del Carmen — Mexico

Peak 29°C 20m viz Moderate current

The Cancún–Playa del Carmen corridor is high-volume Caribbean diving with two genuinely wild seasons stitched in. Winter brings pregnant bull sharks to a 24-metre sand flat off Playa and sailfish to the blue; summer stacks hundreds of whale sharks offshore. Between them you drift warm, easy reefs on the Yucatán Current, land on hurricane-split wrecks, and tour the sculpture galleries of MUSA. Short boat rides and year-round warm water suit everyone from new divers to shark-focused veterans.

What to expect in August: whale sharks swirling in a huge aggregation with mantas mixed in while turtles hold the reefs. Conditions: warmest water of the year with calm spells between tropical squalls.

Maui & Kona — USA

Peak 27°C 24m viz Low current

Hawaii's twin dive hubs pack volcanic drama into calm leeward water. Off Kona, dive lights draw plankton, and feeding manta rays, to Garden Eel Cove nearly every night of the year, while Maui day boats run to Molokini, a sunken crescent crater with visibility pushing 45 m and a sheer back wall dropping past 100 m. Lava caverns off Lanai and turtle-thick shore entries round out a destination that serves new divers and wall-drift veterans alike.

What to expect in August: mantas feeding under the lights while dolphins spin in the warm shallows. Conditions: calm, warm and clear water.

Central America

Cocos Island carries the region this month.

Cocos Island — Costa Rica

Peak 25°C 18m viz Strong current

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

What to expect in August: hammerheads schooling in the hundreds while whale sharks and mantas cruise the current. Conditions: wet-season peak with cold thermoclines and visibility ten to twenty-five metres.

Europe

3 picks, all at peak season — Azores leads.

Azores — Portugal

Peak 24°C 30m viz Strong current

Nine volcanic Portuguese islands anchor the mid-Atlantic, and their diving happens in serious blue water. Offshore seamounts like Princess Alice Bank draw squadrons of mobula rays, Condor Bank stages blue and mako shark encounters, and visibility routinely tops 30 meters. Currents run strong out there, so the banks are boat trips for confident divers, though coastal wrecks and Terceira's anchor graveyard offer easier days. July through October is prime season.

What to expect in August: blue and mako sharks cruising the banks while mantas and dolphins work the seamounts. Conditions: warmest water with calm seas and thirty metre visibility.

Kaş — Turkey

Peak 28°C 35m viz mild to moderate current

Set on Turkey's Lycian coast, Kaş packs 30-plus dive sites within minutes of its harbor, all in the famously clear water of the Turquoise Coast. History does the heavy lifting: an intact DC-3 Dakota rests on sand at around 25 m, a full-size Bronze Age Uluburun replica sits in a shallow bay, and ancient amphorae litter the reefs. Walls, caverns and the current-brushed Canyon add drama, with day boats serving everyone from new divers to seasoned wall hunters.

What to expect in August: turtles gliding over the reefs while pelagics sweep through on nearly every dive. Conditions: warmest water of the year with flat calm days and long visibility.

Full guide: Kaş →

Ustica — Italy

Peak 26°C 40m viz mild to moderate current

A volcanic speck in the Tyrrhenian Sea north of Sicily, Ustica became Italy's first marine protected area in 1986, and four decades of protection show underwater: crystal water, walls thick with gorgonians and sponges, and dusky groupers bold enough to follow you around. Lava rock carves the caves, arches and tunnels of the Scoglio del Medico pinnacle, while two archaeological trails let you hover over ancient anchors and amphorae. Shore and day-boat diving runs from sheltered beginner bays to current-brushed offshore seamounts.

What to expect in August: big groupers holding court while pelagic amberjack and barracuda hunt at their most active. Conditions: warmest water of the year with superb visibility and busy boats.

Africa

2 picks, all at peak season — Sharm El Sheikh leads.

Sharm El Sheikh — Egypt

Peak 29°C 30m viz Moderate current

Sharm El Sheikh is the northern Red Sea's classic dive hub, where desert mountains drop into anthias-carpeted walls at Ras Mohammed and tidal drifts sweep the four reefs of the Tiran Strait. June to September brings the famous spawning aggregations — snapper, batfish and barracuda massing off Shark and Yolanda — while day boats reach the SS Thistlegorm. Diveable all twelve months, it pairs easy training bays with genuinely big-animal drift diving.

What to expect in August: schooling fish still thick; scalloped hammerheads in the blue at Tiran. Conditions: warmest water (28-29C), calm crossings to Tiran and the Thistlegorm.

Full guide: Sharm El Sheikh →

Protea Banks — South Africa

Peak 20°C 24m viz Strong current

Protea Banks starts where most reefs end: a deep offshore reef of caves, pinnacles and gullies lying 27-40 m down off South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal coast, dived as fast blue-water drifts with negative entries and open-water ascents. Zambezi (bull) sharks patrol year-round, wintering ragged-tooth sharks pack the caves, and summer adds tiger sharks and schooling hammerheads, with the sardine run streaming past in June and July. This is uncompromising, experienced-divers-only territory.

What to expect in August: ragged-tooth sharks filling the Northern caves while bull sharks cruise and guitarfish rest on the sand. Conditions: clear winter water, cold on the bottom, with strong drift dives.
