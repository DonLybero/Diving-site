Best Scuba Diving in November

Where the water is at its best in November, region by region.

← OctoberAll monthsDecember →

November puts the Indian Ocean first: the Seychelles' second calm season brings glassy water and whale-shark passes, while the southern Red Sea keeps the shark encounters coming. La Paz opens whale-shark season in the Sea of Cortez, Tiger Beach hits its stride in the Bahamas, and Fernando de Noronha's dry-season visibility tops thirty metres.

Africa

4 picks, all at peak season — Seychelles leads.

Seychelles

Peak 28°C 32m viz Medium current

Granite sets the Seychelles apart from anywhere else in the Indian Ocean: ancient boulders tumble off the beaches and continue underwater as sculpted reefs, swim-throughs and fish-wrapped pinnacles. Inner-island diving off Mahe and Praslin stays mild and beginner-friendly, with hawksbill turtles and reef sharks routine and whale sharks cruising through from August to November. The remote outer atolls, Aldabra among them, are liveaboard territory for divers chasing wilder, sharkier walls.

What to expect in November: Whale sharks possible (rarer than historically); manta peak. Conditions: Calm, clear; excellent all-round.

Tofo — Mozambique

Peak 26°C 22m viz medium current

A laid-back beach town on Mozambique's Inhambane coast, Tofo built its name on megafauna. Reef mantas hold court year-round at deep cleaning stations like Manta Reef and Giants Castle, whale sharks cruise offshore from October to March, and humpbacks migrate past between June and October. Dives launch by RIB through the surf; shallow inshore reefs suit newer divers, but the signature manta sites are deep drifts for experienced hands.

What to expect in November: Whale sharks, mantas, reef sharks. Conditions: Warm, early wet season, strong viz.

Red Sea (Egypt) — Egypt

Peak 25°C 18m viz Medium current

Few places pack this much diving into warm, clear, year-round water. Egypt's Red Sea runs from the Thistlegorm's cargo holds of trucks and motorcycles to the coral walls of Ras Mohammed, then out to the marine-park reefs of the Brothers, Daedalus, and Elphinstone, where oceanic whitetips and schooling hammerheads patrol strong drift. Dive it from shore, day boat, or liveaboard; sheltered bays suit beginners while the offshore reefs demand experience.

What to expect in November: Oceanic whitetip + thresher PEAK (Brothers); mantas. Conditions: Prime shark month; cooling.

Protea Banks — South Africa

Peak 24°C 22m viz Strong current

Protea Banks starts where most reefs end: a deep offshore reef of caves, pinnacles and gullies lying 27-40 m down off South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal coast, dived as fast blue-water drifts with negative entries and open-water ascents. Zambezi (bull) sharks patrol year-round, wintering ragged-tooth sharks pack the caves, and summer adds tiger sharks and schooling hammerheads, with the sardine run streaming past in June and July. This is uncompromising, experienced-divers-only territory.

What to expect in November: Huge schools of scalloped hammerheads at their most regular; bull and tiger sharks over the Southern Pinnacles.. Conditions: Warm summer water returning; strong drift, visibility usually good..

North America

2 picks, all at peak season — Sea of Cortez leads.

Sea of Cortez — Mexico

Peak 25°C 18m viz Medium current

Squeezed between Baja California and mainland Mexico, the Sea of Cortez trades coral walls for sheer biomass: sea lion pups that mouth your fins at Los Islotes, tornadoes of jacks over Cabo Pulmo's hard-coral fingers, and mobula rays massing in the blue. Day boats from La Paz add wrecks like the Salvatierra, and whale sharks gather in the bay from October to April. Advanced divers push out to El Bajo seamount for hammerheads; warm, clear late summer is prime.

What to expect in November: Sea lions, whale sharks, mobulas. Conditions: Warm, great viz, whale sharks return.

Vancouver Island — Canada

Peak 10°C 25m viz Strong current

Cold, current-fed Pacific water turns this British Columbia coastline into an invertebrate riot: Browning Wall is carpeted stem to stern in soft corals and anemones, wolf eels and giant Pacific octopus den in the rocks, and Steller sea lions buzz divers at Race Rocks. Big artificial reefs like HMCS Saskatchewan add wreck steel. Slack-window boat diving in a drysuit, at its clearest October through March, with Ogden Point offering an easy shore option.

What to expect in November: Sea lions, wolf eels, octopus. Conditions: 20-30m viz, cold, superb clarity.

Caribbean

Bahamas carries the region this month.

Bahamas

Peak 26°C 24m viz Medium current

No destination puts big sharks in front of divers more reliably than the Bahamas. Baited dives on shallow sandbanks bring tiger and lemon sharks within arm's length, great hammerheads and oceanic whitetips show in winter, and reef sharks patrol nearly every wall. Between shark runs there are upright freighter wrecks, coral-rimmed blue holes and ripping channel drifts like Current Cut. Day boats and liveaboards cover it, with Nov-May the prime window.

What to expect in November: Tiger sharks (Tiger Beach), reef sharks. Conditions: Tiger season strong, cooling, good viz.

South America

2 picks, 1 at peak — Fernando de Noronha leads.

Fernando de Noronha — Brazil

Peak 27°C 32m viz Medium current

Some 350 km off Brazil's northeast coast, this volcanic archipelago sits inside a strictly protected marine park where visibility routinely tops 30 m. Day boats work canyons, tunnels and arches like Pedras Secas while huge spinner dolphin pods, turtles and lemon and reef sharks patrol the blue. Most sites suit newer divers in the dry Aug-Dec season, and technical divers get a genuine prize: an intact navy corvette upright at 62 m.

What to expect in November: Spinner dolphins, sharks, turtles. Conditions: Dry, excellent viz, calm.

Galapagos Islands — Ecuador

Good 22°C 18m viz Strong current

Converging ocean currents collide at this volcanic archipelago 1,000 kilometers off Ecuador, stacking cold, nutrient-loaded water against the equator, which is why nothing else dives like the Galapagos. Liveaboards push north to Darwin and Wolf, where whale sharks cruise past walls of scalloped hammerheads from June to November, while central sites add playful sea lions, mola mola, and feeding marine iguanas. Expect negative entries, down-currents, and surge: this is advanced diving, and worth every degree of chill.

What to expect in November: Hammerheads; mola; megafauna tapering. Conditions: Transition; warming begins.

Oceania

3 picks, 1 at peak — South West Rocks leads.

South West Rocks — Australia

Peak 22°C 24m viz Medium current

The drawcard at this New South Wales mid-north-coast town is a single rock with a 125m cave running clean through it. Divers enter Fish Rock's deep entrance, pass wobbegongs and bull rays in the dark, and exit the shallow end into gutters patrolled by critically endangered grey nurse sharks - sometimes twenty or thirty at once. Tidal currents and surge demand some experience, and grey nurse numbers peak May to December on straightforward day-boat runs.

What to expect in November: Grey nurse; turtles, rays. Conditions: Warming, good viz.

Palau

Good 29°C 18m viz Strong current

Tides drive everything in Palau. Water pours through this Micronesian archipelago's reef passes, and at Blue Corner divers hook into the reef lip while grey reef sharks parade past in the flow; German Channel trades speed for manta rays circling a cleaning station. Between drifts come the vertical shafts of Blue Holes, the air-filled chambers of Chandelier Cave and lunar spawning aggregations. Day boats and liveaboards both work, but the strong currents flatter experienced divers.

What to expect in November: Mantas; whale sharks (Nov-Apr); Jellyfish Lake. Conditions: Dry season returns; calming.

Yap — Micronesia (FSM)

Good 29°C 27m viz Medium current

Few places deliver mantas as reliably as Yap, a quiet Micronesian island where resident reef mantas queue at cleaning stations in the tidal channels year-round. Dives are timed to the tide as short drifts or hooked hangs at Mi'l and Goofnuw Channels, peaking during the December-to-April mating season. Between manta runs there are shark-lined caverns to thread and a dusk mandarinfish ritual on the rubble, all dived comfortably by day boat.

What to expect in November: Mantas back on the barrier-reef stations as the winter build-up starts.. Conditions: Early dry season, drier and calmer with improving clarity..

Asia

2 picks, 0 at peak — Fuvahmulah leads.

Fuvahmulah — Maldives

Good 28°C 30m viz Strong current

A lone oceanic island in the Maldives' far south, Fuvahmulah has no sheltering atoll, just deep water on every side and the most reliable tiger shark diving on Earth. Named resident tigers patrol the shallow harbor plateau at arm's length, while drifts along the drop-offs deliver thresher shark cleaning stations, schooling hammerheads and oceanic mantas. Day boats run everything, and the exposed blue-water diving favors confident, current-comfortable divers.

What to expect in November: Resident tigers daily; prime thresher season continues and hammerhead schools grow as the thermocline shallows.. Conditions: Early dry northeast monsoon, calmer surface and rising visibility..

Cenderawasih Bay — Indonesia

Good 29°C 20m viz Low current

Tucked into West Papua's northern coast, Cenderawasih Bay is the only place to scuba with resident whale sharks year-round, the giants hanging vertically beneath fishermen's bagan platforms to sip baitfish from the nets. The bay's near-enclosed geography keeps the water glassy and virtually currentless, so the shallow shark encounters, coral-clad WWII wrecks and endemic-rich reefs all dive effortlessly. Access is by liveaboard, and the calm conditions welcome any certification level.

What to expect in November: Whale sharks continue under the bagans; wrecks and endemics active. Conditions: Transition into the wetter season; inner bay stays calm, transits get lumpier.

Central America

Cocos Island carries the region this month.

Cocos Island — Costa Rica

Good 26°C 18m viz Strong current

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

What to expect in November: Hammerheads tail; whale sharks; mantas. Conditions: Wet ends; currents easing.
