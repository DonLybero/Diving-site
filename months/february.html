Best Scuba Diving in February

Where the water is at its best in February, region by region.

← JanuaryAll monthsMarch →

February is Palau's moment — reef-hook season at Blue Corner with grey reef sharks stacked in the current — while Thailand's Similans deliver their calmest seas and Richelieu Rock's whale-shark window opens.

In the southern hemisphere the Poor Knights hit peak subtropical visibility, and Socorro keeps serving mantas and humpback song.

Asia

4 picks, all at peak season — Fuvahmulah leads.

Fuvahmulah — Maldives

Peak 28°C 35m viz Strong current

A lone oceanic island in the Maldives' far south, Fuvahmulah has no sheltering atoll, just deep water on every side and the most reliable tiger shark diving on Earth. Named resident tigers patrol the shallow harbor plateau at arm's length, while drifts along the drop-offs deliver thresher shark cleaning stations, schooling hammerheads and oceanic mantas. Day boats run everything, and the exposed blue-water diving favors confident, current-comfortable divers.

What to expect in February: tiger sharks holding year-round while silvertips arrive in the hundreds with hammerhead schools and whale sharks. Conditions: dry northeast monsoon with settled surface and gin-clear water.

Similan Islands — Thailand

Peak 28°C 32m viz Medium current

Granite defines the Similans: huge smooth boulders tumble from white beaches to well past 40m, forming the swim-throughs and canyons of Elephant Head Rock on the exposed west, with gentle coral bommies and sand on the east. Liveaboards work the chain during the short open season, pushing north to Koh Bon's manta cleaning station and Richelieu Rock, a soft-coral pinnacle with Thailand's best whale shark odds. February to April brings the calmest Andaman seas and the biggest animals.

What to expect in February: mantas circling the pinnacles while whale sharks cruise past and leopard sharks rest below. Conditions: calm seas with excellent visibility.

Okinawa Islands — Japan

Peak 21°C 24m viz Strong current

Japan's subtropical island chain strings across the East China Sea, and its diving swings from easy shore entries to serious drift. Winter sends schooling scalloped hammerheads past Yonaguni's fast currents, near the stepped stone monument that still fuels debate over its origins, while summer mantas hover over Ishigaki's cleaning stations. Around the main island, the glowing Blue Cave suits new divers and the deep USS Emmons wreck demands technical training. Day boats reach nearly all of it.

What to expect in February: hammerhead schools swirling thick off Yonaguni while divers hang in open blue. Conditions: coolest clear water with strong current.

Raja Ampat — Indonesia

Peak 28°C 18m viz Strong current

Off West Papua's Bird's Head Peninsula, Raja Ampat's four big islands and hundreds of karst islets sit at the epicenter of global marine biodiversity; Cape Kri holds the record fish count for a single dive. Nutrient-rich currents pump through the Dampier Strait, feeding seamounts like Blue Magic where oceanic mantas circle above carpeted wobbegongs. Reef hooks earn the fishiest sites, but sheltered cleaning stations and jetty dives balance the itinerary. Dive it by liveaboard or resort, October through April.

What to expect in February: mantas queueing at the cleaning stations while the reef biodiversity dazzles on every dive. Conditions: coolest calm water with good visibility.

Oceania

3 picks, all at peak season — Palau leads.

Palau

Peak 28°C 40m viz Strong current

Tides drive everything in Palau. Water pours through this Micronesian archipelago's reef passes, and at Blue Corner divers hook into the reef lip while grey reef sharks parade past in the flow; German Channel trades speed for manta rays circling a cleaning station. Between drifts come the vertical shafts of Blue Holes, the air-filled chambers of Chandelier Cave and lunar spawning aggregations. Day boats and liveaboards both work, but the strong currents flatter experienced divers.

What to expect in February: snapper and moorish idol spawning on the full moon while mantas and grey reef sharks patrol. Conditions: coolest and calmest seas with thirty to forty metre visibility.

Poor Knights Islands — New Zealand

Peak 22°C 28m viz Medium current

Two volcanic islands off New Zealand's Northland coast, the Poor Knights sit inside a strict marine reserve washed by a warm subtropical current. The diving happens in arches, tunnels and sea caves - Rikoriko is the world's largest by volume - where stingrays hover mid-water, blue maomao school in the light beams, and kelp-fringed walls crawl with nudibranchs. Boat-only day trips run year-round, best January through April, with sites from sheltered arches to pinnacles dropping past 45m.

What to expect in February: stingrays gliding through the arches while mantas, kingfish and mola mola cruise the pinnacles. Conditions: warmest water of the year with twenty-five to thirty metre visibility.

Yap — Micronesia (FSM)

Peak 28°C 30m viz Medium current

Few places deliver mantas as reliably as Yap, a quiet Micronesian island where resident reef mantas queue at cleaning stations in the tidal channels year-round. Dives are timed to the tide as short drifts or hooked hangs at Mi'l and Goofnuw Channels, peaking during the December-to-April mating season. Between manta runs there are shark-lined caverns to thread and a dusk mandarinfish ritual on the rubble, all dived comfortably by day boat.

What to expect in February: mantas mating in full swing with multi-ray gatherings across the barrier reef. Conditions: dry-season peak with calm settled seas and excellent clarity.

Africa

2 picks, all at peak season — Tofo leads.

Tofo — Mozambique

Peak 28°C 25m viz medium current

A laid-back beach town on Mozambique's Inhambane coast, Tofo built its name on megafauna. Reef mantas hold court year-round at deep cleaning stations like Manta Reef and Giants Castle, whale sharks cruise offshore from October to March, and humpbacks migrate past between June and October. Dives launch by RIB through the surf; shallow inshore reefs suit newer divers, but the signature manta sites are deep drifts for experienced hands.

What to expect in February: whale sharks feeding along the coast while mantas and leopard sharks work the reefs. Conditions: warm wet season with occasional rain and strong visibility.

Zanzibar — Tanzania

Peak 29°C 25m viz Moderate current

Zanzibar pairs easy tropical reef diving with a genuine big-animal calendar. Day boats work Mnemba Atoll's coral slopes, where green turtles and dolphins are routine, while Leven Bank's current-raked seamount gives experienced divers kingfish, tuna and barracuda in the Pemba Channel. Two monsoons shape the year: kaskazi brings warm, glassy water from December to March, kusi cooler humpback-season seas from June to October, and the April-May long rains close the window. Suits new divers and drift-hungry veterans alike.

What to expect in February: reef fish forming spawning aggregations while green turtles and resident bottlenose dolphins patrol. Conditions: hot and settled with glassy mornings and top-of-year visibility.

Central America

Coiba carries the region this month.

Coiba — Panama

Peak 27°C 25m viz strong current

Panama's UNESCO-listed Coiba National Park protects a swath of eastern Pacific where cool, nutrient-rich upwelling turns seamounts into pelagic magnets: schooling scalloped hammerheads (strongest March through July), resident whitetip reef sharks, and seasonal whale sharks and mantas. Expect negative entries onto current-swept pinnacles like Wahoo Rock, thermoclines that can dip to 16-20C, and blue-water ascents. Most diving runs as day trips or multi-day expeditions out of Santa Catalina, and it firmly favors experienced divers.

What to expect in February: whale sharks cruising through while turtles and mobulas glide over the seamounts above dense fish schools. Conditions: dry, calm and clear with strong currents on exposed pinnacles.

North America

3 picks, all at peak season — Socorro Island leads.

Socorro Island — Mexico

Peak 24°C 24m viz Strong current

Far out in the open Pacific south of Baja California, the volcanic Revillagigedo islands are reached only by liveaboard, and their giant oceanic mantas rank among the friendliest anywhere, banking within arm's reach at El Boiler's cleaning station. Roca Partida's lone spire stacks whitetips on its ledges while hammerheads, silvertips, and curious bottlenose dolphins patrol midwater. Humpback song fills winter dives. Surge and current across exposed pinnacles keep this advanced territory; the season runs November through May.

What to expect in February: mantas hovering at the boulders while humpbacks pass and dolphins ride the bow. Conditions: coolest water with hammerheads around and clear visibility.

Jupiter — USA

Peak 22°C 18m viz Strong current

On Florida's Atlantic coast, Jupiter puts the Gulf Stream to work: nearly every dive is a one-to-two-knot drift over ledges and wrecks in 15-30 m of blue water. Winter draws great hammerheads and thick lemon shark aggregations to sites like Lemon Drop; late summer packs goliath groupers onto the Wreck Trek for their spawn. Day boats run it all, and the constant current makes this advanced-leaning big-animal diving.

What to expect in February: lemon sharks massing while great hammerheads and bull sharks work the shark dives. Conditions: winter shark season with strong drift and cooler water.

Vancouver Island — Canada

Peak 7°C 25m viz Strong current

Cold, current-fed Pacific water turns this British Columbia coastline into an invertebrate riot: Browning Wall is carpeted stem to stern in soft corals and anemones, wolf eels and giant Pacific octopus den in the rocks, and Steller sea lions buzz divers at Race Rocks. Big artificial reefs like HMCS Saskatchewan add wreck steel. Slack-window boat diving in a drysuit, at its clearest October through March, with Ogden Point offering an easy shore option.

What to expect in February: giant octopus prowling the reef while wolf eels pair up and sea lions buzz divers. Conditions: winter clarity at its best, twenty to thirty metres of visibility.

Caribbean

2 picks, all at peak season — Bahamas leads.

Bahamas

Peak 24°C 18m viz Medium current

No destination puts big sharks in front of divers more reliably than the Bahamas. Baited dives on shallow sandbanks bring tiger and lemon sharks within arm's length, great hammerheads and oceanic whitetips show in winter, and reef sharks patrol nearly every wall. Between shark runs there are upright freighter wrecks, coral-rimmed blue holes and ripping channel drifts like Current Cut. Day boats and liveaboards cover it, with Nov-May the prime window.

What to expect in February: hammerheads sweeping in low over the sand while tiger sharks and reef sharks circle above. Conditions: cooler water with hammerhead encounters at their most reliable.

Bay Islands — Honduras

Peak 27°C 18m viz Low current

Strung along the Mesoamerican Reef off Honduras, Roatan, Utila and Guanaja deliver some of the Caribbean's most affordable wall diving, with seasonal whale sharks as the headline pelagic. Guanaja adds volcanic canyons, a black-coral seamount and the intact Jado Trader wreck at 24-33 m, while the Cayos Cochinos marine reserve keeps pristine elkhorn shallows. Currents stay mostly mild, and shore entries, day boats and liveaboards all work here.

What to expect in February: whale sharks appearing off Utila while turtles cruise the walls and seahorses hide inshore. Conditions: drying out as the whale shark encounters begin.
