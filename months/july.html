Best Scuba Diving in July

← JuneAll monthsAugust →

July is austral-winter diving at its best: Ningaloo stacks whale sharks, mantas and the first humpbacks, the Great Barrier Reef runs minke encounters and humpback highways, and Heron Island sits in thirty-metre visibility.

In the Atlantic, the Azores' seamounts fill with mobulas and blue sharks, while Fiji's soft corals bloom in the trade-wind currents.

Africa

2 picks, all at peak season — Sharm El Sheikh leads.

Sharm El Sheikh — Egypt

Peak 28°C 30m viz Moderate current

Sharm El Sheikh is the northern Red Sea's classic dive hub, where desert mountains drop into anthias-carpeted walls at Ras Mohammed and tidal drifts sweep the four reefs of the Tiran Strait. June to September brings the famous spawning aggregations — snapper, batfish and barracuda massing off Shark and Yolanda — while day boats reach the SS Thistlegorm. Diveable all twelve months, it pairs easy training bays with genuinely big-animal drift diving.

What to expect in July: spawning aggregations at their densest — snapper walls, batfish, jacks; hammerheads off Jackson Reef. Conditions: hot, glassy summer conditions and the year's best visibility.

Protea Banks — South Africa

Peak 20°C 25m viz Strong current

Protea Banks starts where most reefs end: a deep offshore reef of caves, pinnacles and gullies lying 27-40 m down off South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal coast, dived as fast blue-water drifts with negative entries and open-water ascents. Zambezi (bull) sharks patrol year-round, wintering ragged-tooth sharks pack the caves, and summer adds tiger sharks and schooling hammerheads, with the sardine run streaming past in June and July. This is uncompromising, experienced-divers-only territory.

What to expect in July: ragged-tooth mating aggregation filling the caves while bull sharks patrol and the sardine run draws predators. Conditions: cold clear winter water with excellent visibility and brisk deep-reef current.

Oceania

3 picks, all at peak season — Ningaloo Reef leads.

Ningaloo Reef — Australia

Peak 23°C 32m viz Low current

Australia's largest fringing reef hugs the Western Australian coast, its coral gardens starting a short swim off the beach. The diving runs mellow and animal-rich: manta rays circling the cleaning station at Gulliver's, reef sharks queuing over cabbage coral at Asho's Gap, green turtles napping in limestone swim-throughs. The Exmouth Navy Pier alone justifies the trip, its pylons crowded with wobbegongs and frogfish, and whale sharks gather along the reef from March to July.

What to expect in July: late whale sharks lingering offshore while humpback whales stream past the reef. Conditions: cool and clear with excellent visibility.

Fiji

Peak 25°C 30m viz Strong current

Tidal currents ripping through the Somosomo Strait feed the densest soft-coral gardens in the Pacific, and when the white corals on the Great White Wall open to feed, the whole reef seems to cascade. Beqa Lagoon counters with a famous baited shark dive that brings in big bull sharks, while Namena's Grand Central Station funnels barracuda and grey reefs past like commuters. Resort day boats and liveaboards cover it all; July through October is best.

What to expect in July: manta rays feeding in the strong channel flow while bull sharks pack the arenas. Conditions: dry and settled with visibility beyond thirty metres.

French Polynesia

Peak 27°C 18m viz Strong current

The Tuamotu atolls concentrate the drift diving of your life into their passes: ride the incoming tide through Rangiroa's Tiputa alongside a hundred grey reef sharks and resident dolphins, or hang at Fakarava South while the famous wall of sharks stacks overhead. Bora Bora adds a lagoon manta cleaning station for calmer days. The big passes reward divers comfortable in current, reached by resort day boats or liveaboard, June through October.

What to expect in July: the wall of sharks stacking hundreds deep as the first humpbacks arrive offshore. Conditions: dry season with the clearest water of the year.

South America

2 picks, all at peak season — Malpelo Island leads.

Malpelo Island — Colombia

Peak 27°C 25m viz Strong current

Five hundred kilometers off Colombia's Pacific coast, Malpelo is a bare volcanic rock rising from a seamount patrolled by sharks in staggering numbers. Walls of 200-plus scalloped hammerheads stack up over cleaning stations like La Nevera, silkies swarm by the hundred, and cold-season dives can turn up the rarely seen smalltooth sand tiger. Reached only by liveaboard, its surge-scoured pinnacles and powerful currents demand reef hooks and serious drift experience.

What to expect in July: whale sharks arriving while hundreds of silky sharks swirl and hammerhead schools patrol. Conditions: warm calm seas with occasional rain and strong visibility.

Galapagos Islands — Ecuador

Peak 22°C 10m viz Strong current

Converging ocean currents collide at this volcanic archipelago 1,000 kilometers off Ecuador, stacking cold, nutrient-loaded water against the equator, which is why nothing else dives like the Galapagos. Liveaboards push north to Darwin and Wolf, where whale sharks cruise past walls of scalloped hammerheads from June to November, while central sites add playful sea lions, mola mola, and feeding marine iguanas. Expect negative entries, down-currents, and surge: this is advanced diving, and worth every degree of chill.

What to expect in July: whale sharks passing Darwin and Wolf while big hammerhead schools stream overhead. Conditions: cooling water with rough crossings and greener seas.

Europe

2 picks, all at peak season — Azores leads.

Azores — Portugal

Peak 22°C 28m viz Strong current

Nine volcanic Portuguese islands anchor the mid-Atlantic, and their diving happens in serious blue water. Offshore seamounts like Princess Alice Bank draw squadrons of mobula rays, Condor Bank stages blue and mako shark encounters, and visibility routinely tops 30 meters. Currents run strong out there, so the banks are boat trips for confident divers, though coastal wrecks and Terceira's anchor graveyard offer easier days. July through October is prime season.

What to expect in July: blue sharks circling the baited banks while mobulas school and mola mola drift past. Conditions: calm summer seas with twenty-five to thirty metre visibility.

Full guide: Azores →

Kaş — Turkey

Peak 27°C 35m viz mild to moderate current

Set on Turkey's Lycian coast, Kaş packs 30-plus dive sites within minutes of its harbor, all in the famously clear water of the Turquoise Coast. History does the heavy lifting: an intact DC-3 Dakota rests on sand at around 25 m, a full-size Bronze Age Uluburun replica sits in a shallow bay, and ancient amphorae litter the reefs. Walls, caverns and the current-brushed Canyon add drama, with day boats serving everyone from new divers to seasoned wall hunters.

What to expect in July: loggerhead turtles cruising the reefs while amberjack schools pass and grouper and octopus show everywhere. Conditions: warm calm sea with superb clarity in the busiest season.

Asia

3 picks, all at peak season — Nusa Penida leads.

Nusa Penida — Indonesia

Peak 25°C 25m viz Strong current

Twenty minutes across the channel from Bali, Nusa Penida sits square in the Indonesian Throughflow, and its diving runs on that moving water: high-speed drifts along the north coast, reef mantas circling the south-coast cleaning stations all year, and - when cold upwellings arrive from July to October - oceanic sunfish rising to be cleaned at Crystal Bay. Currents shift fast and can pull downward, so dive it with sharp buoyancy and a guide who reads the water.

What to expect in July: mola-mola rising at Crystal Bay while mantas hold steady at Manta Point. Conditions: dry season with cold upwellings, punchy currents and excellent visibility.

Maldives

Peak 29°C 10m viz Medium current

Scattered across 26 atolls in the Indian Ocean, the Maldives runs on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through channel mouths and over isolated thilas, stacking grey reef sharks, eagle rays and schooling snapper wherever it pushes hardest. December to April brings the clearest water; May to November shifts mantas and whale sharks to the plankton-rich eastern atolls. Liveaboards cover the most ground, resorts add house-reef ease, and solid drift skills pay off everywhere.

What to expect in July: whale sharks feeding off South Ari while the Hanifaru manta aggregation begins to build. Conditions: plankton-rich water with lower visibility and stronger currents.

Okinawa Islands — Japan

Peak 29°C 30m viz Strong current

Japan's subtropical island chain strings across the East China Sea, and its diving swings from easy shore entries to serious drift. Winter sends schooling scalloped hammerheads past Yonaguni's fast currents, near the stepped stone monument that still fuels debate over its origins, while summer mantas hover over Ishigaki's cleaning stations. Around the main island, the glowing Blue Cave suits new divers and the deep USS Emmons wreck demands technical training. Day boats reach nearly all of it.

What to expect in July: manta rays circling the cleaning stations while clear reefs stretch into the blue. Conditions: warm with thirty metre visibility and typhoon risk.

Central America

Cocos Island carries the region this month.

Cocos Island — Costa Rica

Peak 25°C 10m viz Strong current

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

What to expect in July: hammerhead schools massing over the pinnacles while whale sharks and mantas patrol the blue. Conditions: wet season with rougher crossings and green water.

North America

2 picks, all at peak season — Cancún & Playa del Carmen leads.

Cancún & Playa del Carmen — Mexico

Peak 29°C 20m viz Moderate current

The Cancún–Playa del Carmen corridor is high-volume Caribbean diving with two genuinely wild seasons stitched in. Winter brings pregnant bull sharks to a 24-metre sand flat off Playa and sailfish to the blue; summer stacks hundreds of whale sharks offshore. Between them you drift warm, easy reefs on the Yucatán Current, land on hurricane-split wrecks, and tour the sculpture galleries of MUSA. Short boat rides and year-round warm water suit everyone from new divers to shark-focused veterans.

What to expect in July: hundreds of whale sharks aggregating offshore while turtles and rays cruise the reefs inshore. Conditions: hot and humid with bathtub-warm water and calm mornings.

Full guide: Cancún & Playa del Carmen →

Oahu — USA

Peak 27°C 24m viz Moderate current

Oahu packs real variety into one very reachable island: intact wrecks like the YO-257 and Sea Tiger minutes off Waikiki, lava-tube caverns at Sharks Cove and Makaha, and shark banks offshore where Galapagos and tiger sharks patrol. Green turtles are near-guaranteed, spinner dolphins escort west-side boats, and winter dives come with humpback song. South and west shores dive year-round; the North Shore is a summer-only prize when the surf sleeps.

What to expect in July: turtles, octopus and eels filling the Sharks Cove lava tubes while tiger sharks patrol offshore banks. Conditions: warmest and calmest stretch with long flat spells and twenty-five-metre-plus visibility.
