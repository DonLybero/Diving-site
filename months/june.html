Best Scuba Diving in June

Where the water is at its best in June, region by region.

← MayAll monthsJuly →

June belongs to the Coral Sea and the passes of French Polynesia.

The Great Barrier Reef opens dwarf-minke-whale season on the Ribbon Reefs, Fakarava's groupers gather for the year's great spawning, Cocos starts building toward hammerhead high season, and the northern Red Sea settles into warm, glassy wreck-diving weather.

Oceania

3 picks, all at peak season — Great Barrier Reef leads.

Great Barrier Reef — Australia

Peak 24°C 24m viz Medium current

Stretching more than 2,300 kilometers along Queensland's coast, the Great Barrier Reef is the largest reef system on Earth, and its best diving lives offshore. Liveaboards run the Ribbon Reefs for Cod Hole's tame potato cod and June-July dwarf minke whale encounters, then push into the Coral Sea for shark action at Osprey's North Horn. Day boats from Cairns cover easy coral gardens, while the current-swept SS Yongala wreck rewards advanced divers.

What to expect in June: dwarf minke whales approaching swimmers on the Ribbon Reefs while humpbacks and mantas pass through. Conditions: dry, calm and clear with choppier outer crossings.

French Polynesia

Peak 27°C 35m viz Strong current

The Tuamotu atolls concentrate the drift diving of your life into their passes: ride the incoming tide through Rangiroa's Tiputa alongside a hundred grey reef sharks and resident dolphins, or hang at Fakarava South while the famous wall of sharks stacks overhead. Bora Bora adds a lagoon manta cleaning station for calmer days. The big passes reward divers comfortable in current, reached by resort day boats or liveaboard, June through October.

What to expect in June: grouper spawn drawing hunters into the pass while the shark wall builds day by day. Conditions: dry season with excellent thirty to forty metre visibility.

Guadalcanal & Western Province — Solomon Islands

Peak 28°C 30m viz Low current

The Solomon Islands wear their WWII history underwater: beached Japanese transports at Bonegi drop from shore-entry shallows to 55 meters, an intact B-17 bomber rests off Honiara, and Gizo's Toa Maru ranks among the Pacific's finest recreational wrecks. Out in the Western Province, reefs like Njari's Grand Central Station have logged 270-plus fish species on a single dive. Mild currents, mandarinfish macro, and empty sites make this a rewarding resort or liveaboard trip, April through November.

What to expect in June: reef sharks riding the current lines while macro life fills the sheltered shallows. Conditions: dry season with visibility beyond thirty metres.

Asia

2 picks, all at peak season — Komodo National Park leads.

Komodo National Park — Indonesia

Peak 28°C 24m viz Strong current

Tides squeeze between the Pacific and Indian Oceans through Komodo's narrow straits, and the currents they generate power some of the most electric diving in Indonesia. Hook into the reef at Castle Rock or Crystal Rock as sharks and trevally hunt the flow, fire through the Shotgun's channel, then drift Makassar Reef's manta cleaning stations. The warm north and cold, nutrient-rich south fish differently by season, so liveaboards cover it best. Bring drift experience.

What to expect in June: mantas feeding across the north and central sites while whale sharks and reef sharks cruise through. Conditions: dry and clear as the southern upwelling builds.

Sipadan Island — Malaysia

Peak 30°C 35m viz Strong current

Sipadan rises 600 m straight from the Celebes Sea floor, an oceanic pinnacle off Malaysian Borneo where daily permits cap diver numbers. Current is the switch that turns the action on: at Barracuda Point a resident school of chevron barracuda coils into its famous tornado, whitetips rest along the ledges, and green and hawksbill turtles are everywhere. Day boats run from nearby islands, conditions favor experienced divers, and the island closes every November.

What to expect in June: turtles abundant on every dive while the barracuda tornado spins and reef sharks cruise the wall. Conditions: settled seas with visibility from twenty to fifty metres.

South America

2 picks, all at peak season — Malpelo Island leads.

Malpelo Island — Colombia

Peak 26°C 25m viz Strong current

Five hundred kilometers off Colombia's Pacific coast, Malpelo is a bare volcanic rock rising from a seamount patrolled by sharks in staggering numbers. Walls of 200-plus scalloped hammerheads stack up over cleaning stations like La Nevera, silkies swarm by the hundred, and cold-season dives can turn up the rarely seen smalltooth sand tiger. Reached only by liveaboard, its surge-scoured pinnacles and powerful currents demand reef hooks and serious drift experience.

What to expect in June: hammerheads schooling while big silky shark schools gather and the first whale sharks converge. Conditions: wet season onset with warm water, deep thermoclines and good visibility.

Full guide: Malpelo Island →

Galapagos Islands — Ecuador

Peak 23°C 18m viz Strong current

Converging ocean currents collide at this volcanic archipelago 1,000 kilometers off Ecuador, stacking cold, nutrient-loaded water against the equator, which is why nothing else dives like the Galapagos. Liveaboards push north to Darwin and Wolf, where whale sharks cruise past walls of scalloped hammerheads from June to November, while central sites add playful sea lions, mola mola, and feeding marine iguanas. Expect negative entries, down-currents, and surge: this is advanced diving, and worth every degree of chill.

What to expect in June: whale sharks arriving at Darwin while hammerheads build and penguins hunt the shallows. Conditions: cool season beginning with choppier seas and dropping visibility.

Central America

Cocos Island carries the region this month.

Cocos Island — Costa Rica

Peak 26°C 18m viz Strong current

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

What to expect in June: hammerheads building on the seamount while whale sharks and mantas move in. Conditions: wet season beginning with strengthening currents and dropping visibility.

Africa

2 picks, all at peak season — Protea Banks leads.

Protea Banks — South Africa

Peak 21°C 25m viz Strong current

Protea Banks starts where most reefs end: a deep offshore reef of caves, pinnacles and gullies lying 27-40 m down off South Africa's KwaZulu-Natal coast, dived as fast blue-water drifts with negative entries and open-water ascents. Zambezi (bull) sharks patrol year-round, wintering ragged-tooth sharks pack the caves, and summer adds tiger sharks and schooling hammerheads, with the sardine run streaming past in June and July. This is uncompromising, experienced-divers-only territory.

What to expect in June: ragged-tooth sharks packing the Northern caves while bull sharks patrol and the sardine run passes offshore. Conditions: clear dry winter, the year's best visibility, cold thermoclines, strong drift.

Hurghada & El Gouna — Egypt

Peak 27°C 25m viz Moderate current

Hurghada is the Red Sea's workhorse dive hub, and it earns the traffic: warm water all year, 20-30m visibility, and day boats fanning out to the Giftun reefs, dolphin lagoons and the four-wreck graveyard at Abu Nuhas. Sheltered coral gardens make it a natural place to learn, while the Carnatic and Giannis D give wreck divers real substance. Winter brings wind and chop, so spring and autumn are the sweet spots.

What to expect in June: reef sharks at the offshore pinnacles, tuna and trevally hunting, turtles nesting season. Conditions: warm settled summer sea, gentle breeze; long days ideal for two-tank wreck trips.

North America

Maui & Kona carries the region this month.

Maui & Kona — USA

Peak 26°C 32m viz Low current

Hawaii's twin dive hubs pack volcanic drama into calm leeward water. Off Kona, dive lights draw plankton, and feeding manta rays, to Garden Eel Cove nearly every night of the year, while Maui day boats run to Molokini, a sunken crescent crater with visibility pushing 45 m and a sheer back wall dropping past 100 m. Lava caverns off Lanai and turtle-thick shore entries round out a destination that serves new divers and wall-drift veterans alike.

What to expect in June: mantas gliding the reef edges while pelagics patrol the deeper blue. Conditions: calm seas with excellent visibility.

Caribbean

Bonaire carries the region this month.

Bonaire — Caribbean (Netherlands)

Peak 28°C 32m viz Low current

Bonaire perfected drive-and-dive freedom: rent a truck, load tanks, and roll into any of the marked sites along the leeward west coast whenever you like. The fringing reef starts meters from shore and stays remarkably healthy, from the fish-thick house reefs off Kralendijk to the sponge-wrapped pilings of Salt Pier and the shore-accessible Hilma Hooker wreck at 30 m. Calm, easy conditions make it ideal for new divers and macro hunters chasing frogfish and seahorses.

What to expect in June: turtles grazing the shallows while the first coral spawning arrives late in the month. Conditions: calm, warm and dry with excellent visibility.

Europe

3 picks, all at peak season — Mallorca leads.

Mallorca — Spain

Peak 22°C 25m viz Low current

Mallorca is the Mediterranean's limestone playground: decades of erosion have drilled its coastline into caverns, arches and swim-throughs, most of them shallow, sunlit and forgiving. The El Toro and Malgrats marine reserves supply the fish — wall-to-wall barracuda and dusky grouper so protected they ignore you. With tide-free seas, easy day-boat logistics and 30-metre summer visibility, it suits new divers building skills and cavern fans alike; May to October is the season that matters.

What to expect in June: barracuda tornadoes swirling and eagle rays passing while grouper patrol the busy cave life. Conditions: warm flat summer seas beginning with long daylight and excellent visibility.

Full guide: Mallorca →

Ustica — Italy

Peak 21°C 35m viz mild to moderate current

A volcanic speck in the Tyrrhenian Sea north of Sicily, Ustica became Italy's first marine protected area in 1986, and four decades of protection show underwater: crystal water, walls thick with gorgonians and sponges, and dusky groupers bold enough to follow you around. Lava rock carves the caves, arches and tunnels of the Scoglio del Medico pinnacle, while two archaeological trails let you hover over ancient anchors and amphorae. Shore and day-boat diving runs from sheltered beginner bays to current-brushed offshore seamounts.

What to expect in June: bold dusky groupers greeting divers while barracuda schools, amberjack and octopus spread across the sites. Conditions: warm calm water with excellent visibility and full operator schedules.

Full guide: Ustica →

Silfra Fissure — Iceland

Peak 4°C 100m viz Low current

Glacial meltwater, filtered through lava for decades, fills this crack between the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates in Iceland's Thingvellir National Park, delivering visibility beyond 100 meters. You drift gently through the Big Crack, over the 23-meter Cathedral canyon, and out across the algae-green lagoon. There is almost no life to watch; the geology is the show. A drysuit shore dive in 2-4 C water, easy enough for any certified diver.

What to expect in June: crystal visibility with no marine life, a pure geology dive between the continental plates. Conditions: over one hundred metres of visibility, midnight sun and easy access.
