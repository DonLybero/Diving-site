# Indonesia dive trip: the complete plan — Bali to Raja Ampat

Indonesia's five best dive regions line up west to east along one flight corridor, which means you can dive a WWII wreck off a Bali beach, hang at a manta cleaning station off Nusa Penida, drift Komodo's pinnacles and finish on the reefs of the Dampier Strait in a single trip. This article gives you the route, the nights per stop, the months that work for all five legs at once, and the transfers and permits in between — everything you need before you start booking.

## The route at a glance

**Bali 5 nights → Nusa Penida 3 → Gili Islands & Lombok 3 → Komodo 4 → Raja Ampat 6. That's 21 nights, or about 3.5 weeks once you add travel days.**

- **Bali, 5 nights.** You land here anyway (Denpasar has nonstop long-haul links), and five nights covers jet-lag recovery, the Tulamben–Amed shore-diving coast and a topside day or two.
- **Nusa Penida, 3 nights.** The island is small and the diving is concentrated; three nights buys you several morning attempts at mola-mola and mantas without idle days.
- **Gili Islands & Lombok, 3 nights.** Easy turtle-heavy reef diving and a car-free mid-trip reset between two current-heavy legs.
- **Komodo, 4 nights.** Park boat days start at 05:30 and the strong tidal currents mean you want multiple days to catch the right windows — plus one day for the dragons.
- **Raja Ampat, 6 nights.** Getting there costs roughly a day in each direction (a ~4 h flight to Sorong, then the ferry to Waisai), and the permits run IDR 1,000,000. Six nights amortises the effort and matches the homestay rhythm of the place.

**The 12–14 day version:** Bali 4 nights → Nusa Penida 3 → Komodo 4, flying home from Labuan Bajo via Denpasar or Jakarta. Cut the Gilis first — their relaxed reef diving overlaps most with Bali's — and leave Raja Ampat for a dedicated trip rather than squeezing it: with a travel day each way, anything under five nights there is mostly transit.

## When to go

The route has one real tension. Komodo's dry season runs April–October (rated Peak throughout, with May singled out as the best month), while Raja Ampat's season is the mirror image: October–April, peaking December–February, with June–August rated Low — rough seas and most liveaboards gone.

The overlap is October, and it happens to be excellent everywhere:

- **Bali:** Peak, its clearest water of the year at 30 m visibility.
- **Nusa Penida:** Good — mola-mola sightings tapering as the upwelling fades, but mantas steady.
- **Gili Islands:** Good, with 24 m visibility and warming water.
- **Komodo:** still Peak — calm seas, the last mola drifting deep and mantas sweeping Karang Makassar.
- **Raja Ampat:** Good and climbing fast, with calm seas returning, 25–40 m visibility and mantas building at the cleaning stations.

**April is the runner-up**, running the route in reverse logic: Raja Ampat's late season (last mantas, calm seas), Komodo's dry season just starting with manageable currents, and Bali at Peak. If mola-mola off Penida are your priority, start the trip in late September so you hit Penida's coldest upwellings (Peak July–September) and reach Raja Ampat as its season opens in October. Avoid June–August for the full route: fine for Bali, Penida and Komodo, but Raja Ampat is stormy then.

## Bali

Bali earns its place with the easiest great diving of the trip. The USAT Liberty at Tulamben is a 120 m WWII cargo ship 30 m off the beach in 5–30 m of water, with bumphead parrotfish grazing it at dawn. Down the coast, Seraya Secrets is a black-sand slope where photographers hunt harlequin shrimp, boxer crabs and ghost pipefish, and Blue Lagoon at Padang Bai turns up rhinopias and blue-ringed octopus in a calm white-sand bay. Menjangan Island, off the northwest tip, adds sheer gorgonian-draped walls in the island's clearest water. The northeast coast is sheltered with gentle currents — a forgiving warm-up (28°C in October) before the Throughflow legs ahead.

Base yourself in Amed or Tulamben for the shore-entry dive coast, then finish in Sanur, a calm beachfront grid with the Nusa Penida fast-boat harbour at its doorstep and a 30–45 min airport run. Spend the surface days well: the Mount Batur sunrise trek puts you on a 1,717 m crater rim by dawn and back by mid-morning; the kecak dance at Uluwatu sets a 60-man chanting choir on a clifftop 70 m above the surf at sunset; and the Sidemen valley's rice-field lanes sit conveniently on the drive to or from Amed. A cooking class — market visit, base gede spice paste, satay lilit — is the best rainy-morning insurance on the island.

**Getting in and out:** fly into Denpasar (DPS) nonstop from most major Asian and Australian hubs plus Doha and Istanbul. Onward is a 30–45 min transfer to Sanur harbour.

## Nusa Penida

Penida is the advanced-rated leg: the Indonesian Throughflow funnels between the islands, driving fast drifts and sudden down-currents that demand solid buoyancy and guide-led planning. The payoff is two of Indonesia's marquee animals. Crystal Bay is the classic mola-mola site, where cold July–October upwellings (water drops to 24–25°C — pack accordingly) bring sunfish to cleaning stations on the deep slope. Manta Point hosts reef mantas year-round on the exposed south coast, with Manta Bay as the calmer alternative. Toyapakeh adds a vivid coral wall in the channel, and Gamat Bay is the sheltered macro option when conditions are up.

Stay on the Ped–Sampalan north-coast strip, where the island's guesthouses, warungs and dive shops line the calm shore road, or in Toyapakeh beside the jetty. Topside, this is a raw limestone plateau with 200 m sea cliffs: the Kelingking viewpoint, the collapsed sea-cave arch at Broken Beach, and the Peguyangan stairs — a steel staircase bolted down a 200 m cliff to a spring-fed temple — fill the afternoons. Evenings are quiet; it's an early-nights island, which suits dawn boat calls.

**Getting there:** fast boats run from Sanur about 21 times daily (30–45 min, IDR 150,000–250,000 each way) to Toyapakeh or Sampalan.

## Gili Islands & Lombok

After two current-conscious stops, the Gilis are the exhale: three flat, car-free coral cays rated beginner-friendly, with mild currents on most sites and turtles at practically every one. Turtle Heaven, a pinnacle north of Gili Meno, is stacked with green and hawksbill turtles; Meno Wall is a gentle wall and the area's top night dive; Shark Point brings reef sharks and rays onto a sloping reef; and Han's Reef off Gili Air is the critter site — frogfish, seahorses, ribbon eels. May–September is peak here, with visibility hitting 25–32 m.

Gili Trawangan has the widest spread of restaurants and dive shops; Gili Air is the middle-ground pace. Between dives, cycle Trawangan's flat 7 km coastal loop, eat grilled reef fish at the harbour night market (go before 20:00 — the best fish sells out), and hop the public boats to Meno's salt lake. With an extra day, cross to Lombok for the Tiu Kelep waterfall walk on Rinjani's northern foothills or a Sasak village visit near Kuta Lombok.

**Getting there:** direct fast boats run from Padang Bai or Serangan on Bali to Gili Trawangan (1.5–2.5 h, ~USD 22–35). Heading onward to Komodo, boat back to Bali and fly from Denpasar.

## Komodo National Park

Komodo is the big-animal, big-current centrepiece. The park's tidal currents are strongest at full and new moon, and its best sites are built on them: Batu Bolong, a current-swept pinnacle dense with fish and pristine coral; Castle Rock and Crystal Rock, submerged seamounts where sharks and trevally hunt in the flow; and Manta Point (Karang Makassar), a long shallow drift past manta cleaning stations at 6–15 m. Calmer days go to Siaba Besar — a sheltered bay dubbed Turtle City — or the easy orange soft-coral drift at Tatawa Besar. In October you get the dry season's closing act: calm seas, 24 m visibility and mantas still working the stations.

Base in Labuan Bajo on the harbourfront, steps from the jetties for 05:30 departures, with the sunset restaurant row on the same street. Give one full boat day to the topside icons: the 30–40 min stair climb to Padar Island's three-bay ridge view, a ranger-guided dragon walk on Komodo or Rinca — the only place on earth to see wild Komodo dragons — and the pink sand of Pantai Merah. If a surface interval needs filling, the Rangko Cave's turquoise tidal pool is a half-day from town.

**Getting there:** fly Denpasar to Labuan Bajo (LBJ), about 1 h nonstop with 4–6 daily flights. The airport is 10–15 min from the harbour.

## Raja Ampat

The final leg is the richest reef system on the route and the reason to keep six nights in hand. Cape Kri holds a world-record fish count for a single dive — snapper, trevally, sharks in one water column. Blue Magic is a Dampier Strait seamount visited by oceanic mantas and wobbegongs; Manta Sandy is a shallow cleaning station with a marked viewing line, friendly to any certification level; Melissa's Garden is a pristine hard-coral plateau with pygmy seahorses in its fans; and The Passage runs like a river between Waigeo and Gam through archways and soft coral. Currents drive the show here — drift diving is central, and the strait's flow is what feeds those fish numbers.

Stay on Kri or Mansuar, the homestay-and-resort belt minutes by boat from the busiest reefs, or on quieter Gam for Kabui Bay's karst. Life runs on boat schedules, rainwater tanks and generator hours — bring cash, patience and dry bags. Between dives: the 320-step boardwalk to the Piaynemo viewpoint over the cone-karst lagoon, a 04:30 dawn walk to watch red birds-of-paradise display at Saporkren, and a paddle through Kabui Bay's mangrove channels.

**Getting there:** fly to Sorong (SOQ) — about 4 h nonstop from Jakarta, typically as a red-eye, or via Makassar — then take the ~2 h fast ferry from Sorong harbour to Waisai on Waigeo (once or twice daily). Homestays collect guests by boat from the Waisai jetty.

## Events worth timing — or planning around

- **Nyepi (March; 19 March in 2026).** Bali's Day of Silence stops the whole province for 24 h — Denpasar airport closes, roads empty, and guests must stay inside their hotels. Penida's boats stop too. The ogoh-ogoh demon parades the evening before are worth seeing if you're already there; just don't schedule a flight or crossing for the day itself.
- **Galungan & Kuningan (17 and 27 June in 2026).** Bamboo penjor poles arch over every Balinese street and temples fill with processions; Penida's Pura Dalem Ped fills with pilgrims and fast boats sell out around the holy days.
- **Raja Ampat Festival (mid-to-late October, Waisai).** Days of Papuan dance, boat races and seafood stalls — but ferries and town rooms run full, so book the Sorong–Waisai crossing early if your October trip lands on it.
- **Lebaran (Idul Fitri; March in 2026).** A week-long national surge when domestic flights, fast boats and hotels fill — Labuan Bajo seats and Lombok crossings are hit hardest.

## The practical bits

- **No-fly days.** This route has three flight legs after diving (to Labuan Bajo, to Sorong, and home). Build a full no-dive day before each one — the itinerary above absorbs them as topside days: a Bali temple day, the Padar-and-dragons boat day, and a Piaynemo or bird-of-paradise day at the end.
- **Permits.** Raja Ampat requires a marine-park permit (IDR 700,000) plus a visitor entry ticket (IDR 300,000), payable in Waisai or online. Komodo diving happens inside a national park, so park fees apply on every boat day — confirm current rates when booking.
- **Cash and connectivity.** Waisai has the ATMs and permit offices for Raja Ampat; the homestay islands run on cash and generator hours, so withdraw everything you need before the ferry. Penida's infrastructure is basic too — carry more cash than you think.
- **The flight thread.** DPS is the hub for the first three stops; DPS–LBJ is 1 h with 4–6 daily flights; LBJ connects to Sorong via Jakarta or Makassar; SOQ–Jakarta gets you home. Book the LBJ and SOQ legs first — they have the least capacity.
