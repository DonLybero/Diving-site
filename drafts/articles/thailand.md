# Thailand dive trip: two coasts, and the one window when both peak

Thailand's diving sits on two seas that take turns. The Andaman coast — Phuket's day-boat pinnacles and the Similan Islands' granite boulder fields — runs on the dry northeast monsoon, roughly November to April, and the Similan National Park closes outright from mid-May to mid-October. The Gulf coast — Koh Tao — runs on almost the opposite clock: its best months are March to May and September, and its worst are November and December, exactly when the Andaman side is flat and clear. The two calendars overlap properly in just one window. **March and April are the only months when all three destinations rate Peak at the same time** — which makes the full three-stop route a March-or-April trip, and everything else a single-coast trip.

## When each destination is at its best

| Destination | Peak | Good | The rest of the year |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phuket | Nov-Apr | — | May-Oct is the monsoon off-season; Aug-Sep crossings are often cancelled |
| Similan Islands | Feb-Apr | Nov-Jan | Shoulder weeks either side of the closure |
| **Similan park closure** | — | — | **Closed to all boats roughly 16 May to 14 October; exact dates shift by year** |
| Koh Tao | Mar-May, Sep | Feb, Jun-Aug, Oct | Nov-Dec: monsoon rain and the year's worst visibility (8-10 m) |

Read the table strictly and the combinations write themselves. All three at Peak: March-April. All three at least Good: February-April. Phuket plus the Similans both at Peak: February-April. Nothing pairs the two coasts outside that band without one side compromised.

## The full route — March or April (13-14 days)

Fly into Phuket, work north up the Andaman coast, then cross the peninsula to the Gulf. The crossing day doubles as the no-fly day after the liveaboard, so no dive time is wasted on decompression waiting.

- **Days 1-4 — Phuket.** Arrive, then three day-boat outings from Chalong Pier covering the mid-channel pinnacles and wreck, the Racha Islands, and Phi Phi, with topside half-days in between.
- **Day 4 evening to day 8 — Similan liveaboard.** A pre-booked minibus takes you 75-90 minutes north to Thap Lamu Pier near Khao Lak; board in the evening, cross overnight, dive four days through the chain to Richelieu Rock, and return mid-afternoon on day 8.
- **Day 9 — the crossing, and the no-fly day.** Overland and ferry via Surat Thani or Chumphon, or a short flight to Koh Samui once you clear the surface-interval maths, then the ferry into Mae Haad. Either way you arrive in the evening with your no-fly obligation spent on a travel day.
- **Days 10-14 — Koh Tao.** Four diving days on the Gulf pinnacles and wreck, then a final non-diving day before the ferry and flight home.

Book the liveaboard first — March and April boats fill months out — and build the rest around its fixed dates.

## Off the peak window

Outside March-April, be honest with yourself about what each half-trip is.

- **November to January — Andaman only.** Phuket rates Peak the whole stretch: calm crossings, 20-25 m visibility, the first mantas back at Racha Noi. The Similans are open but rate Good, not Peak — January visibility runs around 18 m against the 30 m-plus of February and March, and the big manta and whale shark traffic hasn't built yet. Leave Koh Tao out entirely: November-December is its monsoon, with 8-10 m visibility and weather-dependent boats.
- **May to September — Gulf only.** The Similan park is shut and Phuket slides from Shoulder to Low, with August-September crossings often cancelled. Koh Tao carries the whole trip: May is still Peak, June-August rate Good — 18-20 m visibility, occasional southwest chop, east-coast sites sheltered — and September is a second Peak of calm seas and returning whale sharks.
- **February** is the near-miss month: everything at least Good, the Andaman pair at Peak, but Koh Tao's whale sharks aren't reliable until March. Fine if your dates force it; March is better.

## The ultimate 4 days in Phuket

Four days is the number because Phuket's sites sort into three distinct full-day boat runs, and operators schedule them exactly that way — one circuit per day, 2-3 dives each, out of Chalong Pier. Three boat days covers each circuit once; the fourth absorbs arrival and topside.

The mid-channel day is the character day: Shark Point's three soft-coral limestone pinnacles (10-24 m) with leopard sharks flat on the sand, Anemone Reef next door (5-27 m), and the King Cruiser — an 85 m car ferry that struck that same reef in 1997 and now sits at 16-31 m, hazed in schooling snapper with bamboo sharks in the hull. Currents turn brisk here on spring tides. The Racha day heads south: Racha Noi's south tip (18-40 m) is granite boulders and a cleaning station with Phuket's most reliable oceanic manta chances through the dry season, while Racha Yai's sheltered bays handle easy multi-dive days. The third run reaches Phi Phi's limestone walls. Water holds 28-30°C in a 3 mm suit; dry-season visibility runs 20-25 m.

Topside, walk Old Town's Sino-Portuguese shophouses on Thalang and Dibuk Roads (Sunday evenings the street closes for a walking market), climb Nakkerd Hill to the 45 m Big Buddha, sea-kayak the hongs of Phang Nga Bay — collapsed sea caves entered through tunnel passages at low tide — and eat 20-60 THB street plates at the Chillva night market. Wat Chalong sits 10 minutes from the pier, so it pairs with a boat day.

Stay in Chalong or Rawai, closest to the pier, or Kata/Karon for swimmable beaches 20-30 minutes from morning pickups. Fly direct into HKT or connect through Bangkok on the 1 h 25 min hop; the airport sits at the island's north tip, 45-60 minutes from the beaches (800-1,000 THB by taxi to Patong).

## The ultimate 4 days in the Similan Islands

Four days, four nights is the standard liveaboard itinerary for a reason: it is the shortest trip that covers the whole northern run — the Similan chain, Koh Bon, Koh Tachai and Richelieu Rock — at a full four dives a day, about 14 dives in the pattern 4+4+4+2. Shorter 2-night boats exist, but they cut either the northern sites or the dive count; 14 dives across ten named sites is the honest minimum for the area, and our site list bears it out.

The boat is the hotel. In the chain itself, Elephant Head Rock (10-40 m) is the signature terrain — a granite boulder maze of swim-throughs — with Deep Six's arches at Island 7's tip, Christmas Point's sea fans and leopard sharks, and gentler bommies on white sand at Anita's Reef for the check-out dive. North of the chain, Koh Bon's west ridge (10-35 m) is the area's top manta cleaning station, Koh Tachai's current-swept plateau stacks barracuda and trevally, and the trip ends at Richelieu Rock — a horseshoe pinnacle of purple soft coral from 5-35 m with Thailand's best whale shark odds. February-April brings flat seas and visibility past 30 m. Several marquee sites grade advanced, with current and depth.

Boarding logistics: boats leave from Thap Lamu Pier, 15 minutes south of Khao Lak; most liveaboards arrange minibus pickup from Phuket or Khao Lak hotels, board in the evening and cross overnight. If you bracket the boat with a Khao Lak night, add a day at Khao Sok — longtail boats among the limestone towers of Cheow Lan Lake, 1.5 hours inland — or an hour's kayak float through the Little Amazon mangrove channel at Takua Pa (around 500 THB). Stay in Nang Thong for the pier or Bang Niang for the beach and its four-nights-a-week market.

## The ultimate 5 days on Koh Tao

Diver-focused guides put a diving stay at four days to a week; five is the working number because the sites demand four distinct boat days and the fifth is your no-fly day before the flight home. Day one: Chumphon Pinnacle (14-36 m), an anemone-carpeted granite tower swarmed by trevally and chevron barracuda and the island's most reliable whale shark spot — strongest March-May, with a second run in September. Day two: the long run to Sail Rock and its vertical chimney swim-through from 5-40 m. Day three: the HTMS Sattakut, a 48 m former navy vessel at 18-30 m with groupers in her gun turrets, paired with White Rock's easy reef. Day four: Chumphon again — whale shark odds compound with repeat visits — plus Southwest Pinnacle's grouper-patrolled peaks. Water runs 27-30°C; a 3 mm shorty covers it.

The no-fly day is no hardship: longtail out to Koh Nang Yuan's three-islet viewpoint (about 350 THB return), the steep 15-20 minute scramble to John-Suwan above Chalok Baan Kao, or a kayak along the granite coves of Tanote Bay before the afternoon breeze.

Getting in: Koh Tao has no airport. Fly to Koh Samui (USM) and take the 1.5-2 h catamaran into Mae Haad Pier, or fly to Chumphon (CJM) for the shortest mainland crossing, about 1 h 45 min (600-700 THB); combined overnight bus-and-ferry tickets from Bangkok run 10-11 hours. Stay on Sairee Beach for the 1.7 km restaurant strip, Mae Haad for early ferries, or Chalok Baan Kao for quiet.

## Events and timing

**Songkran (13-15 April) lands inside the peak window.** That is the price of an April route: transport and rooms book out weeks ahead, some shops close, and topside turns into a nationwide water fight for three days. Dive boats largely keep running — plan Songkran as your liveaboard days or a Koh Tao dive stretch and the festival barely touches you; just keep phones in dry bags ashore. The **Phuket Vegetarian Festival** (nine days, late September to mid-October; 10-18 October in 2026) falls in the Andaman off-season, filling Phuket Town with firewalking processions and firecrackers. **Loy Krathong** (full moon, usually November) is a calm evening of candle-lit floats — no disruption, and it falls in the Andaman-only window. The **Full Moon Party** on Koh Phangan fills Gulf ferries and rooms for 2-3 days around every full moon; check the date before booking Koh Tao crossings.

## The practical bits

- **Ferries.** Book Koh Tao crossings ahead December-March and around Songkran and full moons; agree island-taxi fares before boarding.
- **Liveaboard timing.** The exact park opening dates shift slightly by year — confirm them before locking flights, and expect evening boarding at Thap Lamu with an overnight crossing.
- **Scooters.** Skip them on both coasts. Rental accidents involving visitors are common on Phuket, and Koh Tao's steep, partly unpaved roads plus frequent rental-damage disputes make shared pickup taxis and longtails the better call.
- **The no-fly rule.** The route already builds both buffers in — the coast-to-coast crossing after the liveaboard, and Koh Tao's final viewpoint day before the flight out.
