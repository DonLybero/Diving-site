# Maldives dive trip: resort, local island or liveaboard — the complete plan

The Maldives is 26 atolls of channel mouths and mid-atoll pinnacles where the monsoon decides which side of the country the fish are on. Every trip starts the same way — a long-haul flight into Velana International (MLE), on its own island next to Malé — and then splits into three very different holidays depending on one decision: sleep on a resort island, in a local-island guesthouse, or on a boat. That choice sets your budget, your dive sites, what you drink with dinner and how much of your money goes to transfers. Here is how to make it.

## How many days

Book 7 to 10 nights. Every atoll leg beyond the airport is a second booking — resort speedboat, seaplane or domestic flight plus boat — and seaplanes fly only from about 06:00 to 16:00, so a late-evening arrival means a night near the airport before you can move. Add the no-fly day before departure and a 5-night trip shrinks to three diving days — thin for the transfer money spent. Seven nights gives you five full diving days; ten lets you split the trip, say a liveaboard followed by two nights on an island to decompress. Hulhumalé, the reclaimed island linked to Malé by bridge, is the practical base for that first or last night by the airport.

## Three ways to do it

**Resort island.** One island, one resort: transfers, meals, the house reef and alcohol are all on site, and prices run from about 250 USD to several thousand per night. The dive centre is a short walk from your room and the house reef fills gaps between boat dives. The catch is the transfer bill — a speedboat within roughly 80 km of Malé costs 100–200 USD return per person for a 20–60 minute ride, while mid-distance atolls need a seaplane at 400–820 USD return. You dive whatever is within range of that one island.

**Local island.** Guesthouses on inhabited islands such as Maafushi, Dhigurah and Fulidhoo start around 50 USD a night with dive centres on the doorstep. Getting there is cheap: scheduled shared speedboats to Maafushi run about 25–30 USD one way in 45 minutes, and the public ferry does the same trip for 2–4 USD in about 90 minutes. In exchange, local law applies — no alcohol anywhere on the island, and covered shoulders away from the designated bikini beach. You get real streets, cafés and boatyards instead of resort lawns, and a diving budget that stretches to twice the dives.

**Liveaboard.** The boat collects you at the airport jetty or the Hulhumalé marina on embarkation afternoon and the transfer problem disappears. Nothing else covers the ground a liveaboard does: a standard route strings North and South Malé, Ari and Vaavu together in a week, hitting each channel when the tide is right rather than when the day boat can reach it. You give up land entirely — surface time is the deck — and the per-night cost sits between guesthouse and resort, with three or four dives a day included.

If channels and pelagics are the point of your trip, take the boat. If cost is the constraint, take the guesthouse. If half the party doesn't dive, take the resort.

## The diving

Maldivian diving is built on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through the channel mouths — kandus — and over isolated pinnacles called thilas, and the standard technique is to drop at a channel corner on an incoming tide, hook in and let the blue deliver the animals. Grey reef sharks are the baseline, holding station in nearly every channel; currents run strongest around full and new moon.

The named sites cover every level. Banana Reef in North Malé, the country's first-discovered site, works from 5 m for new divers. Maaya Thila in North Ari (6–30 m) is the classic night dive, with whitetip reef sharks hunting over the pinnacle, and nearby Fish Head is a protected thila patrolled by grey reefs. Manta Point on Lankan Reef is a 10–17 m cleaning station with reliable mantas from August to November. Broken Rock in South Ari is split by a coral-lined canyon swim-through. For experienced divers, Fotteyo Kandu in Vaavu is a 10–40 m channel of caves and overhangs with grey reef sharks and eagle rays, Kandooma Thila is a current-swept South Malé pinnacle thick with sharks and snapper, and Kuredu Express is a fast Lhaviyani drift. Alimatha Jetty in Vaavu adds a night dive mobbed by dozens of nurse sharks and stingrays. Far south, the oceanic island of Fuvahmulah is the outlier — year-round tiger sharks, advanced diving, and a separate trip in its own right.

The monsoon moves the megafauna. In the northeast monsoon (December–April) you dive the eastern sides of the atolls for visibility and pelagics while mantas work the western cleaning stations; hammerheads rise at dawn off Rasdhoo. When the southwest monsoon greens the water (May–November) the pattern flips — dive the west, and the plankton pulls mantas and whale sharks to the east, peaking at Hanifaru Bay in Baa Atoll from July to October, with hundreds of mantas feeding in August and September and whale sharks off South Ari through the season. Water is 28–30°C all year; a 3 mm suit covers it.

## Surface days

Non-diving time happens within walking range of your island, and it fills easily. Boats will drop you on a bare white sandbank in the lagoon for a few hours of swimming and a packed lunch, about 30–70 USD per person. Malé itself packs the 17th-century coral-stone Friday Mosque, the fish market and the produce market into about 2 square kilometres — two to three hours between transfers covers it. Night fishing is handlines from a dhoni at sunset with the catch grilled afterwards; sunset dolphin cruises follow spinner dolphins along the channels, where pods of 50 or more are common. On dark nights around the new moon in the wetter months, plankton can light the tideline blue — ask locally which beach has had recent displays. From a local island, the 2–4 USD ferry makes visiting a neighbouring inhabited island an easy half day.

## When to go

There is no bad month, only different trips. December to April is the northeast monsoon: calm seas, visibility of 30–40 m in December through February, hammerheads schooling off Rasdhoo, tiger sharks and grey reefs on patrol. April is the warmest water and the hinge, with manta activity strengthening as visibility eases to around 18 m. May and June are the transition — mantas gathering at cleaning stations, whale sharks building in South Ari, visibility 15–25 m. July to October is the plankton season: Hanifaru at full strength, whale sharks feeding, but visibility as low as 10 m in July and September. November closes the cycle as the water clears again. Clear water and sharks, or green water and giants — pick one.

## Events and timing

Ramadan (falling February–March in 2026–27, moving about 11 days earlier each year) closes cafés and shops on Malé and the local islands in daylight hours and shrinks excursion schedules; resort islands run normally. Eid al-Fitr follows with a multi-day holiday of feasts and games on local islands, but domestic transport books out with Maldivians travelling home — avoid transferring on those dates. Independence Day, 26 July, brings parades and fireworks to Malé; resorts and transfers are unaffected.

## The practical bits

- **No-fly day.** Leave a full day between your last dive and your flight, and plan the last night near the airport if your transfer is a seaplane.
- **Transfers.** Speedboat 100–200 USD return within about 80 km of Malé; seaplane 400–820 USD return, daylight only; far atolls by domestic flight plus boat, 350–520 USD return; shared boats to Maafushi 25–30 USD one way.
- **Fridays.** The public ferry network pauses on Fridays — don't build a budget transfer around that day.
- **Alcohol.** None on local islands, full bar on resorts and liveaboards. Dress modestly away from the bikini beach on inhabited islands.
- **No car needed.** Beyond the Malé–Hulhumalé bridge, everything moves by boat or air.
