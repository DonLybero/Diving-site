# Maldives dive trip: two peak seasons, and they are opposites

The most useful fact for planning a Maldives dive trip: the country has two peak seasons, and they pull in opposite directions. December to April is the northeast monsoon — calm seas, visibility of 30–40 m, sharks stacked in the channel mouths. July to October is the southwest, plankton season — visibility can fall to 10 m, and that green water is precisely why mantas gather in the hundreds and whale sharks feed through the atolls. Both windows rate Peak in our data. Neither is better. They are different trips, so choose by what you want to see, then pick the dates — not the other way round.

## The two peaks

**Clear-water season (December–April).** The northeast monsoon flattens the sea and pushes clean ocean water through the eastern channel mouths, so you dive the east sides of the atolls. Visibility runs 30–40 m in December through February, eases to around 24 m in March and 18 m by April, the warmest water of the year. This is the channel-diving window: grey reef sharks holding station in nearly every kandu, hammerheads rising at dawn off Rasdhoo, tiger sharks patrolling Fuvahmulah in the far south, and mantas working the cleaning stations on the western sides. If your idea of the Maldives is hanging on a reef hook at a channel corner watching sharks in blue water, book here.

**Manta season (July–October).** The southwest monsoon greens the water with plankton and the whole pattern flips: the clearer diving moves to the western sides while mantas and whale sharks concentrate where the plankton collects, on the east. The peak of this is Hanifaru Bay in Baa Atoll — hundreds of mantas feeding at its August–September height. Hanifaru itself is a protected zone closed to diving, but the same animals work cleaning stations across Baa that boats dive daily, Manta Point on Lankan Reef in North Malé runs reliably from August to November, and whale sharks feed off South Ari all season. Visibility swings from 10 m in July and September to about 18–25 m in the better weeks. If a manta three metres overhead beats any wall of visibility, book here.

**The honest shoulder.** May–June and November rate Good, not Peak: transition months with visibility of 15–25 m, mantas already gathering at the cleaning stations and whale sharks building in South Ari, at lower prices with fewer boats. A fair deal — just not the headline event of either peak.

## How long — the researched answer

Trip-length advice for the Maldives converges tightly across dive-travel guides and liveaboard schedules: 7 to 10 nights land-based, and 7 nights as the standard liveaboard block (most itineraries run 8 days / 7 nights with up to about 18 dives, routed so the no-fly day is absorbed on the way back to Malé). The transfer math explains why a week is the floor: every atoll leg beyond Velana International (MLE) is a second booking, seaplanes fly only from about 06:00 to 16:00 so a late-evening arrival costs a night near the airport, and you need a full no-fly day between the last dive and the flight home.

Per style:

- **Liveaboard — the ultimate 7 nights.** The boat collects you at the airport jetty or the Hulhumalé marina on embarkation afternoon, dives three to four times a day through the middle of the week, and finishes near Malé with the no-fly period built into the last morning. Seven nights because that buys roughly six diving days and 16–18 dives after the embarkation afternoon and the no-fly close — the schedule the whole fleet is built around.
- **Seaplane resort — 9 nights.** Nine because that buys seven diving days: day one disappears into the arrival and the daylight-only seaplane, the final day is the no-fly day, and everything between is yours. Seven nights still works (five diving days); five nights shrinks to three — thin for a 400–820 USD return seaplane.
- **Local island — 7 nights, stretch to 10.** Shared speedboats run later than seaplanes, so you can often reach Maafushi or Dhigurah the same afternoon. Seven nights buys five diving days after arrival and the no-fly day, and at guesthouse prices the same budget stretches a 7-night trip to 10 with the extra days going straight into dives.

## Three ways to do it

**Resort island.** One island, one operation: transfers, meals, the house reef and alcohol all on site, from about 250 USD to several thousand per night. The catch is the transfer bill — speedboat 100–200 USD return within roughly 80 km of Malé, seaplane 400–820 USD return beyond — and the fact that you dive whatever sits within range of that one island. So pick the island for the season: near South Ari for whale sharks and Baa for mantas in the southwest season, an east-side channel atoll in the clear-water months.

**Local island.** Guesthouses on inhabited islands such as Maafushi, Dhigurah and Fulidhoo start around 50 USD a night with dive centres on the doorstep. Getting there is cheap — shared speedboats to Maafushi run 25–30 USD one way in 45 minutes, the public ferry 2–4 USD in about 90 minutes. In exchange, local law applies: no alcohol anywhere on the island, covered shoulders away from the designated bikini beach. Dhigurah sits beside the South Ari whale-shark run, which makes it the value play for the manta season.

**Liveaboard.** Nothing else covers the ground. The standard central-atolls route strings North and South Malé, Ari and Vaavu together in a week, hitting each channel when the tide is right — and because the boat moves, it follows the season: east-side channels in the northeast monsoon, west sides and manta routes north toward Baa from July to October. Expect roughly 1,500–4,000 EUR per person for the week, three to four dives a day included. You give up land entirely; surface time is the deck.

If channels and pelagics are the point, take the boat. If cost is the constraint, take the guesthouse. If half the party doesn't dive, take the resort.

## The diving

Maldivian diving is built on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through the channel mouths — kandus — and over mid-atoll pinnacles called thilas, and the standard technique is to drop at a channel corner on an incoming tide, hook in and let the blue deliver the animals. Currents run strongest around full and new moon; water holds 28–30°C all year, so a 3 mm suit covers every month.

The named sites span every level. Banana Reef in North Malé, the country's first-discovered site, works from 5 m for new divers. Maaya Thila in North Ari (6–30 m) is the classic night dive, whitetip reef sharks hunting over the pinnacle, with Fish Head nearby — a protected thila patrolled by grey reefs. Manta Point on Lankan Reef is a 10–17 m cleaning station with reliable mantas August to November. Broken Rock in South Ari is split by a coral-lined canyon swim-through at 12–30 m. For experienced divers: Fotteyo Kandu in Vaavu, a 10–40 m channel of caves and overhangs with grey reef sharks and eagle rays; Kandooma Thila, a current-swept South Malé pinnacle thick with sharks and snapper; and Kuredu Express, a fast 12–35 m Lhaviyani drift. Alimatha Jetty in Vaavu adds a night dive mobbed by dozens of nurse sharks and stingrays.

What you meet tracks the calendar above: hammerheads at dawn off Rasdhoo from December to April, mantas and whale sharks massing from May and peaking July to October, grey reef sharks in the channels every month.

## Surface days

Non-diving time happens within walking range of your island. Boats drop you on a bare white sandbank in the lagoon for a few hours of swimming and a packed lunch, about 30–70 USD per person. Malé packs the 17th-century coral-stone Friday Mosque, the fish market and the produce market into about 2 square kilometres — two to three hours between transfers covers it. Night fishing is handlines from a dhoni at sunset with the catch grilled afterwards; sunset dolphin cruises follow spinner dolphins along the channels, where pods of 50 or more are common. On dark nights around the new moon in the wetter months, plankton can light the tideline blue — ask locally which beach has had recent displays. From a local island, the 2–4 USD ferry turns a neighbouring inhabited island into an easy half day, and a spa afternoon fills the surface interval before the flight.

## Events and the practical bits

- **Ramadan** (February–March in 2026–27, moving about 11 days earlier each year) closes cafés and shops on Malé and the local islands in daylight hours and shrinks excursion schedules; resort islands and liveaboards run normally. Eid al-Fitr follows with a multi-day holiday — domestic transport books out with Maldivians travelling home, so avoid transferring on those dates.
- **Independence Day, 26 July,** brings parades and fireworks to Malé; transfers are unaffected.
- **Fridays.** The public ferry network pauses — don't build a budget transfer around that day.
- **Alcohol.** None on local islands, full bar on resorts and liveaboards; dress modestly away from the bikini beach on inhabited islands.
- **Transfers.** Speedboat 100–200 USD return near Malé; seaplane 400–820 USD return, daylight only; far atolls by domestic flight plus boat, 350–520 USD return.
- **Fuvahmulah**, the oceanic island in the far south with year-round tiger sharks, is advanced diving and a separate trip in its own right.
