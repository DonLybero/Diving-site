# Mexico dive trip: Cozumel, the cenotes and the Riviera Maya in one route

Nowhere else puts three completely different kinds of scuba within an hour of each other. Off Cozumel, the Yucatán Current sweeps you along coral walls in 30 m visibility while the boat follows. Inland, the peninsula's limestone is riddled with flooded caverns where stalactites hang over still, 25 °C fresh water. And between them, Playa del Carmen runs drift dives over reefs and hurricane-split wrecks — with bull sharks at 24 m in winter and the world's largest whale shark aggregation offshore in summer. One airport serves all of it. This is how to string the three together into a single trip on a single coast.

## The route at a glance

Land at Cancún (CUN), the region's main hub with nonstops from dozens of North American and European cities. Skip Cancún itself for now: transfer 55-90 minutes south on highway 307 to Playa del Carmen, walk to the pier, and take the 40-minute passenger ferry to Cozumel. Dive the island first, ferry back, then base in Playa del Carmen for the cenote belt with a rental car.

- **Cozumel — 4-5 nights.** Two-tank boat mornings need at least three dive days to cover the southern reefs, the wall sites and a night dive, plus a half-day for the east-coast loop or Punta Sur.
- **Playa del Carmen base for the cenotes — 4-5 nights.** Cenote days run as two guided cavern dives per day; three diving days samples the major systems, and the car earns its keep on a Chichén Itzá or Cobá day between them.
- **Optional whale-shark day — 1 day (June to mid-September).** Dawn departure from Cancún or Isla Mujeres, snorkel only, back on the 307 corridor by evening.
- **No-fly day — 1 day.** Keep 18-24 hours between your last dive and the flight home; spend it at Tulum's clifftop ruins or on a beach.

Total: about 11-12 days door to door.

**The 7-day version:** Cozumel 3 nights (two dive days), Playa del Carmen 3 nights (two cenote days), final morning at the Tulum ruins before the drive to CUN. You drop Chichén Itzá and the whale sharks, but you still get all three kinds of diving.

## Cozumel: the drift-diving island

Cozumel is drift diving at its most effortless. The current does the work; you hang in the water column while Palancar Reef's coral buttresses slide past — 5 km of reef in four named sections from 5 to 30 m, gentle enough for a first-timer. Santa Rosa Wall is the step up: a sheer drop from 10 m with swim-through tunnels and eagle rays. For advanced divers, Punta Sur's Devil's Throat is the island's signature — a tunnel entered at 24 m that exits the wall at 40 m. The C-53 Felipe Xicoténcatl, a 56 m ex-minesweeper sunk in 2000, sits at 12-24 m with penetration possible, and shallow Paradise Reef is the classic night dive for the splendid toadfish, found nowhere else. Water runs 26-29 °C year-round; a 3 mm suit covers it.

Topside, Cozumel is a small-town island with one real settlement, San Miguel. Rent a car or jeep for one day — the main reason to drive here — and loop the empty windward east coast, swimming only in the protected coves. Combine it with San Gervasio, the island's main Maya site and once a pilgrimage sanctuary of the goddess Ixchel (entry about 14-15 USD), and Punta Sur eco park at the southern tip, with its 133-step lighthouse climb and crocodile lagoon (about 20 USD, closed Sundays). For a surface day before the ferry back, boat trips to the shallow El Cielo sandbar are an easy way to off-gas.

Stay in San Miguel: walkable to the ferry pier, the malecón and most morning boat departures, with the island's best-value rooms. The ferry from Playa del Carmen costs roughly 220-290 MXN one way, departing every 30-60 minutes until late evening.

## The cenotes: cavern diving from a rental car

Under the jungle between Playa del Carmen and Tulum, rain has dissolved the limestone into hundreds of flooded sinkholes connected by cave systems. Cavern diving here — daylight always in view, guided, on fixed lines — is open to any certified diver; full cave penetration requires cave training. The water is fresh, still, 25 °C in every month, and clear enough that visibility is measured in tens of metres. Bring a 5 mm suit: repetitive dives in 25 °C water get cold.

Four dives sketch the range. Dos Ojos is the famous one — two connected "eyes" with the Barbie and Bat Cave lines at 6-10 m, decorated and beginner-friendly. Taj Mahal adds laser-sharp light shafts, fossils and limestone halls at 5-15 m. El Pit is the advanced showpiece: a 119 m sinkhole where light beams pierce a hydrogen-sulphide cloud around 30 m. Angelita is its eerier sibling, a sulphide "cloud river" at 30 m with tree branches poking through. Where you dive the halocline — the shimmering boundary where fresh water meets salt — the water blurs like oil.

This is the rental-car leg of the trip. The cenotes are scattered along 60 km of highway 307 and the inland Cobá road, gates open around 08:00-09:00 when the water is clearest and emptiest, and a car lets you chain two or three sites a day on your own clock. Base in Playa del Carmen centro — it keeps the Cozumel ferry, the dive piers and buses in every direction within one walkable grid — or in the Puerto Aventuras/Akumal corridor, which roughly halves drive times to the popular systems.

Between dive days the car opens up the peninsula. Chichén Itzá is 2.5-3 hours west (about 700 MXN foreign entry; leave by 06:30 or overnight in Valladolid, a pastel colonial town with a swimmable cenote in its center). At Cobá, the 42 m Nohoch Mul pyramid reopened to climbers in December 2025 via a new wooden staircase — again the only major pyramid in the peninsula you can ascend. Akumal's green turtles graze seagrass 40 minutes south of Playa; turtle-zone snorkeling is guided-only, booked at the beach modules.

## Cancún: the bookends

Cancún works best as the trip's first and last day rather than a base. Its inshore sites — the sculpture galleries of MUSA on Manchones reef at 8-10 m, and Manchones itself — are calm, shallow and beginner-friendly, a sensible warm-up on arrival day. In summer, the whale-shark day runs from here or Isla Mujeres: from June to mid-September hundreds of whale sharks feed offshore, peaking in July-August, snorkel only. Morning boats sell out in peak weeks, so book a day or two ahead and keep a buffer day for weather. For the no-fly day, ferry 15-20 minutes from Puerto Juárez to Isla Mujeres and loop the 8 km island by golf cart.

## When to go

**December-April is the classic window.** Dry, settled seas, 25-30 m visibility on Cozumel, and peak clarity in the cenotes — November through March brings the year's sharpest light beams through the cavern openings. It is also bull shark season off Playa del Carmen (November to March, pregnant females at 22-25 m), if you want a fourth kind of diving.

**June to mid-September is the summer draw**: the whale shark aggregation, warm 29 °C water and Cozumel's calm season. The caveat is sargassum — drifting seaweed piles onto east-facing mainland beaches roughly April to October, heaviest June-August. Two parts of this route shrug it off: Cozumel's west-facing leeward coast is largely spared by the island's geography, and the cenotes are freshwater, inland and completely unaffected. September is the one month to avoid: peak hurricane season, with possible port closures on Cozumel and the heaviest rains inland.

## Events and timing

Carnaval de Cozumel fills the week before Lent, usually mid-February — parades close the malecón and hotels fill, so book well ahead. Día de Muertos and its Yucatec Maya form, Hanal Pixán (October 31-November 2), put altars and candlelit processions in every plaza from San Miguel to Tulum — one of the best culture windows of the year, with no disruption to diving. Ironman Cozumel in late November closes the coastal road and books out the island for its weekend. In March, US spring break concentrates in the Cancún Hotel Zone; Playa del Carmen and points south feel it far less.

## The practical bits

- **Plan the no-fly day.** Leave 18-24 hours between your last dive and departure; the Tulum ruins, Isla Mujeres or El Cielo fill it well.
- **Cenote gates take pesos, in cash.** Most village gates accept no cards; carry small notes (entries roughly 150-400 MXN for swimmers, more for divers).
- **Rental-car insurance is not optional.** Mexican third-party liability insurance is mandatory and sits on top of teaser base rates — budget for it when comparing quotes.
- **Akumal's turtle zone is guided-only**, with enforced rules: licensed guide, 3 m distance, no touching, reef-safe sunscreen.
- **Rideshare apps cannot pick up inside Cancún airport**; use the first-class buses (about 270 MXN to Playa) or a pre-booked van (45-90 USD, the sensible choice with dive bags).
