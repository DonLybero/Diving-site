# Mexico dive trip: three kinds of diving, two honest windows

A Mexico dive trip on the Yucatán coast puts three completely different kinds of scuba within an hour of each other: Cozumel's drift dives along coral walls in 30 m visibility, the flooded limestone caverns of the cenote belt, and Playa del Carmen's reefs with bull sharks at 22-25 m in winter and the largest whale shark aggregation on the planet offshore in summer. Here is the finding most itineraries hide: the three never all peak in the same month. Cozumel's walls peak April-July, the cenotes peak November-March, and the Cancún-Playa corridor peaks twice — December-February for bull sharks, July-August for whale sharks. So instead of one blended route pretending everything is always on, this guide gives you two season-matched windows. Pick the one that matches your priority.

## When each is at its best

| Destination | Peak | Good | Skip |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cozumel | Apr-Jul | Jan-Mar, Aug, Nov-Dec | Sep (hurricane peak, possible port closures) |
| Cancún & Playa del Carmen | Dec-Feb (bull sharks), Jul-Aug (whale sharks) | Mar-Jun, Nov | Sep-Oct |
| Cenotes | Nov-Mar (sharpest light beams, best clarity) | Apr-May | Sep (heaviest rain, possible tannins) |

All three are at least Good only from November to May. There is no month when all three are at Peak — which is exactly why the window you choose matters.

## Window one — winter (December-February): cenotes and bull sharks at Peak, Cozumel strong

This is the all-round winner: two destinations at Peak, and Cozumel still delivering 25-30 m visibility between passing nortes. The cenotes get their crystal dry-season water and the year's sharpest light beams; pregnant bull sharks hold at Shark Point off Playa del Carmen; sailfish ball baitfish offshore.

The route: land at Cancún (CUN), transfer 55-90 minutes south to Playa del Carmen.

- **Days 1-4 — Playa del Carmen, 3 dive days.** Bull shark afternoons plus reef and wreck mornings (day counts justified below).
- **Days 5-8 — the cenote belt, 4 dive days.** Pick up the rental car; two guided cavern dives per day down highway 307 and the Cobá road.
- **Days 9-13 — Cozumel, 5 days.** Drop the car, walk to the pier, 40-minute ferry. Four two-tank boat mornings plus a surface day.
- **Day 14 — no-fly day.** Ferry back and see the Tulum ruins on the way to CUN.

Short on leave? Trim Cozumel to 3 days and the cenotes to 3 for a 10-11 day trip — the two Peak experiences stay intact.

## Window two — July: Cozumel and the whale sharks at Peak

If big animals outrank caverns, July is the one month when Cozumel's walls and the Cancún-Playa corridor peak together. Hundreds of whale sharks aggregate off Isla Mujeres, Cozumel runs calm and warm with visibility beyond 30 m, and the water hits 29 °C.

The honest caveat: the cenotes are not at their rated best in summer. The fresh water stays clear and 25 °C, but it is the rainy season — occasional stirred sediment, and none of the low-angled winter light that makes the caverns famous. Treat them as a one-day taste, not a leg of the trip. The other summer caveat is sargassum, and this route mostly shrugs it off: the drifting weed piles onto east-facing mainland beaches roughly April to October, but Cozumel's west-facing leeward coast — where all the diving is — is largely spared by the island's geography, and the cenotes are freshwater and inland, completely unaffected.

The route: Cozumel 5 days (ferry over on arrival day), then back to the mainland — 3 days based in Playa del Carmen or Cancún for the whale-shark day, a weather buffer, and a reef morning, with an optional cenote-taste day (two caverns) before the no-fly day. About 10-11 days total. The whale-shark trips are snorkel only by regulation — no tanks — with dawn departures from Cancún or Isla Mujeres and peak numbers in July-August; boats sell out in peak weeks, so book a day or two ahead.

## The ultimate 5 days in Cozumel

Why five: the island has eight-plus named must-dives, boats run as two-tank mornings, and dive-forum consensus puts the minimum for the headline sites at 3-5 days — five covers them all plus a surface day, with no repeats. Four boat mornings map cleanly onto the list. Palancar Reef fills one: 5 km of coral buttresses in four named sections from 5 to 30 m, gentle drift, the current doing the work while the boat follows. Santa Rosa Wall is the step up — a sheer drop from 10 m with swim-through tunnels and eagle rays. Punta Sur's Devil's Throat is the advanced signature, a tunnel entered at 24 m exiting the wall at 40 m. The C-53 Felipe Xicoténcatl, a 56 m ex-minesweeper sunk in 2000, sits at 12-24 m with penetration possible. Add a night dive on Paradise Reef for the splendid toadfish, found nowhere else on earth. Water runs 26-29 °C year-round; a 3 mm suit covers it.

Day five is the surface day, and the one day worth renting a car: loop the empty windward east coast (swim only in the protected coves), stop at San Gervasio — the island's main Maya site, a sanctuary of the goddess Ixchel, about 14-15 USD entry — and finish at Punta Sur eco park with its 133-step lighthouse climb and crocodile lagoon (about 20 USD, closed Sundays).

Stay in San Miguel: walkable to the ferry pier, the malecón and most morning boat departures, with the island's best-value rooms. Getting around otherwise is fixed-rate taxis. The ferry from Playa del Carmen runs 220-290 MXN one way, roughly every 30-60 minutes until late evening.

## The ultimate 4 days in the cenotes

Why four: two guided cavern dives per day is the operator standard (a third is possible but repetitive dives in 25 °C fresh water get cold even in a 5 mm suit), multi-day packages typically cover about seven cenotes in three days, and a fourth day adds the deep pair without rushing. Eight caverns in four days covers the full range. Dos Ojos is the famous one — two connected "eyes" with the Barbie and Bat Cave lines at 6-10 m, decorated and beginner-friendly. Taj Mahal adds laser-sharp light shafts and fossil-studded halls at 5-15 m. Then the deep showpieces: El Pit, a 119 m sinkhole where light beams pierce a hydrogen-sulphide cloud around 30 m, and Angelita, a sulphide "cloud river" at 30 m with tree branches poking through. Wherever fresh water meets salt, the halocline blurs the water like oil. Cavern diving — daylight in view, guided, on fixed lines — is open to any certified diver; full cave penetration requires cave training.

This is the rental-car leg, and the clearest rental-car case on the coast: the cenotes are scattered along 60 km of highway 307 and the inland Cobá road, gates open around 08:00-09:00 when the water is clearest and emptiest, and a car lets you chain sites on your own clock. Two notes before you sign: Mexican third-party liability insurance is mandatory and sits on top of teaser base rates, and most cenote gates take pesos only, in cash — carry small notes (entries roughly 150-400 MXN for swimmers, more for divers).

Between dive days the car earns its keep topside. Cobá's 42 m Nohoch Mul pyramid reopened to climbers in December 2025 via a new wooden staircase — again the only major pyramid in the peninsula you can ascend. Tulum's walled Maya port stands on 12 m cliffs above the sea (about 515 MXN combined entry; go for the 08:00 opening). Chichén Itzá is 2.5-3 hours west, about 700 MXN foreign entry — leave by 06:30 or overnight in Valladolid. Base in Playa del Carmen centro for the most connections, or in the Puerto Aventuras/Akumal corridor, which roughly halves drive times to the popular systems.

## The ultimate 3 days in Cancún & Playa del Carmen

Why three: the corridor's headline dive changes with the season, and each version needs a spare day. In winter, the bull shark dive off Playa del Carmen is a single-tank afternoon on a sandy bottom at 22-25 m — and sightings vary day to day, so plan two afternoons to be sure of your encounter, with two-tank reef and wreck mornings in between. In summer, the whale-shark day runs from Cancún or Isla Mujeres and needs a weather buffer day beside it. Either way, day three covers the inshore sites: the sculpture galleries of MUSA on Manchones reef at 8-10 m, calm and shallow — a sensible warm-up on arrival day too. General itinerary consensus gives the towns themselves only 1-2 days; the diving is why you are here.

Stay in Playa del Carmen centro (ferry, dive piers and the Quinta Avenida grid in one walkable center) or downtown Cancún near the Puerto Juárez ferry. First-class buses run CUN to Playa about every 30 minutes for roughly 270 MXN; pre-booked vans (45-90 USD) are the sensible choice with dive bags, since rideshare apps cannot pick up inside the airport.

## Events and timing

Carnaval de Cozumel fills the week before Lent, usually mid-February — parades close the malecón and hotels fill, so book well ahead if your winter window overlaps. Día de Muertos and its Yucatec Maya form, Hanal Pixán (October 31-November 2), put altars and candlelit processions in every plaza — a fine culture add-on to an early-November cenote trip. Ironman Cozumel in late November closes the island's coastal road for its weekend. In March, US spring break concentrates in the Cancún Hotel Zone; Playa del Carmen and points south feel it far less. And around March 20-21, the equinox draws huge crowds to Chichén Itzá to watch the serpent shadow descend El Castillo — plan your ruins day around it, or well away from it.

## The practical bits

- **Plan the no-fly day.** Leave 18-24 hours between your last dive and departure; the Tulum ruins or an Isla Mujeres golf-cart loop fill it well.
- **Akumal's turtle zone is guided-only**, with enforced rules: licensed guide, 3 m distance, no touching, reef-safe sunscreen. It is a snorkel session, booked at the beach modules — a surface-interval plan, not a dive.
- **September is the month to avoid everywhere**: peak hurricane season, possible port closures on Cozumel, heaviest rain inland.
