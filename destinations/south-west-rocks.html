South West Rocks

Australia · Tasman Sea / New South Wales — Fish Rock Cave swim-through with grey nurse shark aggregations

Photo: Photographer: Richard Ling &lt;wikipedia@rling.com&gt; (CC BY-SA 3.0) via Wikimedia Commons

The drawcard at this New South Wales mid-north-coast town is a single rock with a 125m cave running clean through it. Divers enter Fish Rock's deep entrance, pass wobbegongs and bull rays in the dark, and exit the shallow end into gutters patrolled by critically endangered grey nurse sharks - sometimes twenty or thirty at once. Tidal currents and surge demand some experience, and grey nurse numbers peak May to December on straightforward day-boat runs.

South West Rocks is at its best May-Dec (grey nurse aggregations); Feb-Apr warmest water, with May, Jun, Oct and Nov rating as peak season. Expect subtropical/temperate water of 19–25°C (suit: 5-7mm), visibility up to ~32m, and medium currents — EAC variable; Fish Rock has sheltered gutters. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 4 reefs, 1 cave and 1 pinnacle — ranging from The Aquarium (8-18m) for beginner divers up to Fish Rock Cave (12-24m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

This is warm-temperate diving with an edge: 19°C and a thick wetsuit in late winter, 24-25°C when the East Australian Current pushes clear blue water down in summer, and tidal current and surge that keep it an advanced destination. The centrepiece dive threads Fish Rock's 125 m tunnel between 12 and 24 m, torch-lit the whole way. Outside, The Pinnacle's sheer walls overlook sandy gutters, the fish-packed Aquarium corner sits by the shallow entrance, and Black Rock adds morays, lionfish and schooling morwong.

What you’ll encounter

Grey nurse sharks are the reason most divers come, aggregating at Fish Rock from May through December and hovering almost motionless in the gutters while you settle in behind them. Wobbegongs and bull rays hold the darker recesses of the cave, black cod lurk along the reef, and green turtles and eagle rays ride in on the warm current through summer. In winter, humpback whales pass offshore on migration.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
