Raja Ampat

Indonesia · Coral Triangle / SE Asia — Most biodiverse reefs on Earth; manta peak Dec-Mar.

Photo: Fakhrizal Setiawan (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Off West Papua's Bird's Head Peninsula, Raja Ampat's four big islands and hundreds of karst islets sit at the epicenter of global marine biodiversity; Cape Kri holds the record fish count for a single dive. Nutrient-rich currents pump through the Dampier Strait, feeding seamounts like Blue Magic where oceanic mantas circle above carpeted wobbegongs. Reef hooks earn the fishiest sites, but sheltered cleaning stations and jetty dives balance the itinerary. Dive it by liveaboard or resort, October through April.

Raja Ampat is at its best Oct-Apr (Dec-Feb peak), with Jan, Feb, Mar and Dec rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 27–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and strong currents — Currents define diving; reef hooks at Blue Magic/Sardine. Divers here work 9 recognised sites — 5 reefs, 2 pinnacles, 1 channel and 1 muck dive — ranging from Manta Sandy (10-18m) for beginner divers up to Mike's Point (5-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Current is the engine. On the Dampier Strait seamounts you set a reef hook and hang in the flow, water at 27 to 30°C swinging from a plankton-thick 10 m in the shoulder season to 40 m when it clears. Elsewhere the mood flips completely: Melissa's Garden is a still hard-coral plateau, The Passage a river-like channel of archways, and Arborek's jetty pilings crawl with macro in the shallows. The variety across one itinerary is the real signature.

What you’ll encounter

Mantas are the headline, piling into the cleaning stations at Manta Sandy and Manta Ridge through the northwest-monsoon months from December into March. Down on the reef the cast turns odd and small: wobbegongs flattened under ledges, epaulette sharks walking on their fins after dark, pygmy seahorses buried in the sea fans. When the mantas thin, macro and night dives take over.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
