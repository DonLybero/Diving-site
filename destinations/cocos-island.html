Cocos Island

Thirty-six hours of open Pacific separate Cocos Island from mainland Costa Rica, and the payoff is some of the densest scalloped hammerhead schooling on the planet. Hook in on Bajo Alcyone's seamount as the schools stack overhead, watch for whale sharks and tigers off Dirty Rock, then finish with Manuelita's frenzied night dive of hunting whitetips. Strong currents, surge, and thermoclines make this strictly advanced, liveaboard-only diving; the June-November wet season brings the biggest shark numbers.

Cocos Island is at its best Jun-Nov (hammerheads) / Dec-May (visibility), with Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep and Oct rating as peak season. Expect tropical-temperate water of 25–29°C (suit: 5-7mm + hood), visibility up to ~32m, and strong currents — Strong wet-season currents, advanced-only pelagic diving. Divers here work 8 recognised sites — 3 pinnacles, 2 seamounts, 2 reefs and 1 shark dive — ranging from Manuelita (10-25m) for intermediate divers up to Submerged Rock (12-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You back-roll into open Pacific, drop fast to the rock, and wedge a reef hook in at 25-35 m while current pulls your body flat like a flag. Thermoclines slide cold 22°C water under a 5-7mm suit and hood, and surge works the pinnacles even in the December-to-May calm, when visibility opens past 30 m. The wet season trades clarity for current — green water, rougher crossings — and the arch at Dos Amigos Grande is wide enough to swallow the whole group.

What you’ll encounter

Scalloped hammerheads are the draw, massing in schools of hundreds over the seamounts through the June-to-November wet season, when whale sharks and mantas ride the same plankton-rich currents. The drier months swap them for tiger sharks on the cleaning stations, mobula trains and silkies in the blue; whitetips pile onto Manuelita's reef after dark in any month.

What to pack here

Water sits around 27°C here, so a 5 mm wetsuit is the right call. A 7 mm is worth packing for winter or deeper, repeat dives.
