Cayman Islands

Cayman Islands (UK) · Caribbean — Crystal walls, Stingray City, and Little Cayman's Bloody Bay Wall

Photo: Scott Loarie (CC0) via Wikimedia Commons

Three islands, one theme: walls that fall away into genuinely deep blue. Little Cayman's Bloody Bay Wall starts at 6 m and plunges past 300 m, Grand Cayman stacks caverns, pinnacles and the diver-friendly Kittiwake wreck off Seven Mile Beach, and Cayman Brac holds a Russian frigate with its guns still mounted. Conditions stay clear and mostly mild, so day boats handle nearly everything, and the famous stingray sandbar makes an easy interval between wall dives.

Cayman Islands is at its best Apr-Jun & Oct-Nov, with Mar, Apr and May rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 26–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and low currents — Limited tidal range, gentle currents on walls. Divers here work 8 recognised sites — 2 wrecks, 2 pinnacles, 2 caverns, 1 reef and 1 wall — ranging from Stingray City (3-4m) for beginner divers up to Ghost Mountain (15-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You roll in over white sand at 26-30°C and the bottom simply ends: at Bloody Bay Wall the crest sits at 6 m and the face drops sheer past 300 m, leaving you suspended in 30 m visibility with blue under your fins. The rest stays easy in the mild current — swim the Kittiwake's decks from 5 m, thread Trinity Caves' coral canyons, or push through Devil's Grotto's tunnels as silversides part around your torch.

What you’ll encounter

Southern stingrays crowd the North Sound sandbar in barely 3 m of water, brushing past on every pass. Turtles work the wall tops year-round while eagle rays glide by in the blue, and around the February full moon Nassau grouper mass to spawn. In summer, silversides pack the George Town caverns with tarpon hunting through them.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
