Heron Island

Australia · Coral Sea / Southern Great Barrier Reef — Coral cay with shore diving, manta cleaning stations and turtle nesting

Photo: Bruce Deagle (CC BY 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

A true coral cay on the southern Great Barrier Reef, Heron Island puts its dive sites just minutes from the jetty, some reachable straight from shore. Heron Bommie stacks manta rays, eagle rays, and turtles on a single outcrop, and gentle drifts along the Tenements carry you past reef sharks with barely a fin kick. Come May through August for mantas, or midsummer when green and loggerhead turtles nest on the beach. Few places make beginner diving this rich.

Heron Island is at its best May-Aug (mantas), Jan-Mar (turtle nesting/hatching), with May, Jun, Jul and Aug rating as peak season. Expect tropical/subtropical water of 21–28°C (suit: 3mm summer, 5mm winter), visibility up to ~32m, and low currents — Gentle tidal drift dives, beginner-friendly. Divers here work 8 recognised sites — 4 reefs, 2 bommies and 2 drift dives — ranging from Heron Bommie (5-20m) for beginner divers up to The Tenements (8-20m), intermediate territory.

What to expect down there

Everything happens shallow here; most sites hold between 5 and 20 m on the fringing edge of a true coral cay. You circle isolated coral heads like Harry's Bommie and the Three Rocks trio, cruise the fan-coral ledges of Gorgonia Hole, and let mild tidal flow carry you along Coral Cascades toward the Tenements. Summer water sits at 26-28°C; the austral winter cools to 21-22°C but pays you back with the year's best visibility, stretching past 30 m at its clearest.

What you’ll encounter

Mantas own the cooler months, circling the cleaning stations from autumn into early spring while humpback whales pass beyond the reef. Summer belongs to turtles: greens and loggerheads nest on the cay nightly, and by late season hatchlings scramble for the water. Reef sharks patrol the drop-offs year-round, wobbegongs rest beneath Harry's Bommie, and epaulette sharks and cod fill out the cast.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
