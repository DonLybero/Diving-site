Marsa Alam

Egypt · Red Sea / Egypt — Southern Red Sea base pairing easy fringing-reef shore diving and dugong seagrass bays with world-class pelagic walls — oceanic whitetips at Elphinstone from October to December, hammerheads at Daedalus and spinner dolphin pods at Sataya.

Photo: Kora27 (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Marsa Alam is the quieter, wilder counterweight to Egypt's northern resorts: a long ribbon of fringing reef where you can shore dive straight off the sand, then boat out to serious pelagic theatre. Elphinstone's plateaus host oceanic whitetips from October to December, Abu Dabbab's seagrass shelters dugong and green turtles, and Daedalus delivers hammerheads in liveaboard range. It suits relaxed reef divers and shark-focused drift specialists alike — often on the same trip.

Marsa Alam is at its best Apr-May & Sep-Nov, with Sep, Oct and Nov rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 23–29°C (suit: 3mm full Jun-Sep), visibility up to ~30m, and moderate currents — calm inshore; strong, shifting drift on offshore pinnacles. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 4 reefs and 2 walls — ranging from Abu Dabbab (5-18m) for beginner divers up to Daedalus Reef (20-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

The fringing reef starts at the sand, so you can wade in at Marsa Shagra and drop over unhurried coral gardens on your own schedule. Offshore is a different animal: Elphinstone and Daedalus rise as sheer walls to 40 m, swept by north-south currents that can top a knot, with occasional downcurrents off the plateau edges. Water runs from a 7mm-cold 23°C in late winter to 29°C in August, and winter visibility inshore hits its 30 m annual peak.

What you’ll encounter

Oceanic whitetips patrol Elphinstone's plateaus from October into December, and Daedalus is the Red Sea's most reliable stop for schooling scalloped hammerheads. Inshore, the seagrass at Abu Dabbab shelters green turtles and, on a lucky day, a dugong, while resident spinner dolphin pods rest inside the lagoons at Samadai and Sataya. Napoleon wrasse and dense reef fish fill out the walls.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
