Palau

Palau · Micronesia / Pacific — Blue Corner drift & reef-hook diving; lunar spawning aggregations.

Photo: Ben Shoshana (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Tides drive everything in Palau. Water pours through this Micronesian archipelago's reef passes, and at Blue Corner divers hook into the reef lip while grey reef sharks parade past in the flow; German Channel trades speed for manta rays circling a cleaning station. Between drifts come the vertical shafts of Blue Holes, the air-filled chambers of Chandelier Cave and lunar spawning aggregations. Day boats and liveaboards both work, but the strong currents flatter experienced divers.

Palau is at its best Dec-Apr (Feb-Apr peak), with Feb, Mar and Apr rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 28–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~40m, and strong currents — Blue Corner up to 3 knots; reef hooks essential. Divers here work 9 recognised sites — 3 caverns, 2 drift dives, 2 channels and 2 walls — ranging from Big Drop-Off (3-30m+) for beginner divers up to Siaes Tunnel (20-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Tidal current is the engine here: you drop onto the reef lip at Blue Corner, set a reef hook and let flow of up to three knots stream you like a flag while the show comes to you. Between drifts the terrain turns hollow — four shafts drop into the huge cavern of Blue Holes, Siaes Tunnel bores through the wall at 20-40 m, and in Chandelier Cave you surface among stalactites. Water holds 28-30°C, with 30-40 m visibility in the settled early months.

What you’ll encounter

Grey reef sharks parade nonstop at the corners, and mantas circle German Channel's cleaning station in every season, feeding in formation when the plankton thickens. Palau runs on a lunar calendar: red snapper spawn on the full moon, bumphead parrotfish mass on the new moon in spring, camouflage grouper erupt in early summer — each event pulling in predators, with whale sharks cruising past in the dry season.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
