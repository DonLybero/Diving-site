Mallorca

Spain · Mediterranean / Balearic Islands (Spain) — Sunlit Mediterranean caverns, swim-throughs and air-dome caves paired with the barracuda schools and tame dusky grouper of the El Toro and Malgrats marine reserves.

Photo: Strubbl (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Mallorca is the Mediterranean's limestone playground: decades of erosion have drilled its coastline into caverns, arches and swim-throughs, most of them shallow, sunlit and forgiving. The El Toro and Malgrats marine reserves supply the fish — wall-to-wall barracuda and dusky grouper so protected they ignore you. With tide-free seas, easy day-boat logistics and 30-metre summer visibility, it suits new divers building skills and cavern fans alike; May to October is the season that matters.

Mallorca is at its best May-Oct, with Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect warm-temperate Mediterranean water of 14–26°C (suit: 3-5mm full suit Jun-Oct), visibility up to ~30m, and low currents — mostly still water; light surge at exposed islets. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 3 caves, 2 walls and 1 reef — ranging from El Toro Marine Reserve (8-40m) for beginner divers up to The Cathedral (10-22m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Eroded limestone gives Mallorca its underwater character: shallow, sunlit caverns like the Cave of Light at Portals Vells, tunnel-riddled rocks like El Formatge, and the Cathedral, a vast chamber holding a breathable air dome in its roof. The sea is essentially tide-free, so you explore at your own pace rather than ride current. Summer water hits 25-26°C with visibility around 30 m; by winter it drops toward 14°C and calls for a 7mm semi-dry or drysuit.

What you’ll encounter

The marine reserves carry the fish life: at El Toro and the Malgrats, dusky groupers grown bold under protection patrol beside walls of schooling barracuda, with eagle rays passing in summer and amberjack hunting hard by September. Octopus, moray and camouflaged scorpionfish work every rocky dive, cuttlefish move inshore to mate in spring, and nudibranchs carpet the reef in the cooler months.

What to pack here

The water here is cold enough that most divers use a drysuit. If you dive wet, a 7 mm and a hood are the warmest option.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
