Larnaca

Cyprus · Mediterranean / Cyprus — The Zenobia, a 172m roll-on/roll-off ferry that sank in 1980 and now lies on her port side from 16m to a 42m seabed, is the centrepiece and ranks among the world's great wreck dives, its cargo of articulated lorries still chained to the deck. Beyond it Larnaca Bay strings together more accessible wrecks and reefs, while day trips reach the caves, tunnels and arches of Cape Greco. Warm, clear summer water and an easy shore-and-boat mix make it a year-round Mediterranean base that peaks from June to October.

One wreck made Larnaca a pilgrimage: the Zenobia, a 172 m ferry that rolled onto her port side in 1980 and settled between 16 and 42 m, her cargo of articulated lorries still chained to the decks. Recreational divers tour the hull and twin propellers while technical teams thread the car decks, and the surrounding bay adds easier wrecks, shore entries and the sunlit caves of Cape Greco. Mild currents and warm, clear summers keep this Cyprus base busy from June through October.

Larnaca is at its best Jun-Oct, with Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect temperate/subtropical water of 16–27°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~30m, and mild currents — Sheltered Larnaca Bay is usually calm; mild drift and surge can build on exposed reefs and inside the Zenobia when wind picks up, and deeper penetration carries silt-out and depth hazards. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 3 wrecks and 3 reefs — ranging from Green Bay (3-10m) for beginner divers up to Cape Greco Caves (5-25m), intermediate territory.

What to expect down there

The Zenobia rearranges your sense of up: you descend onto a hull lying on its side at 16 m, follow railings that run vertically, and pass lorries hanging in the gloom on the way to the twin propellers near 42 m. Around the bay, HMS Cricket rests upside down with a sandy swim-through beneath the hull, and Cape Greco's tunnels and arches fill with Mediterranean light. Summer water reaches 27°C with visibility to 30 m; winter drops to 16°C but stays clear.

What you’ll encounter

Dusky grouper hold the wrecks year-round, and moray eels and octopus keep hunting the structure even through winter. Summer brings the pelagics, with barracuda and amberjack schooling over the Zenobia and the Alexandria from late spring into September. Nudibranchs carpet the reefs as spring stirs, sea turtles turn up in Green Bay's seagrass, and lionfish shelter around the Cricket.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
