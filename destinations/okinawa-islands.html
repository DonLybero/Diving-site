Okinawa Islands

Japan · East China Sea — Winter hammerhead schools at Yonaguni, summer mantas at Ishigaki

Photo: keiyac (CC BY-SA 2.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Japan's subtropical island chain strings across the East China Sea, and its diving swings from easy shore entries to serious drift. Winter sends schooling scalloped hammerheads past Yonaguni's fast currents, near the stepped stone monument that still fuels debate over its origins, while summer mantas hover over Ishigaki's cleaning stations. Around the main island, the glowing Blue Cave suits new divers and the deep USS Emmons wreck demands technical training. Day boats reach nearly all of it.

Okinawa Islands is at its best Apr-Nov mantas; Dec-Feb hammerheads, with Feb, Jul, Sep and Oct rating as peak season. Expect subtropical water of 21–29°C (suit: 5mm winter / 3mm summer), visibility up to ~32m, and strong currents — Yonaguni fast currents, drift diving. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 3 reefs, 2 caverns, 1 monument and 1 wreck — ranging from Blue Cave (Cape Maeda) (3-10m) for beginner divers up to USS Emmons (36-45m), technical-diving territory.

What to expect down there

Okinawa's diving shifts shape island by island. Off Yonaguni you drift fast current past a stepped stone monument at 25 m, then hang in open water at 21-22°C in winter, waiting for shadows to resolve into hammerheads. Around the main island the pace softens: the glowing Blue Cave, soft corals along the Sunabe Seawall, and Manza's Dream Hole, a coral chimney that swallows you from 5 m into dark tunnels. Technical divers get the USS Emmons at 36-45 m.

What you’ll encounter

The calendar splits cleanly: scalloped hammerheads school off Yonaguni through the winter months, thickest in February, while reef mantas work Ishigaki's cleaning stations from spring to late autumn, with mating trains snaking over the reefs in September. Sea turtles cruise the Kerama Islands' coral gardens year-round, joined by schooling jacks, dense reef fish and the occasional whale shark.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
