Phuket

Thailand · Andaman Sea / Thailand — Easy day-boat diving from a full-service island hub: leopard sharks and soft-coral pinnacles at Shark Point, the 85m King Cruiser wreck, manta chances at Racha Noi, and Phi Phi's limestone walls all within a day's reach.

Photo: smmohsinnaseem (CC BY-SA 3.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Phuket is Thailand's day-trip diving workhorse: big comfortable boats leave Chalong every morning for the granite boulders of the Racha Islands, the leopard-shark pinnacles of Shark Point and Anemone Reef, the King Cruiser wreck, and Phi Phi's karst walls. Warm, easy water and sheltered training bays suit new divers, while the wreck, currents and Racha Noi's manta-patrolled south tip give experienced divers real teeth. November to April delivers the calm seas and best visibility.

Phuket is at its best Nov-Apr, with Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, Nov and Dec rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 28–30°C (suit: 3mm shorty or full suit year-round), visibility up to ~25m, and moderate currents — strongest at the mid-channel pinnacles and wreck on spring tides. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 3 reefs, 2 walls, 1 wreck and 1 drift dive — ranging from Racha Yai Bay 1 (5-25m) for beginner divers up to Racha Noi South Tip (18-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Mid-channel is where Phuket earns its reputation: limestone pinnacles rising off open sand, smothered in soft coral and anemones, with tidal current that turns brisk on spring tides. The King Cruiser lies nearby at 16-31 m, its 85 m hull hazed in schooling snapper. South at the Rachas the topography shifts to clean granite boulders, while Koh Doc Mai drops a sheer fan-covered wall from a limestone islet. The water stays a reliable 28-30°C, with dry-season visibility around 20-25 m.

What you’ll encounter

Leopard sharks resting on the sand at Shark Point are the classic sighting, and through the dry season oceanic mantas patrol Racha Noi's south tip. Blacktip reef sharks and hawksbill turtles work the walls at Koh Bida Nok, barracuda swirl over the pinnacles, and bamboo sharks hide inside the King Cruiser. Koh Doc Mai's wall rewards slow hunting for tigertail seahorses.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
