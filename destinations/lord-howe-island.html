Lord Howe Island

Australia · Tasman Sea — World's southernmost coral reef blending tropical and temperate species

Photo: PotMart186 (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Six hundred kilometers off the Australian mainland, Lord Howe grows the world's southernmost coral reef, and the crossover shows underwater: tropical corals and temperate species share sites with fish found nowhere else, like the doubleheader wrasse. Sheltered lagoon holes give easy 8m dives, the volcanic Admiralty Islands stack up pinnacles, canyons and caves, and boat runs out to Ball's Pyramid - the world's tallest sea stack - bring Galapagos whalers and hunting kingfish. December through May is prime.

Lord Howe Island is at its best Dec-May, with Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr and Dec rating as peak season. Expect subtropical water of 18–25°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~32m, and medium currents — Island sites sheltered; strong drift at Ball's Pyramid. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 6 reefs and 1 pinnacle — ranging from Erscott's Hole (3-8m) for beginner divers up to Ball's Pyramid (15-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

The lagoon eases you in: sheltered coral holes like Erscott's and Comets bottom out at 8-10 m, ringed by resident fish. Boat sites raise the stakes - the volcanic Admiralty islets stack pinnacles, canyons, caves and swim-throughs in 10-30 m, and the run out to Ball's Pyramid, the world's tallest sea stack, means strong drift over reef at 15-30 m. This is subtropical diving: 25°C with 30 m visibility at summer's height, 18°C in a 5 mm suit by late winter.

What you’ll encounter

Galapagos whaler sharks own the warm months, patrolling the reef passes and sometimes buzzing divers on the descent, while kingfish blast through the bait schools. On the world's southernmost coral reef, tropical and temperate fish share the same dive, headlined by the doubleheader wrasse of the lagoon holes, with grazing turtles and Spanish dancers rounding out the cast.
