Malapascua Island

Philippines · Southeast Asia — Year-round dawn thresher sharks at Kimud Shoal (formerly Monad, now visited by tiger sharks), plus rich macro

Photo: Matt Kieffer (CC BY-SA 2.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Pre-dawn boats leave this small island off Cebu for one reason: pelagic thresher sharks rising from the deep to cleaning stations on sunken shoals, a near-daily encounter found almost nowhere else. The threshers now gather at Kimud Shoal, while Monad - the original site - draws tiger sharks. Between dawn runs there is real variety: Gato Island's swim-through tunnel, the Dona Marilyn ferry wreck and sunset mandarinfish at Lighthouse. Early starts and deeper shoal dives favor comfortable, experienced divers.

Malapascua Island is at its best Oct-May, with Mar, Apr and May rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 27–29°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and low currents — Shoal dawn dives gentle; current mainly at full moon. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 2 pinnacles, 2 wrecks, 1 cavern, 1 wall and 1 reef — ranging from Lighthouse (5-15m) for beginner divers up to Dona Marilyn Wreck (18-33m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

The alarm goes off in the dark: thresher boats leave before sunrise so you're settled on Kimud Shoal at 18-30 m as first light greys the water above. Currents on the shoal stay gentle outside the full moon, and 27-29°C water keeps a 3mm comfortable through three or four dives. The rest of the day is gentler still — whitetips and sea snakes in rocky swim-throughs, steep coral walls off Calanggaman's sandbar, soft-coral shallows at Chocolate Island.

What you’ll encounter

Pelagic thresher sharks are the constant, rising from deep water to the cleaning stations at dawn in every month of the year. March to May adds mantas and devil rays gliding over the shoal, and Monad now draws tiger sharks. Inshore it's a macro island: seahorses in the seagrass, and mandarinfish pairing off at Lighthouse as the sun drops.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
