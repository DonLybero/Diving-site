Oahu

USA · Central Pacific / Hawaii (USA) — Two intact wrecks minutes off Waikiki, summer lava-tube diving on the North Shore, green turtles at every cleaning station and pelagic sharks on the offshore banks.

Photo: nick hoke (CC BY 3.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Oahu packs real variety into one very reachable island: intact wrecks like the YO-257 and Sea Tiger minutes off Waikiki, lava-tube caverns at Sharks Cove and Makaha, and shark banks offshore where Galapagos and tiger sharks patrol. Green turtles are near-guaranteed, spinner dolphins escort west-side boats, and winter dives come with humpback song. South and west shores dive year-round; the North Shore is a summer-only prize when the surf sleeps.

Oahu is at its best May-Oct, with May, Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 24–27°C (suit: 3mm full May-Nov), visibility up to ~24m, and moderate currents — mild on reefs; current and surge on wrecks and in winter. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 3 wrecks, 2 reefs, 1 reef / cave and 1 cave / reef — ranging from Sharks Cove (5-18m) for beginner divers up to Corsair (32-35m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You pull down a mooring line off Waikiki, sometimes against noticeable current, and the YO-257 and San Pedro resolve out of the blue at 20-35 m; the Sea Tiger sits deeper, its swim-throughs opening at the edge of recreational depth. Summer flat spells unlock the North Shore's lava tubes, while skylights pierce the collapsed tunnels at Makaha Caverns on the calmer leeward side. Water runs 24-27°C - a 3 mm in summer, a 5 mm through the winter swell season.

What you’ll encounter

Green sea turtles are the sure thing, queuing at cleaning stations and holding the Waikiki wrecks alongside schooling bluestripe snapper and spotted eagle rays. Spinner dolphins buzz west-side sites like Kahe Point, whitetip reef sharks rest in the lava tubes, and Galapagos and tiger sharks patrol the offshore banks. From December to March humpback song runs under every dive, and a monk seal is the rare bonus.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
