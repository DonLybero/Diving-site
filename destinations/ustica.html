Ustica

Italy · Mediterranean / Italy — Italy's first Marine Protected Area (established 1986), where decades of protection have produced crystal water, walls draped in gorgonians and sponges, and unusually bold dusky groupers. Volcanic topography carves caves, arches and tunnels, headlined by the offshore basalt pinnacle Scoglio del Medico. Two underwater archaeological trails let divers hover over ancient anchors and amphorae in situ.

Photo: Lauraguarnieri (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

A volcanic speck in the Tyrrhenian Sea north of Sicily, Ustica became Italy's first marine protected area in 1986, and four decades of protection show underwater: crystal water, walls thick with gorgonians and sponges, and dusky groupers bold enough to follow you around. Lava rock carves the caves, arches and tunnels of the Scoglio del Medico pinnacle, while two archaeological trails let you hover over ancient anchors and amphorae. Shore and day-boat diving runs from sheltered beginner bays to current-brushed offshore seamounts.

Ustica is at its best Jun-Oct, with Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect temperate Mediterranean water of 14–26°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~40m, and mild to moderate currents — Sheltered bays and pinnacles are usually calm, but exposed offshore seamounts like Secca della Colombara and the pinnacle at Scoglio del Medico can pick up moderate current when wind and swell build. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 2 archaeologicals, 1 pinnacle/wall, 1 cave, 1 seamount/wall, 1 reef/bay and 1 cave/wall — ranging from Punta Galera (5-25m) for beginner divers up to Grotta dei Gamberi (20-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Grotta dei Gamberi is the standout - a cave near the southern tip whose walls glow orange with countless narval shrimp, its main entrance dropping toward 40 m. On the exposed north coast, Secca della Colombara flies vast gorgonian fans and Ustica's finest sponges on a seamount that catches current when swell builds. Water climbs from a cool 14°C in winter to 26°C by August, and viz on these offshore sites can open to 40 m.

What you’ll encounter

Decades of protection show in the fish: dusky groupers here are bold enough to follow you around the walls, unbothered by divers. Through the warm months barracuda school and amberjack hunt the pinnacles at their most active, while morays and octopus work the ledges year-round. In the plankton-rich spring water, nudibranchs crowd the rock in numbers before the summer pelagics take over.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
