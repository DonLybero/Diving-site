Tubbataha Reefs Natural Park

Philippines · Southeast Asia — UNESCO atoll with sharks, mantas and pristine walls, liveaboard-only short season

Photo: Ron Van Oers (CC BY-SA 3.0 igo) via Wikimedia Commons

Marooned in the middle of the Sulu Sea, Tubbataha's two atolls and Jessie Beazley Reef are reachable only by liveaboard during a short season peaking April to June. The reward is the Philippines at full strength: sheer walls draped in gorgonians, grey reef and whitetip sharks on nearly every dive, and mantas or hammerheads riding the flow at sites like Washing Machine. Strong oceanic currents make drift diving the norm, so this UNESCO park suits experienced divers.

Tubbataha Reefs Natural Park is at its best Apr-Jun, with Apr and May rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 28–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and strong currents — Persistent currents; all dives drift with live boat. Divers here work 8 recognised sites — 3 reefs, 2 walls, 2 wrecks and 1 drift dive — ranging from Shark Airport (8-25m) for intermediate divers up to Black Rock (10-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You dive Tubbataha from a live boat, drifting sheer walls that drop out of sight into the Sulu Sea, gorgonians fanning off faces like Amos Rock and current running on nearly every dive. The water is 28-30°C, and in the April-May heart of the short season visibility stretches past 30 m over the drop-offs. Sites shift character fast: a broad plateau where whitetips rest at Shark Airport, confused water at the aptly named Washing Machine, wreckage swarmed by snappers at Delsan.

What you’ll encounter

Grey reef and whitetip sharks are on nearly every dive — resting in ranks on Shark Airport's plateau, stacking in the current along the walls — while mantas glide past and jacks spin into tornadoes overhead. Washing Machine's unpredictable water can pull in hammerheads, whale sharks cruise the reef edges in the calm mid-season, and the Malayan Wreck adds nudibranchs and crustaceans for macro eyes.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • Closed means closed. Jan, Feb, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct, Nov and Dec are out of season here — weather windows or park rules, not a scheduling choice.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
