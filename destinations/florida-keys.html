Florida Keys

United States · Atlantic / Florida (USA) — America's reef tract in one island chain — the towering Spiegel Grove and Vandenberg wrecks, the Christ of the Abyss statue, spur-and-groove gardens at Molasses Reef and Looe Key, and a late-summer goliath grouper aggregation that turns the deep wrecks into cathedrals of giants.

Photo: Tane Casserley/National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (Public domain) via Wikimedia Commons

The Florida Keys string 200km of the continental US's only coral barrier reef alongside a fleet of massive purpose-sunk warships. Days here alternate easy spur-and-groove reef dives at Molasses and Looe Key with serious deep wrecks — Spiegel Grove, Duane, Vandenberg — and in August and September goliath grouper aggregate on those hulls by the dozen. Easy logistics and warm water suit new divers; the wrecks reward advanced tickets. Hurricane season demands flexible plans.

Florida Keys is at its best Apr-Sep, with May, Jun, Jul and Aug rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 23–30°C (suit: Shorty or 3mm in summer), visibility up to ~20m, and moderate currents — Deep wrecks can rip when the Gulf Stream swings in; descend on the mooring line. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 3 wrecks and 3 reefs — ranging from Christ of the Abyss (5-8m) for beginner divers up to USCGC Duane (18-37m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Days alternate between two registers. Inshore, spur-and-groove coral fingers run 2-21 m at Molasses Reef and Looe Key, with snapper and grunts packed into the grooves and water reaching 30°C by high summer. Offshore sits the deep steel: you pull down the mooring line onto superstructure from 14 m to past 40 m, and when the Gulf Stream swings across the Duane or Spiegel Grove, the current can rip. Winter cold fronts bring chop, then leave clear 20 m water between blows.

What you’ll encounter

Late summer is the marquee act, when goliath grouper of two hundred kilograms aggregate by the dozen on the Spiegel Grove and Vandenberg. The rest of the year runs on Caribbean reef sharks and great barracuda patrolling the hulls, nurse sharks and green turtles resting on the reefs, spotted eagle rays cruising the edges in the cooler months, and queen angelfish working the coral.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
