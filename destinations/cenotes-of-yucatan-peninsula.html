Cenotes of Yucatán Peninsula

Beneath the Yucatan's jungle, rain has dissolved the limestone into vast flooded cave systems, and cenotes are the sinkhole windows that let divers in. Expect gin-clear fresh water with virtually no current, cathedral light beams, stalactite-hung passages and haloclines that blur vision like oil in water. Guided cavern lines at Dos Ojos and Gran Cenote welcome any certified diver; El Pit and Angelita, with their eerie sulfide clouds at 30 m, reward deeper experience.

Cenotes of Yucatán Peninsula is at its best Nov-Mar, with Jan, Feb, Mar, Nov and Dec rating as peak season. Expect freshwater of 25°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~60m, and low currents — Calm still water, no current or waves. Divers here work 10 recognised sites — 6 cenotes and 4 caverns — ranging from Cenote Dos Ojos (6-10m) for beginner divers up to Cenote Zapote (Hells Bells) (max 30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You descend through a jungle sinkhole into water that never moves and barely changes: 25°C every month, with winter visibility reaching 60 m. Cross the halocline and the diver ahead smears into a shimmer until you sink beneath it. Each cavern has its own trick - a breathable air dome at Chac Mool, fossils in Taj Mahal's limestone halls, bell-shaped speleothems in Zapote's deep funnel, lily pads glowing over the entrance at Car Wash. A 5 mm suit earns its keep on long, slow dives.

What you’ll encounter

Life here is sparse by design; the geology is the encounter. Catfish shoal in the open pools, blind cave fish hover where daylight gives out, turtles paddle the shallows at Gran Cenote, and the odd tarpon patrols mangrove-lined Casa Cenote. The other residents are the light beams: strongest in the dry months when the water is clearest, they slant through the cavern mouths like stage lighting.
