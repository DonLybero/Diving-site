Great Barrier Reef

Australia · Coral Sea / Australia — World's largest reef; dwarf minke whales Jun-Jul; coral spawning Nov.

Photo: Ank Kumar (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Stretching more than 2,300 kilometers along Queensland's coast, the Great Barrier Reef is the largest reef system on Earth, and its best diving lives offshore. Liveaboards run the Ribbon Reefs for Cod Hole's tame potato cod and June-July dwarf minke whale encounters, then push into the Coral Sea for shark action at Osprey's North Horn. Day boats from Cairns cover easy coral gardens, while the current-swept SS Yongala wreck rewards advanced divers.

Great Barrier Reef is at its best Jun-Oct, with May, Jun, Jul, Aug, Sep and Oct rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 23–30°C (suit: 3mm summer / 5mm winter), visibility up to ~70m, and medium currents — Cod Hole gentle; strong drift only at Osprey/Coral Sea. Divers here work 9 recognised sites — 3 reefs, 3 pinnacles, 2 walls and 1 wreck — ranging from Cod Hole (10-25m) for beginner divers up to North Horn (10-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

The scale changes with every mooring. Coral pinnacles like Steve's Bommie climb from 30 m to within 5 m of the surface, Osprey Reef's walls drop into Coral Sea blue where visibility can pass 40 m, and the current-swept SS Yongala is dived only at slack tide. Inner reefs stay mild while the outer Ribbon Reef walls drift with the tides. Winter water dips to 23°C, summer reaches 30°C, and at Cod Hole giant potato cod meet you on the descent.

What you’ll encounter

June and July belong to the dwarf minke whales, which approach hovering divers on the Ribbon Reefs while humpbacks pass on their winter migration. Giant potato cod are resident at Cod Hole, green turtles graze the coral gardens, and grey reef and whitetip sharks patrol North Horn. Mantas arrive with the first plankton of the dry season, and after the November full moon the coral itself spawns.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
