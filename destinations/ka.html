Kaş

Turkey · Mediterranean / Turkey — The premier dive base on Turkey's Turquoise Coast, with 30-plus sites minutes from the harbour: dramatic walls and the Canyon, the intact sunken DC-3 Dakota, a full-size replica of the Bronze Age Uluburun wreck, amphora fields and swim-through caverns, all in famously clear warm-summer water.

Photo: HALUK COMERTEL (CC BY 3.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Set on Turkey's Lycian coast, Kaş packs 30-plus dive sites within minutes of its harbor, all in the famously clear water of the Turquoise Coast. History does the heavy lifting: an intact DC-3 Dakota rests on sand at around 25 m, a full-size Bronze Age Uluburun replica sits in a shallow bay, and ancient amphorae litter the reefs. Walls, caverns and the current-brushed Canyon add drama, with day boats serving everyone from new divers to seasoned wall hunters.

Kaş is at its best Jun-Oct, with Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect temperate/warm Mediterranean sea water of 16–28°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~35m, and mild to moderate currents — Most sheltered reefs are calm, but exposed sites like the Canyon can run a brisk southerly current across the shallow gorge before the wall. Divers here work 7 recognised sites — 2 wrecks, 1 wall / canyon, 1 reef / wall, 1 wreck / archaeological, 1 archaeological / reef and 1 cavern / wall — ranging from Uluburun Replica (Kaş Archaeopark) (8-22m) for beginner divers up to Fener (Lighthouse) Cave (5-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Clarity is the constant — often 30 m or more, so the Canyon reads like a corridor as you cross its shallow gorge, sometimes against a southerly push, and drop into the rift between anemone-cloaked walls. History fills the rest: the DC-3's intact fuselage on sand near 25 m, the Bronze Age Uluburun replica laid out with copper ingots at easy depth, amphorae scattered across the reefs. Summer water reaches 28°C; winter falls to 16°C and thick suits.

What you’ll encounter

Dusky grouper hold territory on the walls year-round, joined by morays and octopus that shelter in the crevices — and inside the ancient amphorae themselves. Summer brings the movement: greater amberjack sweep the reefs, barracuda school over the drop-offs, and loggerhead turtles cruise the shallows from early summer into September. In winter the residents tuck deep into the rock and the visitors thin out.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
