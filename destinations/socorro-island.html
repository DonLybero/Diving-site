Socorro Island

Mexico · Eastern Pacific — Liveaboard-only Revillagigedo with friendly giant oceanic mantas

Photo: Talia Cohen taliacohen (CC0) via Wikimedia Commons

Far out in the open Pacific south of Baja California, the volcanic Revillagigedo islands are reached only by liveaboard, and their giant oceanic mantas rank among the friendliest anywhere, banking within arm's reach at El Boiler's cleaning station. Roca Partida's lone spire stacks whitetips on its ledges while hammerheads, silvertips, and curious bottlenose dolphins patrol midwater. Humpback song fills winter dives. Surge and current across exposed pinnacles keep this advanced territory; the season runs November through May.

Socorro Island is at its best Nov-May, with Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, May and Dec rating as peak season. Expect subtropical water of 23–28°C (suit: 5mm), visibility up to ~32m, and strong currents — Exposed open-Pacific pinnacles, advanced. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 2 shark dives, 2 reefs, 1 manta dive and 1 pinnacle — ranging from El Boiler (San Benedicto) (5-30m) for advanced divers up to Roca O'Neal (Socorro) (15-35m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

These are open-Pacific dives on volcanic pinnacles with nothing but blue in every direction: you hold position in the surge at El Boiler while giant mantas bank overhead, or hang off Roca Partida, a lone twin-peaked spire with whitetips stacked on its ledges between 10 and 40 m. Water runs from 23°C with thermoclines in the cool months to 28°C late in the season, so the 5mm suit earns its keep, and in winter humpback song carries through entire dives.

What you’ll encounter

Giant oceanic mantas define Socorro, circling the cleaning stations and banking within arm's reach at El Boiler. Scalloped hammerheads school in the blue, silvertips, Galapagos and silky sharks cross The Canyon, and playful bottlenose dolphins seek divers out at Cabo Pearce. In the winter months humpbacks pass singing, and the first whale sharks appear when boats return in November.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • Closed means closed. Jul, Aug, Sep and Oct are out of season here — weather windows or park rules, not a scheduling choice.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
