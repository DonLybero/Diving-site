Seychelles

Seychelles · Indian Ocean — Granite-boulder reefs; whale shark season Aug-Nov; calm transition-month diving.

Photo: Tobias Alt, Tobi 87 (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Granite sets the Seychelles apart from anywhere else in the Indian Ocean: ancient boulders tumble off the beaches and continue underwater as sculpted reefs, swim-throughs and fish-wrapped pinnacles. Inner-island diving off Mahe and Praslin stays mild and beginner-friendly, with hawksbill turtles and reef sharks routine and whale sharks cruising through from August to November. The remote outer atolls, Aldabra among them, are liveaboard territory for divers chasing wilder, sharkier walls.

Seychelles is at its best Apr-May & Oct-Nov, with Apr, May, Oct and Nov rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 26–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and medium currents — Granite sites mild-moderate; current at Shark Bank/Brissare. Divers here work 8 recognised sites — 3 reefs, 2 pinnacles, 2 wrecks and 1 drift dive — ranging from Brissare Rocks (8-16m) for beginner divers up to Aldabra Atoll (10-30m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Granite gives these dives their shape: rounded boulders stack into swim-throughs and pinnacles, from Brissare Rocks at 8-16 m to the exposed drop-offs and caves of Trois Bancs at 40 m. Inner-island sites off Mahe stay mild and forgiving, with 26-30°C water and visibility that can stretch past 30 m in the calm transition months. Shark Bank's offshore plateau carries some current, the Ennerdale's tanker hull rests near 30 m, and L'Ilot comes alive after dark.

What you’ll encounter

Whale sharks are the headline, feeding on plankton blooms off the Amirantes in April and May and returning in force to northwest Mahe from August into November, when manta numbers climb too. Hawksbill turtles are dependable year-round — Ave Maria Rocks near La Digue is the reliable spot — with nesting through the northwest monsoon. Reef and nurse sharks, eagle rays and dense reef fish fill the granite between.
