Aruba

Aruba · Caribbean / Aruba — The 120m Antilla — one of the Caribbean's largest diveable wrecks — headlines a leeward coast of WWII ships, sunken airplanes and shore-accessible reefs that dive well twelve months a year outside the hurricane belt.

Photo: Serge Melki from Indianapolis, USA (CC BY 2.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Aruba sits at the Caribbean's southern edge, below the hurricane belt, where steady trade winds shape a dry, sunny island and a sheltered leeward coast that dives reliably all year. Wrecks are the story here: the vast Antilla, the torpedoed Pedernales, a coral-covered freighter and a pair of sunken airplanes, most in easy recreational depths. Warm water, short boat rides and genuine shore entries make it a natural pick for new wreck divers and mixed-ability groups.

Aruba is at its best Mar-Jun & Sep, with Mar, Apr and May rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 26–29°C (suit: 3mm shorty or full suit year-round), visibility up to ~24m, and moderate currents — mild on the leeward wrecks; can run strong at the southern reef sites. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 4 wrecks and 2 reefs — ranging from SS Antilla (3-18m) for beginner divers up to Jane Sea (15-27m), advanced territory.

Currents detailConstant northeast trade winds drive a west-flowing current along the island, so diving concentrates on the sheltered leeward coast where it is usually mild. Southern sites like the Jane Sea and Baby Beach Reef can run strong and are dived as drifts when it picks up.

Recognised dive sites (6)

Depths are typical published ranges — always confirm with your operator.

SiteTypeDepthLevelWhy it’s known
SS AntillaWreck3-18mbeginnerA 120m German freighter scuttled in 1940 and one of the Caribbean's largest wrecks, shallow enough to cover in a single dive and blanketed in tube sponges and fish.
PedernalesWreck6-10mbeginnerThe scattered midsection of an oil tanker torpedoed by a U-boat in 1942, lying in calm shallows with cabins and pipeline sections to weave through.
Jane SeaWreck15-27madvancedAn upright 52m cement freighter sunk as an artificial reef in 1988, thickly overgrown with coral and swept by the currents that bring in barracuda and rays.
Sonesta AirplanesWreck12-24mintermediateA YS-11 turboprop and a deeper DC-3 sunk off Renaissance Island, giving Aruba its signature airplane-wreck double bill.
Mangel HaltoReef5-33mbeginnerThe island's best shore dive: a mangrove-fringed lagoon spilling onto a sloping reef where turtles are near-guaranteed and seahorses hide in the grass.
Baby Beach ReefReef6-18mintermediateA hard-coral reef at the island's southeastern tip known for barracuda and turtles, with current that rewards a watchful drift.
