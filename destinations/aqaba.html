Aqaba

Jordan · Red Sea / Jordan — Jordan's 27km Red Sea window packs the Cedar Pride wreck, a scuttled C-130 Hercules, a sunken tank and an underwater military museum alongside pristine coral gardens — nearly all divable straight off the beach.

Photo: Tiia Monto (CC BY-SA 3.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Jordan's single short coastline squeezes remarkable variety into the calm, current-free northern tip of the Red Sea: a legendary freighter wreck, a scuttled cargo plane, sunken military hardware and genuinely healthy fringing reefs, almost all reached by walking in from the beach. Water stays warm and flat year-round, visibility rarely drops below 20m, and macro hunting is superb. Ideal for new divers, wreck lovers and anyone pairing dives with Petra and Wadi Rum.

Aqaba is at its best Apr-Jun & Sep-Nov, with Sep and Oct rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 21–28°C (suit: 3mm full suit May-Nov), visibility up to ~30m, and low currents — sheltered gulf, near-still water most days. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 2 wrecks, 2 artificial reefs, 1 reef and 1 wall — ranging from Cedar Pride (8-26m) for beginner divers up to Power Station (12-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Most dives start with a walk in off the beach into near-still water. Within a few fin kicks you can be circling the Cedar Pride on her port side at 8-26 m, swimming the C-130 Hercules from cockpit to tail at 12-17 m, or following the scuttled column of tanks and troop carriers down the reef slope. Visibility rarely drops below 20 m, the sheltered gulf almost never shows current, and the water runs from 21°C in winter to 28°C in late summer.

What you’ll encounter

Hawksbill turtles graze the coral gardens and spotted eagle rays glide past schooling fusiliers, but Aqaba's quiet specialty is macro: seahorses, frogfish, pipefish and nudibranchs, with octopus and cuttlefish emerging at dusk. Blue-spotted ribbontail rays rest on the sand near the wrecks, and when the early-summer plankton bloom arrives, whale sharks cruise the gulf offshore.

Currents detailThe Gulf of Aqaba is deeply sheltered, so currents are typically negligible to mild, with only a light longshore drift on some reef sites. This is one of the calmest stretches of the Red Sea and rarely dictates dive planning.
What to pack here

Water sits around 25°C here, so a 3 mm wetsuit is the right call. A 5 mm is worth packing for winter or deeper, repeat dives.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
