Sharm El Sheikh

Egypt · Red Sea / Egypt — Anthias-carpeted walls at Ras Mohammed, the summer spawning aggregations of snapper and barracuda off Shark and Yolanda reefs, drift dives across the four Tiran reefs and day trips to the SS Thistlegorm.

Photo: Diego Delso (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Sharm El Sheikh is the northern Red Sea's classic dive hub, where desert mountains drop into anthias-carpeted walls at Ras Mohammed and tidal drifts sweep the four reefs of the Tiran Strait. June to September brings the famous spawning aggregations — snapper, batfish and barracuda massing off Shark and Yolanda — while day boats reach the SS Thistlegorm. Diveable all twelve months, it pairs easy training bays with genuinely big-animal drift diving.

Sharm El Sheikh is at its best Jun-Sep, with Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 22–29°C (suit: 3mm full Jun-Oct), visibility up to ~30m, and moderate currents — strong on Tiran and Ras Mohammed corners; sheltered bays stay calm. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 3 walls, 2 drift dives and 1 wreck — ranging from Ras Um Sid (12-30m) for beginner divers up to Thomas Reef (12-35m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

At Shark and Yolanda Reef you drift twin pinnacles from 10 m to past 40 m over the scattered toilet-bowl cargo of the Yolanda, anthias streaming off every coral head. Tidal currents funnel through the Straits of Tiran, so the signature dives run as drifts, while sheltered bays and Ras Um Sid's gorgonian forest stay gentle. Summer water hits 28-29°C with 30 m visibility; winter dips to 22°C. The Thistlegorm, her holds still packed with motorbikes and trucks, is an early-start day trip away.

What you’ll encounter

Summer brings the spawning aggregations: walls of bohar snapper, circular batfish and barracuda massing off Shark and Yolanda, with scalloped hammerheads hanging in the blue off Jackson Reef. The rest of the year leans on the residents - Napoleon wrasse, grey reef sharks, eagle rays and turtles - cruising reefs permanently carpeted in orange anthias.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
