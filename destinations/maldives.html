Maldives

Maldives · Indian Ocean — Monsoon-driven channel drifts; Hanifaru manta aggregation Jul-Oct.

Photo: Tiennegolf (CC BY-SA 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Scattered across 26 atolls in the Indian Ocean, the Maldives runs on current. Monsoon-driven flow funnels through channel mouths and over isolated thilas, stacking grey reef sharks, eagle rays and schooling snapper wherever it pushes hardest. December to April brings the clearest water; May to November shifts mantas and whale sharks to the plankton-rich eastern atolls. Liveaboards cover the most ground, resorts add house-reef ease, and solid drift skills pay off everywhere.

Maldives is at its best Dec-Apr (clear water) / May-Nov (mantas & whale sharks), with Jan, Feb, Mar, Apr, Jul, Aug, Sep, Oct and Dec rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 28–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~35m, and medium currents — Tidal channel currents, manageable drift; strong at kandus. Divers here work 10 recognised sites — 3 thilas, 2 shark dives, 1 manta dive, 1 reef, 1 channel, 1 pinnacle and 1 drift dive — ranging from Banana Reef (5-30m) for beginner divers up to Fuvahmulah (Tiger Zoo) (15-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

You dive the flow, not just the reef: drop at a kandu mouth on an incoming tide, hook in at the corner, and let the blue deliver sharks and eagle rays to you. Thilas like Maaya and Kandooma rise mid-atoll into the current, flanks packed with snapper, while Fotteyo's caves and overhangs offer shelter between blasts. The water holds 28-30°C year-round; visibility swings from 30-40 m in northeast-monsoon calm to barely 10 m when southwest-monsoon plankton thickens the water.

What you’ll encounter

Grey reef sharks are the baseline, holding station in nearly every channel. In the northeast monsoon, hammerheads rise at dawn off Rasdhoo; when the southwest monsoon greens the water, mantas gather at Hanifaru Bay in the hundreds and whale sharks feed off South Ari. Tiger sharks patrol Fuvahmulah in every month, and dozens of nurse sharks mob the Alimatha night dive.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
