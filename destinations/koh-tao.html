Koh Tao

Thailand · Gulf of Thailand / Thailand — Granite pinnacles rising from the Gulf of Thailand where whale sharks cruise through walls of barracuda, ringed by calm, shallow bays that make some of the easiest learning conditions anywhere.

Photo: User: (WT-shared) Whatsinaname at wts wikivoyage (Public domain) via Wikimedia Commons

Koh Tao is where the Gulf of Thailand earns its reputation as an easy, generous place to dive: warm, calm bays over shallow reef for new divers, and offshore granite pinnacles that punch far above their weight. Chumphon Pinnacle and Sail Rock stack barracuda, trevally and — in spring and early autumn — whale sharks over anemone-covered rock. Budget-friendly and boat rides are short; skip the November-December monsoon and it delivers year-round.

Koh Tao is at its best Mar-May & Sep-Oct, with Mar, Apr, May and Sep rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 27–30°C (suit: 3mm shorty or rash guard year-round), visibility up to ~28m, and moderate currents — calm bays; moderate flow possible on the offshore pinnacles. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 3 pinnacles, 2 reefs and 1 wreck — ranging from White Rock (5-22m) for beginner divers up to Sail Rock (5-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Close to shore it's all ease: shallow boulder reefs like Twins and White Rock in near-still bays, and 27-30°C water a rash guard or 3mm handles year-round. Offshore the character changes — granite pinnacles rise from deeper water and moderate current packs the fish in tight, from Chumphon at 14-36 m to Sail Rock's vertical chimney swim-through and the 48 m navy wreck Sattakut, groupers holding her gun turrets. Spring is the calmest, clearest stretch, with visibility to 28 m.

What you’ll encounter

Whale sharks are the headline, most reliable when the spring plankton blooms pull them in to Chumphon Pinnacle, with a second run of sightings in early autumn. Around them the schooling never stops — chevron barracuda in slow wheels, giant trevally smashing into fusilier balls, longfin batfish hanging midwater — while titan triggerfish guard nests on the reefs and blue-spotted ribbontail rays settle on the sand.
