Similan Islands

Thailand · Southeast Asia — Granite boulders, mantas and whale sharks in the Andaman Sea, liveaboard season only

Photo: TANAKA Juuyoh (田中十洋) (CC BY 2.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Granite defines the Similans: huge smooth boulders tumble from white beaches to well past 40m, forming the swim-throughs and canyons of Elephant Head Rock on the exposed west, with gentle coral bommies and sand on the east. Liveaboards work the chain during the short open season, pushing north to Koh Bon's manta cleaning station and Richelieu Rock, a soft-coral pinnacle with Thailand's best whale shark odds. February to April brings the calmest Andaman seas and the biggest animals.

Similan Islands is at its best Feb-Apr, with Feb, Mar and Apr rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 28–30°C (suit: 3mm), visibility up to ~32m, and medium currents — Mostly mild-moderate; strong at Koh Tachai/Richelieu. Divers here work 10 recognised sites — 5 pinnacles, 3 reefs, 1 wall and 1 drift dive — ranging from Anita's Reef (5-25m) for beginner divers up to Deep Six (15-40m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Granite sets the terrain: smooth boulders the size of houses stack into swim-throughs and canyons that drop past 40 m at Elephant Head Rock and Deep Six, torchlit gaps opening suddenly into blue. The east side is gentler, coral bommies on white sand at Anita's Reef. Water holds 28-30°C in a 3mm, and in the February-April calm visibility runs beyond 30 m. The season is short: the park closes for the monsoon from mid-May to mid-October.

What you’ll encounter

Mantas circle the cleaning station on Koh Bon's west ridge, and Richelieu Rock gives Thailand's best whale shark odds, strongest from February to April. Leopard sharks doze on the sand throughout the open season, napoleon wrasse patrol Shark Fin Reef's long ridge, and clouds of reef fish and the odd octopus keep the granite busy between the big visitors.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • Closed means closed. Jun, Jul, Aug and Sep are out of season here — weather windows or park rules, not a scheduling choice.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
