Mauritius

Mauritius · Indian Ocean / Mauritius — A volcanic island ringed by lagoon and drop-off, pairing the cavern-lit Cathedral at Flic en Flac with fish-thick wrecks like the Stella Maru and Djabeda and shark-patrolled walls off Coin de Mire islet.

Photo: "dronepicr" (CC BY 2.0) via Wikimedia Commons

Mauritius wraps a volcanic island in barrier reef, so most diving happens minutes offshore where the lagoon wall drops into blue water. The west coast around Flic en Flac delivers the light-shafted Cathedral cavern and macro-rich ridges, while the north stacks wrecks and shark walls around Coin de Mire islet. It suits divers who want variety — caverns, scuttled ships, coral gardens — without long crossings, though January-to-March cyclones can interrupt; October to December and April to May dive best.

Mauritius is at its best Oct-Dec & Apr-May, with Apr, May, Oct, Nov and Dec rating as peak season. Expect tropical water of 23–28°C (suit: 3mm full in summer (Nov-Apr)), visibility up to ~30m, and moderate currents — gentle inside the reef, brisker on exposed northern passes. Divers here work 6 recognised sites — 2 wrecks, 2 reefs, 1 cave and 1 wall — ranging from Blue Bay Marine Park (5-18m) for beginner divers up to Rempart Serpent (20-26m), advanced territory.

What to expect down there

Most sites sit minutes off the beach, where the lagoon wall tips into blue water. The signature moment is the Cathedral at Flic en Flac: you drop to 18-28 m and swim into a boulder-roofed chamber where a single shaft of light pours through. The north stacks wrecks — the coral-dressed Stella Maru, the Djabeda upright at 28-34 m — with boats timing slack water on the exposed passes. Water swings from 28°C in summer to 23°C in winter, and Nov-Dec visibility reaches 30 m.

What you’ll encounter

Green turtles and giant morays anchor most dives, and Rempart Serpent packs an improbable density of scorpionfish, stonefish and moray eels onto one small ridge. Off Coin de Mire, clear oceanic water brings grey reef sharks, barracuda and dogtooth tuna in close, and spinner dolphins work the reefs. In the cooler months humpback whales migrate past offshore, while the wrecks hold frogfish year-round.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
