Scapa Flow

Scotland · North Atlantic / Europe — WWI German High Seas Fleet - world-class wreck diving

Photo: Bernard Picton (CC BY 4.0) via Wikimedia Commons

In 1919 the interned German High Seas Fleet was scuttled in this Orkney anchorage, leaving battleships and light cruisers scattered across the seabed, still bristling with guns. The dreadnoughts lie deep, 38 to 45 meters, and reward advanced and technical divers; cruisers like SMS Karlsruhe and the light-filled Burra Sound blockships keep recreational depths honest. Cold, dark, and utterly compelling, it is dived by hardboat from Stromness, May to September.

Scapa Flow is at its best May-Sep, with Jun, Jul and Aug rating as peak season. Expect saltwater of 7–14°C (suit: Drysuit required), visibility up to ~12m, and medium currents — Sheltered Flow centre; blockship channels slack-only. Divers here work 10 recognised sites — 10 wrecks — ranging from F2 & YC21 Barge (10-18m) for beginner divers up to SMS Markgraf (24-45m), technical-diving territory.

What to expect down there

Everything is done under a drysuit and torchlight. Descend onto a battleship's upturned hull - Kronprinz Wilhelm's 12-inch guns still under a century of growth - and plumose anemones carpet every rail and capstan in feeding white. Water holds 7 to 14°C, viz a modest 8 to 15 m, clearest in the cold months. Seals grow bold on the shallower wrecks in summer, buzzing divers mid-dive. The scale of the steel looming out of the dark is the whole point.

What you’ll encounter

The wildlife here is the wrecks themselves, colonised into artificial reef. Plumose anemones smother the steel in white and orange, thickest through the summer feeding months, softening every gun and railing. Seals are the charismatic draw, growing bold around the shallower hulls from spring onward and often escorting divers across a dive. Inside the hulls, fish life is sparse but atmospheric.

The honest picture

What can change on the day?

  • Seasons drift. Marine-life timing shifts year to year with plankton blooms and lunar cycles — a peak landing a couple of weeks early or late is normal, not bad luck.
  • Temperatures are ranges. Water figures here are typical monthly ranges (±1°C); an upwelling or a heatwave can step outside them.
  • Visibility is seasonal, not daily. Wind, swell, rain and plankton move it day to day — read our metres as the month's typical form, not a promise for your dive.
  • Currents can run above the rating. Conditions on the day decide — brief with your operator before the first dive, and sit one out if it's beyond your training.
  • A score compares, it doesn't guarantee. We rank months from dive-operator and liveaboard calendars so you can weigh destinations like for like — always confirm current conditions with a local dive centre.
